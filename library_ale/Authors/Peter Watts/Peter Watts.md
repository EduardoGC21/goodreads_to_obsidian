## Biography
Peter Watts (b. 1958) is a Canadian science fiction author and former marine biologist known for combining hard scientific rigor with bleak, philosophically challenging narratives. Trained as a scientist, he earned degrees in biology and worked extensively on marine mammals, experiences that deeply inform his fiction’s focus on cognition, ecology, and the limits of human understanding. His work is often characterized by detailed extrapolation from real neuroscience, evolutionary biology, and astrophysics, and by an unflinching willingness to depict humanity as just one more contingent outcome of natural processes rather than a privileged species.

Watts gained wide recognition with novels such as "Blindsight" and its companion "Echopraxia," which use first-contact and transhuman scenarios to interrogate consciousness, free will, and the nature of intelligence. These books, along with his shorter fiction, have earned him critical acclaim, a devoted readership, and a reputation as one of the foremost voices in hard science fiction. His career has also been marked by outspoken commentary on technology, surveillance, and social structures, reinforcing the sense that his fiction is part of a larger engagement with contemporary scientific and ethical debates.

Throughout his life Watts has navigated personal and professional challenges, including legal and health difficulties, which he has addressed candidly in essays and blog posts. These experiences, together with his scientific background and Canadian upbringing, contribute to the recurring themes of marginalization, resilience, and systemic critique in his work. His influence is evident in the way later authors and readers discuss consciousness, alienness, and posthuman futures, and he is frequently cited as a key figure in 21st-century hard SF.
