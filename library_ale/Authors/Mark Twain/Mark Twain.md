## Biography
Mark Twain (Samuel Langhorne Clemens, 1835–1910) was an American writer, humorist, and lecturer whose sharp wit and vivid storytelling made him one of the most influential figures in U.S. and world literature. Born in Florida, Missouri, and raised in the river town of Hannibal, he worked as a printer’s apprentice, typesetter, and steamboat pilot on the Mississippi River, experiences that deeply shaped his imagination and provided the settings and characters for many of his later works.

Twain achieved fame with works such as *The Adventures of Tom Sawyer* and *Adventures of Huckleberry Finn*, which combined colloquial speech, satire, and moral complexity to explore themes like childhood, freedom, race, and hypocrisy in American society. He traveled extensively across the United States and abroad, turning his observations into popular travel narratives and lectures. Despite financial troubles and personal tragedies, including the deaths of several of his children, Twain remained a sharp social critic and observer of human nature, whose blend of humor and seriousness has left a lasting mark on literature and public discourse.

In his later years, Twain became increasingly outspoken on issues such as imperialism, organized religion, and social injustice, often adopting a darker, more pessimistic tone but retaining his characteristic irony. His friendships with other prominent figures of his time and his status as a public intellectual cemented his reputation as both a national icon and a global literary figure. His life and work continue to be studied for their artistic innovation and their probing, often uncomfortable questions about American identity and moral responsibility.
