## Biography
Andy Weir (1972– ) is an American novelist best known for blending hard science with gripping, character-driven stories. A lifelong science enthusiast and former software engineer, he drew on his interests in physics, orbital mechanics, and engineering to craft technically grounded yet accessible fiction. His breakout novel "The Martian," first self-published online, showcased his meticulous research and problem-solving approach, following an astronaut stranded on Mars and turning realistic survival challenges into a global phenomenon.

Before becoming a full-time writer, Weir worked in the tech industry, which shaped his pragmatic, detail-oriented style and his comfort with scientific complexity. He cultivated a strong relationship with an online readership, iterating his work based on feedback and releasing chapters serially, a process that helped refine his storytelling and eventually led to major publishing and film deals. Weir’s success opened doors for more rigorously scientific, optimistic science fiction in the mainstream and cemented his reputation as a leading contemporary voice in the genre.

Weir is often noted for his focus on collaborative problem solving, the competence of scientists and engineers, and the human capacity to adapt and improvise under pressure. His work has resonated with both general readers and technical audiences, and he continues to write novels that sit at the intersection of scientific plausibility, humor, and human resilience.
