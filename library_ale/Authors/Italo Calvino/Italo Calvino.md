## Biography
Italo Calvino (1923–1985) was an Italian novelist, short story writer, and essayist whose work bridged neorealism, fantasy, and experimental literature. Born in Santiago de Las Vegas, near Havana, Cuba, to Italian parents and raised in Sanremo, Italy, he grew up in a family of scientists and freethinkers, an environment that sharpened both his skepticism and his curiosity. During World War II he joined the Italian Resistance against Fascism, an experience that informed his early neorealist writings and his enduring concern with ethics, responsibility, and the ambiguities of history.

After the war Calvino became associated with the Einaudi publishing house and the Italian literary left, gradually moving from realistic narratives toward allegory, fable, and highly structured experiments with form. Works like "Il visconte dimezzato" and "Il cavaliere inesistente" ("El caballero inexistente") use chivalric and fantastic settings to explore identity, fragmentation, and the tension between ideals and reality. Later books such as "Le città invisibili" and "Se una notte d'inverno un viaggiatore" established him as a central figure of postwar European literature, admired for his clarity of style, playfulness, and philosophical depth.

Calvino lived and worked between Italy and France, forming close friendships with other writers and intellectuals while contributing criticism, essays, and literary theory. In his final years he prepared the lectures later published as "Six Memos for the Next Millennium," articulating his values of lightness, exactitude, and multiplicity in literature. He died in Siena, Italy, in 1985, leaving a body of work that continues to influence novelists, theorists, and readers worldwide through its blend of imagination, formal rigor, and moral inquiry.
