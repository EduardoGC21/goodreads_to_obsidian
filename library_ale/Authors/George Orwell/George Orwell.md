## Biography
George Orwell (1903–1950), born Eric Arthur Blair, was a British novelist, essayist, journalist, and critic whose work profoundly shaped twentieth‑century political and cultural thought. Raised in a lower‑middle‑class family connected to the British colonial service, he spent part of his childhood in England and later served as an imperial policeman in Burma, an experience that fostered his deep opposition to imperialism and authoritarian power. His early years were marked by a sense of social injustice and class division, themes that would recur throughout his writing.

Orwell’s life was defined by his commitment to political honesty and moral clarity. He lived among the poor in Paris and London, fought on the Republican side in the Spanish Civil War, and emerged as a fierce critic of totalitarian regimes of both the right and the left. His major works, including "Animal Farm" and "1984", use allegory and dystopian fiction to expose the dangers of propaganda, ideological fanaticism, and the erosion of individual freedom; his critical essays, such as those collected in volumes like "All Art Is Propaganda", helped establish him as one of the twentieth century’s most influential essayists. Despite chronic ill health and financial hardship, Orwell remained dedicated to plain, truthful prose and a humane, skeptical socialism, leaving a legacy that continues to inform debates about power, language, and conscience.

Orwell’s personal relationships, including his marriages to Eileen O’Shaughnessy and later Sonia Brownell, as well as friendships with fellow writers and intellectuals in Britain, placed him at the center of the literary and political networks of his time, yet he often chose a life of relative seclusion to write and reflect. He spent his final years struggling with tuberculosis while working intensively on "1984", which he completed shortly before his death in 1950. His life and work are widely regarded as a testament to the responsibility of the writer to confront lies, expose injustice, and defend the dignity of ordinary people.
