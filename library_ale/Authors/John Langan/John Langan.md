## Biography
John Langan (born 1969) is an American writer best known for his intricate and emotionally resonant contributions to contemporary horror and weird fiction. Raised and educated in New York State, he earned advanced degrees from the State University of New York at New Paltz and the CUNY Graduate Center, training both as a literary scholar and as a practicing writer. Over the years he has balanced his work as a novelist and short story writer with an academic career, teaching creative writing and gothic literature at SUNY New Paltz and other institutions, and mentoring a generation of students in the craft and criticism of horror.

Langan first gained wider recognition through his short fiction and collections, which showcased his blend of literary technique, folklore, and supernatural terror. His novel “The Fisherman,” a grief‑haunted tale set in upstate New York, became a landmark in modern horror, winning the Bram Stoker Award and solidifying his reputation for ambitious, character‑driven narratives. He has also written essays on horror and co‑edited the anthology “Creatures: Thirty Years of Monsters” with fellow writer Paul Tremblay, reflecting his active engagement with the broader horror community.

Beyond his own books, Langan has helped shape the field institutionally as one of the founders and a board member of the Shirley Jackson Awards, which honor outstanding achievements in psychological suspense, horror, and dark fantasy. He lives in New York’s Hudson Valley with his family and numerous animals, continuing to write fiction that intertwines intimate human dramas with cosmic and folkloric dread.
