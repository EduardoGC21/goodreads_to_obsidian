## Biography
Steven Best (born December 1955) is an American philosopher, writer, and activist whose work spans critical theory, animal rights, environmental ethics, and global justice. Educated in philosophy at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, the University of Chicago, and the University of Texas at Austin, he went on to join the faculty of the University of Texas at El Paso, where he became Associate Professor of Humanities and Philosophy. Early in his career he gained recognition for influential collaborations with Douglas Kellner on postmodern theory and cultural studies, helping to shape debates over postmodernism and critical social theory.

From the 1990s onward, Best became a prominent voice in animal rights and what he calls “total liberation,” arguing that struggles for human, animal, and earth emancipation are deeply interconnected. He co-founded the Institute for Critical Animal Studies and helped establish the North American Animal Liberation Press Office, positioning himself at the intersection of scholarship and radical activism. Through books such as the co-edited volume “The Global Industrial Complex: Systems of Domination” and numerous public lectures, he has analyzed how capitalism, state power, and industrial systems generate interlocking forms of oppression and ecological crisis.

Best is also known for his uncompromising critique of reformist approaches and his willingness to defend militant direct action in certain contexts, a stance that has made him both influential and controversial, including being barred from entering the United Kingdom in 2005 for his outspoken support of radical animal advocacy. Over several decades he has published more than a dozen books and hundreds of articles, worked with diverse movements from environmentalists to Black liberation and Indigenous activists, and sought to make philosophy an engaged, public practice aimed at transformative social change.
