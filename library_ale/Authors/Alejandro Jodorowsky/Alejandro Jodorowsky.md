## Biography
Alejandro Jodorowsky (born 1929 in Tocopilla, Chile) is a Chilean‑born French artist whose life has spanned theatre, film, comics, literature, and esoteric practice. The son of Ukrainian Jewish immigrants who ran a shop in northern Chile, he grew up feeling both culturally displaced and artistically driven, beginning to write poetry and experiment with puppetry and mime as a teenager. After briefly attending the University of Chile, he left formal studies to pursue performance, and in 1953 moved to Paris, where he studied mime with Marcel Marceau and immersed himself in the European avant‑garde.

In the 1960s Jodorowsky co‑founded the Panic Movement with Fernando Arrabal and Roland Topor, staging transgressive, ritual‑like theatrical events that fused shock, satire, and mysticism. He later divided his time between Mexico and Europe, directing cult films such as “El Topo” and “The Holy Mountain,” whose violent surrealism and spiritual symbolism made him a central figure in midnight‑movie and countercultural circles. Parallel to his filmmaking, he became a prolific writer of comics and graphic novels; his collaboration with the artist Moebius on “The Incal” linked his visionary, metaphysical storytelling with a broad international readership.

Throughout his life Jodorowsky has cultivated a public persona as a spiritual experimenter and healer, developing practices he calls psychomagic and psychogenealogy, while also becoming known for his intense engagement with the tarot. He has had several long‑term relationships and five children, some of whom have acted in his films and continued the family’s artistic lineage. In later decades he settled in Paris with his wife and collaborator Pascale Montandon, with whom he creates joint artworks, and he remains active into old age as an irrepressible, sometimes controversial figure whose work seeks personal and collective transformation through art.
