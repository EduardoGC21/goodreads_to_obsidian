## Biography
Laila Lalami (born 1968) is a Moroccan-born American writer, essayist, and academic whose work explores migration, identity, colonial legacies, and the experience of living between cultures. Raised in Rabat, Morocco, she was educated both in her home country and abroad, eventually moving to the United States, where she completed graduate studies in linguistics and began writing fiction and nonfiction in English. Her own trajectory as an immigrant and bilingual writer deeply informs her work, which often centers on characters navigating borders—geographical, cultural, and social.

Lalami first gained wide recognition with fiction that examined the lives of Moroccans and the Moroccan diaspora, and she later became particularly acclaimed for historical and contemporary novels that reimagine familiar narratives from marginalized perspectives. Her books have been finalists or longlisted for major literary awards, and she has received prestigious fellowships and prizes recognizing both her literary achievement and her contributions to public discourse. Alongside her books, she has written essays and criticism for prominent outlets, frequently addressing questions of citizenship, race, and belonging.

In addition to her writing career, Lalami has taught creative writing at American universities, mentoring newer generations of writers. She is widely regarded as a significant voice in contemporary literature, especially in conversations about postcolonial history and the complexity of immigrant identities. While many readers know her through her best-known novels and essays, she has also contributed shorter fiction and criticism, and her influence extends across international literary and academic circles.
