## Biography
Abraham Verghese (b. 1955) is an Ethiopian‑born, Indian‑origin physician and writer whose work has powerfully shaped contemporary thinking about the human side of medicine. Born in Addis Ababa to Malayali Christian parents recruited from Kerala, India, to teach in Ethiopia, he began medical school there before political upheaval forced his family to relocate. After a period working as a hospital orderly in the United States, he completed his medical education at Madras Medical College in India and then returned to the U.S. for residency and fellowship training.

Verghese’s early career unfolded in Johnson City, Tennessee, and later Boston, where he confronted the emerging HIV/AIDS epidemic. The emotional and moral complexity of caring for patients in this context led him to writing as a parallel vocation, eventually earning an MFA from the Iowa Writers’ Workshop and producing acclaimed nonfiction such as "My Own Country" and "The Tennis Partner," and later the bestselling novels "Cutting for Stone" and "The Covenant of Water." Throughout, he has argued for the centrality of attentive bedside practice and the physical exam, emphasizing empathy, narrative, and the lived experience of illness.

A long‑time academic physician, Verghese became founding director of the Center for Medical Humanities and Ethics in San Antonio and later joined Stanford University School of Medicine, where he serves as a senior professor and vice chair. His contributions have been recognized with major honors, including the National Humanities Medal, reflecting his influence at the intersection of literature and medicine, his advocacy for patient‑centered care, and his mentoring of both clinicians and writers.
