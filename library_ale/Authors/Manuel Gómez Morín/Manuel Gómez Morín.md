## Biography
Manuel Gómez Morín (1897–1972) was a Mexican lawyer, economist, and politician whose work helped shape the institutional foundations of modern Mexico. Born in the mining town of Batopilas, Chihuahua, and raised by his widowed mother, he moved through several cities before settling in Mexico City, where he studied law during the upheavals of the Mexican Revolution. At the National University he became part of the influential “Generation of 1915” and the circle later known as the Seven Sages of Mexico, combining legal scholarship with an early commitment to university autonomy and public service.

As a young technocrat, Gómez Morín played a central role in designing Mexico’s monetary and banking framework. He was a key architect and board member of the newly created Bank of Mexico, promoted early agricultural credit legislation, and helped draft important financial and constitutional laws that regulated credit, insurance, and aspects of property and state intervention. In parallel, he maintained a distinguished academic career, teaching constitutional and political law and later serving as rector of the National Autonomous University of Mexico, where he promoted an austere but democratic vision of university government.

Disillusioned with the direction of postrevolutionary one‑party rule, Gómez Morín turned to opposition politics. In 1939 he co‑founded the National Action Party (PAN) and became its first president, giving the Mexican opposition a coherent legalistic and ethical framework rather than a purely caudillo‑based challenge. Throughout his life he combined legal practice, investment, teaching, and party leadership, influencing generations of jurists and politicians. His legacy endures in Mexico’s central banking system, its legal thought, and the long‑term presence of PAN as a major force in Mexican political life.
