## Biography
Sabahattin Ali (1907–1948) was a Turkish writer, poet, and journalist whose work became a powerful voice for social realism in early 20th‑century Turkey. Born in Gümülcine (then part of the Ottoman Empire, now Komotini in Greece), he trained as a teacher and worked in various Anatolian towns, experiences that deeply informed his depictions of rural hardship, class inequality, and the tensions of a rapidly modernizing society. His fiction and essays often centered on the struggles of ordinary people, combining psychological depth with sharp social critique.

Ali was also an engaged intellectual and political critic, which repeatedly brought him into conflict with the authorities. He was imprisoned more than once for his writings and outspoken views, and lived under constant surveillance and pressure. His celebrated novel “Madonna in a Fur Coat” (“Madonna con abrigo de piel”) later became one of the most widely read works in modern Turkish literature, admired for its exploration of love, alienation, and individuality against the backdrop of interwar Europe. In 1948, while attempting to leave Turkey under fraught political circumstances, he was killed near the Bulgarian border under mysterious conditions, widely believed to have involved state agents, turning him into a symbol of artistic and intellectual repression.

During his short life, Sabahattin Ali maintained close ties with other progressive writers, teachers, and journalists, contributing to literary journals and fostering a culture of critical debate. His blend of lyrical prose, empathy for the marginalized, and moral seriousness left a lasting mark on Turkish letters and continues to influence readers and writers well beyond his era.
