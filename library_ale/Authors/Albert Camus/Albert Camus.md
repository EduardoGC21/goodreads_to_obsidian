## Biography
Albert Camus (1913–1960) was a French-Algerian writer, philosopher, and journalist whose work explored the themes of absurdity, rebellion, and moral responsibility in a seemingly indifferent universe. Born in Mondovi, in French Algeria, to a poor pied-noir family, he lost his father in World War I and was raised by his mother in modest circumstances. A gifted student, he studied philosophy in Algiers but struggled with recurring tuberculosis, an illness that shaped both his life and his reflections on human vulnerability and contingency.

Camus gained international recognition with novels such as "The Stranger," whose detached protagonist Meursault exemplifies his idea of the absurd confrontation between human longing for meaning and the world's silence. Alongside his fiction, he wrote influential philosophical essays, including "The Myth of Sisyphus" and "The Rebel," in which he argued for a form of measured revolt grounded in human dignity rather than ideological fanaticism. During World War II he was active in the French Resistance, editing the underground newspaper "Combat," and his postwar stance made him an important moral voice in debates over justice, violence, and political extremism.

Awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1957, Camus was celebrated for illuminating “the problems of the human conscience” in the modern age. His relationships and intellectual conflicts—most notably with contemporaries like Jean-Paul Sartre—highlighted his refusal to sacrifice individual ethics to abstract political causes. Camus died prematurely in a car accident in 1960, leaving an enduring legacy as a novelist and moral thinker whose work continues to shape discussions of freedom, responsibility, and the search for meaning.
