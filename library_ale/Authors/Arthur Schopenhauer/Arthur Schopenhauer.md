## Biography
Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860) was a German philosopher best known for his monumental work "The World as Will and Representation," in which he argued that the fundamental reality underlying all phenomena is a blind, striving will. Born in Danzig (then a Free City with strong German cultural ties) to a wealthy merchant family, he was educated in Hamburg, Göttingen, and Berlin, where he studied under major thinkers of his time but soon developed a fiercely independent and often combative philosophical stance, especially in opposition to Hegel. His pessimistic view of existence—emphasizing suffering, frustration, and the illusory nature of happiness—set him apart from more optimistic strains of Enlightenment and post-Kantian philosophy.

Schopenhauer never married and was known for a solitary, often irascible personality, though he maintained a few close friendships and had complicated relationships with his parents, particularly his mother, the writer Johanna Schopenhauer. For much of his life he lived in relative obscurity in Frankfurt, frustrated by the lack of recognition from academic philosophy. Only later in the 19th century did his writings gain wide attention, influencing figures such as Nietzsche, Wagner, Freud, and Thomas Mann. His integration of Western philosophy with ideas drawn from Indian traditions, along with his reflections on art, compassion, and asceticism as responses to the will, made him a pivotal figure in the history of modern European thought.
