## Biography
M. E. O’Brien is a contemporary writer and theorist whose work explores the intersections of gender freedom, communist politics, and the transformation of family and care under and beyond capitalism. Trained as a scholar of LGBTQ social movements, she completed a PhD at New York University with research on how capitalism has shaped queer and trans organizing in New York City, and she previously coordinated the New York City Trans Oral History Project while also engaging in HIV and AIDS activism and services. Her writing has helped articulate a vision of “family abolition,” arguing for the reorganization of care and kinship beyond the constraints of traditional family structures and the wage relation.

O’Brien is the author of "Family Abolition: Capitalism and the Communizing of Care" and co-author, with Eman Abdelhadi, of the speculative oral history "Everything for Everyone: An Oral History of the New York Commune, 2052–2072," which imagines revolutionary upheaval and collective life in a post-capitalist New York. She is a member of the editorial collective of Pinko, a magazine of gay communism, and co-edits Parapraxis, devoted to psychoanalytic theory and politics. Across her scholarship, fiction, and political work, O’Brien has become a significant voice in contemporary Marxist, queer, and trans debates about how care, desire, and social reproduction might be reorganized in a liberated future.

Her influence extends internationally through translations of her work into multiple languages and through collaborations with activists, writers, and communities engaged in anticapitalist, abolitionist, and queer liberation struggles. As a public intellectual and practicing psychotherapist, she bridges theory and practice, bringing insights from radical politics, psychoanalysis, and trans life experiences into dialogue with movements seeking to abolish oppressive institutions and build new forms of collective care.
