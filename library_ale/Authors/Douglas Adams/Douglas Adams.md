## Biography
Douglas Adams (1952–2001) was an English author and humorist best known for blending science fiction with absurdist, philosophical comedy. Born in Cambridge and raised in Essex, he studied English at St John’s College, Cambridge, where he became involved in the Footlights theatrical club. After university he struggled to establish himself as a writer, doing a variety of odd jobs while placing sketches on British radio and television, including work on "Monty Python’s Flying Circus."

Adams achieved international fame with "The Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy," which began as a BBC radio series before becoming a bestselling novel and subsequent series of books. His work is celebrated for its playful language, satirical treatment of bureaucracy and technology, and its exploration of existential questions through comedy. Beyond Hitchhiker’s, he wrote novels such as the "Dirk Gently" series, contributed to television and computer game projects, and became an early advocate for environmental conservation and technology, particularly personal computing and digital innovation.

Adams’s personal life included close friendships within the British comedy and science fiction communities, and he often collaborated with other writers and performers. He lived for periods in both the United Kingdom and the United States, married Jane Belson in 1991, and they had one daughter. Adams died suddenly of a heart attack in 2001 while in California, leaving a lasting legacy as one of the most distinctive voices in late 20th-century British popular culture.
