## Biography
Noboru Takeuchi (born 1959) is a Colombian-born Mexican physicist, professor, and science communicator whose work has been central to the development of nanoscience and nanotechnology in Latin America. After early schooling in Bogotá, he studied physics at the National University of Colombia, graduating with honors, and went on to earn a PhD in physics from Iowa State University followed by postdoctoral research at the International School for Advanced Studies in Trieste, Italy. Since the mid‑1990s he has been a leading researcher at the Center for Nanosciences and Nanotechnology of the National Autonomous University of Mexico (UNAM), where he has investigated the structural, electronic, and magnetic properties of solids, liquids, amorphous materials, and nanostructures, with particular contributions to the physics of metal and semiconductor surfaces and to the design of new materials for catalysis, optoelectronics, and spintronics.

Beyond his scientific research, Takeuchi has become widely known for his commitment to public outreach and education in nanoscience. He has written technical and popular books, including "Nanociencia y nanotecnología: La construcción de un mundo mejor átomo por átomo," and has coordinated extensive projects to communicate topics such as nanotechnology, clean energy, and climate change to children, young people, and the general public. A distinctive feature of his work is the promotion of science in Indigenous languages of Mexico and other Latin American countries: under his leadership, books and materials on nanotechnology and related sciences have been translated into numerous languages such as Nahuatl, Maya, Zapotec, Mixtec, and Quechua, helping to build bridges between cutting‑edge research and communities that have historically had limited access to scientific education.

Takeuchi is also part of a family deeply connected to science and education. He is the son of Yu Takeuchi, a Japanese-born mathematician and physicist who became a key figure in the popularization of mathematics in Colombia, and this legacy is reflected in Noboru Takeuchi’s own dedication to both high-level research and the democratization of scientific knowledge. Over the course of his career he has received multiple distinctions from Mexican and Latin American institutions for both his scientific contributions and his pioneering work in science communication, reinforcing his standing as a major figure in contemporary nanoscience and in the cultural integration of science and Indigenous knowledge in the region.
