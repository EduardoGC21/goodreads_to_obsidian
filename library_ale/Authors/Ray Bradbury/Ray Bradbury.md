## Biography
Ray Bradbury (1920–2012) was an American writer best known for blending poetic language, speculative imagination, and sharp social observation in his fiction. Raised primarily in Waukegan, Illinois, he developed an intense love for libraries, films, and pulp magazines that shaped both his style and his lifelong advocacy for reading. Largely self‑educated, he spent countless hours in public libraries, later crediting them as his true university and the source of much of his creative power.

Bradbury emerged as a major voice in mid‑20th‑century American literature through works that used fantasy and science‑fiction settings to explore censorship, technological alienation, colonization, and the fragility of human empathy. Collections like “The Illustrated Man” and “The Martian Chronicles” and the novel “Fahrenheit 451” brought him international recognition and helped move speculative fiction into the literary mainstream. Beyond his books, he worked in radio, film, and television, collaborated with directors such as John Huston, and remained an energetic public speaker and essayist, championing imagination, individual freedom, and the cultural importance of libraries until his death in Los Angeles in 2012.
