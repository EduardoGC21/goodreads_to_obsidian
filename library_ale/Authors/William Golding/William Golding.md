## Biography
William Golding (1911–1993) was an English novelist, playwright, and schoolteacher whose work probed the darker aspects of human nature. Born in Cornwall to a schoolmaster father and a suffragist mother, he was educated at Oxford, initially studying science before switching to English literature. Before the Second World War he worked as a teacher, an experience that deeply informed his later depictions of children and group dynamics.

During World War II, Golding served in the Royal Navy, taking part in actions including the D-Day landings. The brutality and moral chaos he witnessed at sea and in combat profoundly shaped his worldview and his literary themes. After the war he returned to teaching while writing fiction; his breakthrough novel, "Lord of the Flies" (1954), used the story of stranded schoolboys to explore innate violence, the fragility of civilization, and the ease with which order collapses. The book initially had modest sales but went on to become one of the most studied and influential novels of the twentieth century.

Golding continued to write novels, essays, and plays that examined myth, history, and moral choice, often focusing on flawed protagonists placed in extreme situations. His work earned him major recognition, including the Booker Prize and the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1983. Knighted in 1988, he spent his later years in Cornwall, maintaining a relatively private life with his wife, Ann Brookfield, and their children, while his reputation grew as a central figure in postwar British literature.
