## Biography
Noam Chomsky (born 1928) is an American linguist, philosopher, cognitive scientist, historian, and political activist whose work has profoundly influenced multiple disciplines. Trained in linguistics at the University of Pennsylvania and long based at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), he revolutionized the study of language in the 1950s and 1960s by developing generative grammar, arguing that humans possess an innate linguistic capacity. This work reshaped modern linguistics, impacted cognitive science and psychology, and made him one of the most cited scholars in the humanities and social sciences.

Beyond his academic contributions to language and mind, Chomsky is widely known for his sharp critiques of U.S. foreign policy, state power, and the role of mass media in shaping public opinion. In books such as “Media Control: The Spectacular Achievements of Propaganda,” he examines how media systems can manufacture consent and marginalize dissent, themes that have made him a central figure in contemporary political discourse. Over decades, he has combined scholarly work with activism, speaking and writing extensively on war, human rights, and social justice.

Chomsky’s life has been marked by long-standing engagement with left-wing and libertarian socialist movements, as well as collaboration with other intellectuals and activists around the world. Raised in a Jewish family in Philadelphia with strong cultural and political interests, he became politically aware at an early age and remained an outspoken critic of authoritarianism in all forms. His combination of rigorous scholarship and public engagement has made him one of the most influential and controversial public intellectuals of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries.
