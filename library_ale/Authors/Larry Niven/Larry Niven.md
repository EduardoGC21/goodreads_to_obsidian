## Biography
Larry Niven (born 1938) is an American science fiction author best known for his hard-science approach to speculative ideas and his influential contributions to the genre’s depiction of space travel, alien ecologies, and future societies. Emerging in the 1960s New Wave era yet remaining strongly grounded in physics and engineering, he quickly gained prominence through a series of stories set in his “Known Space” universe, which explored interstellar politics, human and alien cultures, and the limits of technology. His 1970 novel “Ringworld,” which imagines a vast artificial ring encircling a star, became a landmark of science fiction worldbuilding and cemented his reputation among both fans and scientists for extrapolations that were simultaneously imaginative and technically minded.

Over his career, Niven has been closely associated with other major science fiction figures, frequently collaborating with authors such as Jerry Pournelle on works that blend military, political, and near-future themes with rigorous speculative detail. He has received multiple Hugo and Nebula Awards and has been credited with helping to shape modern space opera and hard science fiction, influencing subsequent generations of writers and readers. Though trained in mathematics, he devoted himself fully to writing, and his work has remained notable for its logical treatment of advanced technologies, its exploration of human and alien motivations, and its continued presence in discussions of classic science fiction.

Niven’s personal life has been more private than his public literary career, but he has long been associated with the American science fiction community through conventions, writers’ groups, and collaborative projects. His stories often reflect a fascination with how individuals and societies adapt to radically changed technological and cosmic conditions, and his enduring popularity rests on his ability to combine adventurous storytelling with careful scientific speculation.
