## Biography
Juan Rulfo (1917–1986) was a Mexican writer, screenwriter, and photographer whose small but extraordinarily influential body of work transformed 20th‑century Latin American literature. Born Juan Nepomuceno Carlos Pérez Rulfo Vizcaíno in the state of Jalisco, he grew up amid the economic ruin and violence that followed the Mexican Revolution and the Cristero War, losing both parents at a young age and being raised by relatives and in boarding schools. These early experiences of orphanhood, dispossession, and rural hardship became the emotional and thematic core of his fiction, which is set in stark landscapes populated by peasants, widows, and ghostly figures marked by violence and poverty.

Rulfo moved to Mexico City as a young man and worked in various government and commercial posts while he developed as a writer under the encouragement of literary friends. He published the short‑story collection "El llano en llamas" and later the novel "Pedro Páramo," works that combined experimental narrative techniques with a haunting blend of reality and the supernatural, and that powerfully reimagined rural Mexico after revolution. Alongside his lesser‑known novella "El gallo de oro," these books secured his reputation as one of the greatest Mexican authors, deeply admired by later writers such as Gabriel García Márquez and Jorge Luis Borges. Rulfo also gained recognition for his photography, which, like his prose, captured the desolate towns and landscapes of Mexico.

Despite publishing relatively little and abandoning book‑length fiction after his major works, Rulfo remained an important figure in Mexico’s cultural life. He worked for institutions dedicated to indigenous communities and served as an editor and mentor to younger authors, helping to shape subsequent generations of Mexican writers. His evocation of rural voices, his innovative handling of time, memory, and death, and his synthesis of local history with universal themes have made him a central reference point for studies of modern narrative and for the development of what came to be known as magical realism.
