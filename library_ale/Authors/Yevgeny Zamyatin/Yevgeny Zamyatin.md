## Biography
Yevgeny Zamyatin (1884–1937) was a Russian novelist, critic, and engineer whose work and political independence made him one of the early, emblematic dissident writers of the Soviet era. Born in Lebedyan, in the Russian Empire, he studied naval engineering in St. Petersburg and joined the Bolsheviks in his youth, for which he was arrested and exiled under the tsarist regime. After the 1917 Revolution he initially supported the new order, but his sharp, ironic view of authority and his insistence on artistic freedom soon brought him into conflict with Soviet cultural policy.

Zamyatin’s most famous work, the dystopian novel "We" (written in the early 1920s), used a mathematically ordered future state to critique totalitarianism, regimentation, and the suppression of individuality. Circulating abroad after being banned in the USSR, it influenced later dystopian classics and cemented his reputation as a prophetic figure in 20th‑century literature. As censorship tightened, Zamyatin was blacklisted, denied the right to publish or work, and subjected to intense pressure from the Soviet literary establishment.

Refusing to recant or compromise his artistic principles, Zamyatin appealed directly to Stalin for permission to leave the country. His request was granted, and he spent his final years in exile in Paris, living in relative poverty but continuing to write and mentor other émigré authors. He died in 1937, largely ignored in his homeland, but later recognized as a pioneering critic of totalitarianism and a significant precursor to modern dystopian fiction.
