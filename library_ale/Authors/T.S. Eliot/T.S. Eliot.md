## Biography
T. S. Eliot (Thomas Stearns Eliot, 1888–1965) was an American-born poet, critic, and dramatist who became one of the defining voices of literary modernism. Raised in a prominent New England-descended family in St. Louis, Missouri, he received an elite education at Harvard, the Sorbonne, and Oxford, developing a deep engagement with philosophy, comparative literature, and European culture. In 1914 he settled in England, where friendships with figures such as Ezra Pound helped shape his early experimental verse and launch his career.

Eliot spent years working as a teacher, bank clerk, and ultimately as an influential editor and publisher at Faber & Faber, where he championed many younger poets. His poems and essays, including landmark works like “The Waste Land” and later the meditative Four Quartets, transformed English-language poetry through their dense allusiveness, fragmented voices, and religious and philosophical depth. Alongside poetry he wrote verse dramas such as “Murder in the Cathedral,” which extended his exploration of spiritual crisis and authority into the theater.

In 1927 Eliot became a British citizen and an Anglo-Catholic, and questions of faith, tradition, and moral responsibility increasingly shaped both his writing and critical thought. His complicated personal life included an unhappy first marriage to Vivienne Haigh-Wood, periods of emotional strain, and later a more settled second marriage to Valerie Fletcher. Celebrated in his lifetime as a leading man of letters, he received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1948 and left a lasting imprint on twentieth-century poetry, criticism, and drama.
