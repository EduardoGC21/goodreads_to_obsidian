## Biography
Bram Stoker (1847–1912) was an Irish novelist and civil servant best known as the author of the Gothic horror classic "Dracula." Born in Clontarf, Dublin, he was a sickly child who spent much of his early years confined to bed, an experience that later fed his fascination with the uncanny and the supernatural. Stoker studied mathematics at Trinity College Dublin, where he also developed interests in theater, writing, and public speaking, and he initially worked as a clerk in the Irish civil service while writing reviews and short fiction on the side.

Stoker’s life changed direction when he became involved in the theater world, particularly through his admiration for the famous actor Sir Henry Irving. After meeting Irving, Stoker became his close friend, business manager, and the longtime manager of Irving’s Lyceum Theatre in London, a role that immersed him in late Victorian artistic and literary circles. During these years he wrote several novels and stories, with "Dracula" (1897)—inspired in part by European folklore, anxieties about modernity, and the atmosphere of fin-de-siècle London—becoming his most enduring work. The novel’s innovative blend of diary entries, letters, and reports helped define the modern vampire myth and left a lasting mark on popular culture.

Though Stoker never achieved great wealth from his fiction during his lifetime, his work gradually gained critical and popular recognition after his death, especially as stage and film adaptations of "Dracula" spread worldwide. He was married to Florence Balcombe, who carefully protected and managed his literary legacy after he died in London in 1912. Today Stoker is regarded as a central figure in Gothic literature, and his vision of the vampire continues to shape horror storytelling, spirituality, and cultural ideas about fear, sexuality, and the unknown.
