## Biography
Richard Burton Matheson (1926–2013) was an American writer and screenwriter whose work reshaped mid‑20th‑century horror, science fiction, and fantasy. Born in Allendale, New Jersey, to Norwegian immigrant parents and raised in Brooklyn by his mother after his parents’ divorce, he served in the U.S. Army in Europe during World War II before earning a journalism degree from the University of Missouri. Those experiences, especially his wartime service, informed novels such as "The Beardless Warriors" and gave his later fantastic tales a grounded, psychological realism.

After moving to California around 1951, Matheson became a prolific author of novels and short fiction and an in‑demand writer for film and television. His 1954 novel "I Am Legend" and other stories collected in volumes like "I Am Legend and Other Stories" helped define modern apocalyptic and psychological horror, influencing generations of writers and filmmakers. He also wrote many celebrated episodes of "The Twilight Zone" (including "Nightmare at 20,000 Feet"), as well as screenplays and teleplays such as the Spielberg‑directed "Duel" and adaptations of Edgar Allan Poe tales and his own novels. Over his career he received major honors including World Fantasy and Bram Stoker lifetime achievement awards and induction into the Science Fiction Hall of Fame.

In 1952 Matheson married Ruth Ann Woodson; they had four children, several of whom became writers, extending his creative legacy within his own family. He spent much of his later life in California, continuing to write across genres—horror, fantasy, science fiction, westerns, and metaphysical fiction—until his death in 2013. Widely regarded as a master craftsman of genre fiction, Matheson’s blend of everyday settings with existential and supernatural terrors left a lasting mark on literature, film, and television.
