## Biography
Delia Owens (born 1949) is an American zoologist, conservationist, and author whose work bridges rigorous wildlife science and popular storytelling. Raised in the woods around Thomasville, Georgia, she developed an early bond with nature that shaped both her scientific career and her later fiction. After earning a BS in zoology from the University of Georgia and a PhD in animal behavior from the University of California, Davis, she spent more than two decades living in remote parts of Africa, conducting field research on lions, elephants, and other wildlife and publishing in leading scientific journals. Her conservation work, including efforts to combat elephant poaching and support local communities in Zambia, earned her honors such as the John Burroughs Award for Nature Writing and the Order of the Golden Ark.

Together with her then-husband, Mark Owens, she co‑authored the bestselling memoirs "Cry of the Kalahari," "The Eye of the Elephant," and "Secrets of the Savanna," which chronicle their years in the Kalahari Desert and North Luangwa National Park. These books helped introduce a wide audience to the ecological and ethical complexities of wildlife conservation in southern Africa and cemented her reputation as a vivid chronicler of the natural world. Later, she settled in the United States, living first in the Northern Rockies and then in the mountains of North Carolina and Idaho, while continuing conservation and educational work.

Owens’s debut novel, "Where the Crawdads Sing" (2018), drew directly on her lifelong fascination with solitary lives in wild landscapes and became a global publishing phenomenon, topping bestseller lists for an extended period and inspiring a major film adaptation. The novel’s blend of marsh ecology, coming‑of‑age story, and mystery brought her scientific sensitivity to a new readership and intensified discussion of her earlier life and work in Africa. Across both her scientific and literary careers, Owens is known for exploring how isolation, belonging, and the behavior of social animals—including humans—are shaped by the environments they inhabit.
