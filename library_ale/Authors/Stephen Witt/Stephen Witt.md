## Biography
Stephen Richard Witt (born 1979) is an American journalist and nonfiction author whose work explores the technological and economic forces reshaping culture and industry. Raised in Minnesota after being born in New Hampshire, he studied at the University of Chicago and later trained as a reporter at Columbia University’s Graduate School of Journalism, grounding his investigative writing in both historical research and on‑the‑ground reporting. He has worked as an author, television producer, and investigative journalist, frequently contributing to major magazines while living in Los Angeles.

Witt first came to wide attention with his book on digital music piracy and the MP3 format, in which he reconstructed the interplay between inventors, executives, and underground release groups that transformed the music business. His later book, “The Thinking Machine: Jensen Huang, Nvidia, and the World’s Most Coveted Microchip,” extended his interest in technology and power into the world of semiconductors and artificial intelligence, framing Nvidia’s rise and Jensen Huang’s life story within the broader economic and geopolitical stakes of modern computing. Across his work, Witt is known for combining narrative drive with deeply reported accounts of how individual lives intersect with global systems.

In his personal life, Witt maintains close ties to the world of journalism; he is the elder brother of investigative journalist Emily Witt, and his own career has often overlapped with television and documentary projects adapted from his reporting. His books have been recognized by business and literary prizes, cementing his reputation as a significant contemporary chronicler of how media and technology shape everyday life.
