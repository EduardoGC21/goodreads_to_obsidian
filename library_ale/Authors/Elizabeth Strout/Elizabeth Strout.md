## Biography
Elizabeth Strout (born 1956 in Portland, Maine) is an American novelist celebrated for her nuanced, compassionate portrayals of ordinary people in small-town New England. Raised in Maine and New Hampshire by a science-professor father and an English-teacher mother, she grew up reading extensively, roaming the woods and coastlines that would later inform the emotional and physical landscapes of her fiction. From childhood she imagined herself a writer, honing her craft through notebooks and short stories before studying English at Bates College and later earning a law degree from Syracuse University, a profession she briefly practiced before committing fully to writing and teaching.

After years of publishing short fiction and working various jobs in New York City, Strout achieved major recognition with the novel "Amy and Isabelle" (1998), but it was "Olive Kitteridge" (2008) that brought her the Pulitzer Prize and established her as a central voice in contemporary American literature. Her interconnected novels and story cycles—set largely in fictional Maine towns and, in the Amgash/Lucy Barton books, in Illinois and New York—explore memory, class, family estrangement, and the quiet dramas of everyday life. A book like "Lucy by the Sea" extends these concerns into the recent past, tracing how long-standing relationships and old wounds play out under the pressures of historical events such as the COVID-19 pandemic.

Strout has taught writing at colleges including the Borough of Manhattan Community College and has been involved in MFA programs, mentoring younger writers while continuing a prolific late-career output. She divides her time between New York City and Maine with her husband, James Tierney, a former Maine attorney general, and has one daughter, the playwright Zarina Shea. Admired for spare prose, delicate psychological insight, and an ability to revisit characters across decades, Strout occupies a distinctive place in American letters as a chronicler of interior lives and the complicated bonds of families and communities.
