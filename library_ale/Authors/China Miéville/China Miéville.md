## Biography
China Miéville (born 1972) is a British writer and academic best known for his inventive, politically engaged works of speculative fiction. Often associated with the “New Weird” movement, he blends elements of fantasy, science fiction, crime, and horror with dense world‑building and an interest in social theory and radical politics. Trained in social anthropology and holding a PhD in international law, Miéville frequently draws on Marxism, critical theory, and urban studies, giving his imagined settings a strong sense of economic and political texture.

Over his career Miéville has won multiple major genre awards, including the Hugo, Arthur C. Clarke, and British Fantasy Awards, and he is widely regarded as one of the most original voices in contemporary speculative fiction. “The City & the City,” for example, uses the framework of a detective novel set across two overlapping cities to explore themes of borders, perception, and state power, illustrating his ability to combine genre conventions with ambitious conceptual experimentation. Beyond his fiction, he has written non‑fiction on international law and politics, and has been associated with left‑wing activism and commentary, situating his literary work within a broader intellectual and political project.

Miéville has also been active in the British literary scene as a critic, essayist, and teacher, influencing a generation of writers interested in pushing the boundaries of genre. His work often foregrounds marginalized characters and contested spaces, and he has been outspoken about the role of speculative fiction in challenging dominant narratives about society and history. Through his novels, short stories, and essays, he has become a key figure in discussions about the political potential of imaginative literature.
