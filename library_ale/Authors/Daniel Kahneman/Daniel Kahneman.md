## Biography
Daniel Kahneman (1934–2024) was an Israeli‑American psychologist whose work reshaped the understanding of human judgment, decision‑making, and economic behavior. Born in Tel Aviv and raised partly in France, he survived the Nazi occupation as a Jewish child before emigrating to what would become Israel. He studied psychology at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, later completing his PhD at the University of California, Berkeley, and began his career as a researcher and lecturer in Israel before taking positions at major American universities, including Princeton.

Kahneman’s landmark research, much of it conducted with his close collaborator Amos Tversky, demonstrated that people rely on systematic mental shortcuts—heuristics—that can lead to predictable biases in judgment under uncertainty. This work gave rise to the field of behavioral economics and profoundly influenced psychology, economics, public policy, and law. His book “Thinking, Fast and Slow” synthesized decades of research on the interplay between intuitive, fast thinking and deliberate, slow thinking, bringing his ideas to a broad audience. In 2002 he received the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for integrating psychological insights into economic theory, and he remained an influential voice in academia and public discourse until his death in 2024.
