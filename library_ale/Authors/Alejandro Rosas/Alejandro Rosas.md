## Biography
Alejandro Rosas (1969– ) is a Mexican historian, writer, and popularizer of history known for bringing Mexico’s past to broad audiences with a witty, accessible style. Born in Mexico City, he studied International Relations at the National Autonomous University of Mexico (UNAM) and soon oriented his career toward historical research and communication rather than traditional diplomacy or academia. He has worked as coordinator of Historical Research at Editorial Clío and as a content and outreach coordinator for official commemorations of Mexican history, including roles in the Presidential Internet System and the national commission for the 2010 bicentennial, which positioned him at the intersection of scholarship, public institutions, and mass media.

Rosas has built a prolific body of work focused on Mexican history, writing books that range from synthetic overviews and day‑by‑day chronicles to irreverent, humorous takes on national myths. Among his best‑known titles are “Mitos de la historia mexicana,” “365 días para conocer la historia de México,” and the trilogy “Érase una vez México,” which includes volumes such as “Érase una vez México I: De las cavernas al virreinato” and its continuation on the Independence and Revolution periods. Through these and other works like “México bizarro,” he has helped demystify historical figures and events, making them approachable without losing critical rigor.

In addition to his books, Rosas is a frequent presence in Mexican media, contributing to radio, television, and magazines devoted to historical and cultural commentary. He has collaborated with outlets such as W Radio, Radio Fórmula, TVUNAM, ADN 40, and the magazine “Relatos e Historias en México,” and has scripted documentaries and curated historical exhibitions. His combination of archival curiosity, narrative flair, and media savvy has made him one of the most recognizable contemporary voices in Mexican public history, influencing how many readers understand and debate the country’s past.
