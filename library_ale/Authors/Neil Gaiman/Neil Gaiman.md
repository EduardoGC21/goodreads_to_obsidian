## Biography
Neil Richard MacKinnon Gaiman (born 1960) is an English writer whose work has reshaped contemporary fantasy, horror, and comics. Raised in a Jewish family of Polish‑Eastern European origin, he grew up in Sussex after being born in Hampshire, England, and was an obsessive reader from childhood. He began his career as a journalist before moving into comics during the 1980s “British Invasion” of DC Comics, eventually creating the landmark series *The Sandman*, which established him as one of the most influential voices in modern speculative fiction.

Gaiman went on to write acclaimed novels, short fiction, children’s books, and screenplays, often blending myth, folklore, religion, and urban life into darkly imaginative narratives. Works such as *Neverwhere*, *American Gods*, *Coraline*, *Stardust*, and *The Graveyard Book* have earned major awards and broad popular readership, while *Good Omens*, co‑written with Terry Pratchett, and later adapted for television, showcases his long‑standing creative friendships and collaborative style. *Neverwhere*, first a BBC TV series and then a novel, helped cement the “London Below” urban‑fantasy tradition that many later writers drew on.

Over his career, Gaiman has lived in both the United Kingdom and the United States, raised four children, and become a prominent public advocate for libraries, free expression, and refugee rights. His personal life has included high‑profile relationships, notably his marriage to musician Amanda Palmer, and he has remained an active figure across literature, comics, and television. Despite recent public controversy and allegations that have affected some projects, his body of work continues to be regarded as central to late‑20th‑ and early‑21st‑century fantasy and graphic storytelling.
