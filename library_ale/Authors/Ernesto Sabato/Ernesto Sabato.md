## Biography
Ernesto Sabato (1911–2011) was an Argentine novelist, essayist, and physicist whose work and public life made him one of the central moral and literary voices of 20th‑century Argentina. Born in the small town of Rojas to Italian immigrant parents, he trained as a physicist at the National University of La Plata, earned a doctorate, and conducted research at the Curie Laboratory in Paris and later at MIT. His early political engagement in left‑wing and student movements, combined with his immersion in European avant‑garde circles, pushed him to abandon a promising scientific career in the 1940s to devote himself entirely to literature and reflection on the human condition.

Sabato’s first major success came with the philosophical essays of “Uno y el universo” and the existential novel “El túnel,” whose portrayal of obsessive, isolated subjectivity brought him international attention. Over the following decades he wrote only two more novels, “Sobre héroes y tumbas” and “Abaddón el exterminador,” along with a substantial body of essays in which he examined technology, dehumanization, faith, and the crisis of modern civilization. His work often returned to the tensions between reason and mystery, political commitment and metaphysical doubt, echoing his own journey from militant communist to anguished, independent critic of ideology.

Beyond literature, Sabato became a key public figure in the transition from dictatorship to democracy in Argentina. In 1983–1984 he presided over the National Commission on the Disappearance of Persons (CONADEP), whose report, published as “Nunca más,” documented state terror during the military regime and helped enable the trials of the juntas. Widely honored, including with the Miguel de Cervantes Prize, he spent his later years increasingly withdrawn, partly due to failing eyesight, turning more to painting and memoir. By the time of his death in the Buenos Aires suburb of Santos Lugares in 2011, he was widely regarded as one of Argentina’s last great “classic” writers and a conscience of his nation.
