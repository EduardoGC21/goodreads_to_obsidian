## Biography
J.K. Rowling (born 1965) is a British novelist best known as the creator of the Harry Potter series, one of the most successful and influential literary franchises of the late 20th and early 21st centuries. Raised in England and Wales, she developed a love of storytelling early in life and conceived the idea for Harry Potter while on a train journey in the early 1990s. During the years she spent drafting the first book, she faced significant personal and financial challenges, including single parenthood and periods of economic hardship, writing in cafes while her child slept.

The publication of the Harry Potter novels transformed Rowling into a global cultural figure, with the series praised for its imaginative world-building, moral complexity, and appeal to both children and adults. Companion works such as "The Tales of Beedle the Bard" further expanded the fictional universe and demonstrated her interest in creating a richly layered mythos. Beyond commercial success, she has received numerous literary honors and honorary degrees, and she has used her wealth and visibility to support philanthropic initiatives, particularly in the areas of poverty, children’s welfare, and multiple sclerosis research. Her public statements and views in later years have generated substantial debate and controversy, making her an enduringly prominent and sometimes polarizing figure in contemporary culture.
