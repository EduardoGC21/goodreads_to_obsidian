## Biography
Jorge Luis Borges (1899–1986) was an Argentine writer whose short stories, essays, and poems transformed 20th‑century literature. Born in Buenos Aires into an educated, bilingual family with Argentine and British roots, he grew up surrounded by his father’s vast library and learned English before Spanish. As a young man he lived in Geneva and Spain during World War I, absorbing European languages and avant‑garde movements before returning to Argentina in the early 1920s, where he became a central figure in Buenos Aires literary circles, helped introduce Ultraism in South America, and began publishing poems and essays that mythologized the city’s streets, suburbs, and knife‑fighters.

In the 1930s and 1940s Borges turned increasingly to fiction, often in concentrated forms that suited his failing eyesight. A serious head injury and subsequent infection in 1938, followed by progressive blindness, coincided with an extraordinary burst of creativity that produced many of his most celebrated stories, later gathered in collections such as *Ficciones* and *El Aleph*. Working part of his life as a modest librarian and later as director of the National Library of Argentina, he also collaborated closely with fellow writer Adolfo Bioy Casares on detective stories under shared pseudonyms. His intricate tales of labyrinths, infinite libraries, mirrors, and invented books made him a foundational figure of modern fantastic literature and a major influence on writers worldwide.

Borges’s personal life was marked by intense attachments and frustrations in love, a long, devoted relationship with his mother Leonor, and later companionship and collaboration with María Kodama, whom he married shortly before his death. From the 1960s on he traveled widely, lectured across Europe and the Americas, and received many international honors, even as the Nobel Prize eluded him. By the time he died in Geneva in 1986, Borges was recognized as one of the great cosmopolitan voices of the Spanish language, whose compressed, metaphysical fictions reshaped how readers around the world think about time, identity, and storytelling itself.
