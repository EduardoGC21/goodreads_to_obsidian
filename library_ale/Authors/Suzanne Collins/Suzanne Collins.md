## Biography
Suzanne Collins (born 1962) is an American author and screenwriter whose work reshaped contemporary young adult fiction through her blend of dystopian storytelling and social critique. Raised in a military family—the youngest of four children of U.S. Air Force officer Michael John Collins and Jane Brady Collins—she moved frequently between posts in the United States and Europe, experiences that deepened her awareness of war, history, and political power. She studied theater and telecommunications at Indiana University before earning an M.F.A. in dramatic writing from New York University, training that gave her a strong grounding in narrative structure and character-driven storytelling.

Collins began her career writing for children’s television in the early 1990s, contributing to series on networks such as Nickelodeon and PBS and eventually serving as head writer on the animated show “Clifford’s Puppy Days.” At the same time she developed her own fiction, first with the fantasy sequence The Underland Chronicles and later with The Hunger Games trilogy, whose bleak, media-saturated dystopia and morally conflicted heroine made the books a global phenomenon and inspired a hugely successful film franchise. Her work is notable for bringing themes of war trauma, state violence, and resistance into mainstream youth literature while remaining accessible and emotionally resonant.

Collins has generally guarded her private life, but it is known that she married actor Charles “Cap” Pryor in 1992 and has two children. She has lived for many years in Connecticut, continuing to write novels and screenplays, including prequels expanding the world of The Hunger Games. Her enduring significance lies in the way she fused commercial popularity with serious questions about power, propaganda, and the ethics of violence for a young readership.
