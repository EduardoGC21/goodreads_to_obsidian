## Biography
Rebecca F. Kuang (publishing as R.F. Kuang) is a Chinese-American novelist born in 1996 in Guangzhou who has become one of the most prominent contemporary voices in speculative and literary fiction. Emigrating to the United States at the age of four, she grew up in Dallas, Texas in a family deeply shaped by the history of twentieth‑century China, including relatives who experienced the Japanese occupation of Hunan and fought for Chiang Kai-shek. This interwoven legacy of war, revolution, and diaspora underpins much of her fiction, which frequently interrogates empire, violence, and the political uses of history and language.

Kuang studied history and international affairs at Georgetown University, then pursued advanced degrees in Sinology at the University of Cambridge and the University of Oxford as a Marshall Scholar before beginning doctoral work at Yale University. While still an undergraduate she drafted her debut novel, The Poppy War, launching a critically acclaimed trilogy that reimagines modern Chinese history through dark military fantasy. With later works such as "Babel"—an alternative-history novel about translation, colonialism, and resistance that reached the top of The New York Times Best Seller list and won the Nebula Award—she consolidated a reputation for combining rigorous historical and linguistic research with accessible, emotionally intense storytelling.

Across her career Kuang has been recognized with honors including the Compton Crook and Crawford Awards and the Astounding Award for Best New Writer, and has been named to lists such as Time’s 100 Next. Her novels often explore how institutions—universities, armies, publishing itself—shape identity and opportunity, and she has become an influential figure in conversations about representation, power, and authorship in contemporary Anglophone literature.
