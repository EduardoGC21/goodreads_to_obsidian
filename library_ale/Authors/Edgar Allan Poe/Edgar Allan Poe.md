## Biography
Edgar Allan Poe (1809–1849) was an American writer, poet, editor, and critic whose macabre tales and psychologically intense poems helped define modern horror and dark Romanticism. Born in Boston and orphaned at a young age, he was taken in by the Allan family of Richmond, Virginia, from whom he derived his middle name, though he never was formally adopted and his relationship with his foster father was strained and often adversarial. Financial instability and personal loss marked much of his life, shaping the bleak emotional landscapes of his work.

Poe worked as an editor and critic for several literary magazines, becoming known for his sharp, often ruthless reviews and for championing the idea that literature should aim at artistic effect rather than moral instruction. He married his much younger cousin, Virginia Clemm, whom he deeply loved; her long illness and early death intensified the themes of beauty, death, and mourning that run through his stories and poems. Across tales of mystery and the grotesque as well as pioneering detective fiction, Poe exerted a profound influence on later writers in America and Europe.

Despite periods of recognition and some public fame, Poe struggled with poverty, ill health, and episodes of heavy drinking. His death in Baltimore in 1849, under mysterious circumstances, fueled the legend that surrounds him. Posthumously, his reputation grew, and he is now regarded as one of the most important figures in American literature, a master of atmosphere whose innovations in short fiction, poetry, and literary theory helped shape genres from crime and science fiction to psychological horror.
