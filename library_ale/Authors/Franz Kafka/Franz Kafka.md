## Biography
Franz Kafka (1883–1924) was a German-language writer born in Prague to a middle-class Jewish family in what was then the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Trained as a lawyer, he spent most of his adult life working in insurance while writing fiction privately and largely unpublished in his lifetime. His difficult relationship with his domineering father, his sense of cultural and linguistic marginality as a German-speaking Jew in Prague, and his fragile health all fed into the themes of alienation, guilt, and bureaucratic oppression that characterize his work.

Kafka’s fiction, including novellas such as "The Metamorphosis" and other animal tales, developed a unique blend of the ordinary and the nightmarishly surreal, in which characters find themselves trapped in incomprehensible situations and labyrinthine institutions. He had a small circle of close friends and confidants, most notably Max Brod, who ignored Kafka’s request to destroy his manuscripts after his death and instead edited and published them. This act ensured Kafka’s posthumous influence, and he is now regarded as one of the most significant writers of the 20th century, shaping modern understandings of existential anxiety and the absurdity of modern bureaucracy.

Kafka never married, though he had several intense and often troubled romantic relationships, and he died relatively young of complications from tuberculosis in a sanatorium near Vienna. His life was marked by a tension between his conventional professional existence and his inner drive to write, a tension that mirrors the split identities and divided loyalties of his protagonists. The adjective "Kafkaesque" has entered many languages to describe situations reminiscent of his fictional worlds, underscoring the lasting cultural impact of his work.
