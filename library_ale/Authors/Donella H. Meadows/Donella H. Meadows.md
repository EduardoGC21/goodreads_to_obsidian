## Biography
Donella Hager “Dana” Meadows (1941–2001) was an American environmental scientist, systems thinker, and writer whose work reshaped global conversations about ecological limits and sustainable development. Born in Elgin, Illinois, she studied chemistry at Carleton College and earned a Ph.D. in biophysics from Harvard University. Early in her career she joined Jay Forrester’s systems dynamics group at MIT, where she helped develop the World3 model for the Club of Rome and became the lead author of the landmark 1972 study and book *The Limits to Growth*, which brought systems analysis and planetary boundaries into public debate.

In 1972 Meadows joined the Environmental Studies program at Dartmouth College, where she taught for nearly three decades while maintaining an active research, writing, and public‐education career. She wrote the syndicated column “The Global Citizen,” co‑founded the Balaton Group, and later established the Sustainability Institute, combining rigorous systems modeling with practical experiments in sustainable living on communal farms and eco‑villages in New England. Her posthumously published book *Thinking in Systems: A Primer* distilled her lifetime of teaching into an accessible framework that continues to influence policymakers, activists, and scholars seeking to understand and change complex social‑ecological systems.

Meadows’ life was deeply rooted in both local community and international collaboration. She lived for many years on an organic farm in Plainfield, New Hampshire, shared her work and reflections through personal newsletters, and was known for integrating personal ethics, community building, and global responsibility. She received major honors including a MacArthur Fellowship and a Pew Scholarship in Conservation and the Environment, and remained active in networks for sustainability until her death from bacterial meningitis in 2001.
