## Biography
Iain Menzies Banks (1954–2013) was a Scottish novelist whose work spanned both literary fiction and large‑scale science fiction. Born in Dunfermline, Fife, to a Royal Navy officer father and a former professional ice skater mother, he grew up around North Queensferry and later studied English and philosophy at the University of Stirling. After university he worked a series of jobs while writing, enduring years of rejection before the dark and controversial novel “The Wasp Factory” (1984) brought him sudden acclaim and allowed him to become a full‑time writer.

Under the name Iain Banks he produced a string of influential contemporary novels rooted largely in Scottish settings, while as Iain M. Banks he became a defining figure in modern space opera with the Culture series, launched by “Consider Phlebas” (1987). His books are noted for their imaginative scope, moral and political engagement, and playful structural experimentation, helping to blur the boundaries between genre and mainstream fiction and earning him recognition as one of the most important British writers of his generation.

Banks’s personal life included a long, sometimes turbulent relationship and later marriage to Annie Banks, and in his later years a partnership and marriage with Adele Hartley, who became his widow shortly before his death. Openly atheist and politically outspoken, he was admired for his warmth and candor with readers as well as for his fiction. In March 2013 he announced that he had terminal gallbladder cancer; he died a few months later, in June 2013, leaving a body of work that has continued to influence both literary and science‑fiction writers worldwide.
