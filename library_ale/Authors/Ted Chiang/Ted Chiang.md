## Biography
Ted Chiang (1967– ) is an American science fiction writer known for his meticulous, idea-driven short fiction that blends rigorous speculative concepts with emotional and philosophical depth. Born in Port Jefferson, New York, to Chinese immigrant parents, he studied computer science at Brown University and has long balanced a career in the software industry with his literary work, publishing relatively few stories but earning an outsized critical reputation. His background in both technology and literature informs his precise, patient prose and his interest in how scientific advances reshape human identity, memory, and morality.

Chiang’s stories have received numerous major awards, including multiple Hugo, Nebula, and Locus Awards, despite his modest output and preference for short forms over novels. Collections such as “Stories of Your Life and Others” and “Exhalation” have solidified his status as one of contemporary science fiction’s most influential voices, with works like “Story of Your Life” adapted into the acclaimed film "Arrival". Pieces such as “The Lifecycle of Software Objects” and those in “Exhalation” exemplify his focus on artificial intelligence, free will, and the ethics of creation, often exploring intimate relationships—between parents and children, creators and their creations, or humans and technology—against speculative backdrops.

Chiang is also noted for his deliberate publishing pace and his reluctance to engage heavily in public self-promotion, giving his career a somewhat reclusive aura compared with many of his peers. Nevertheless, he has become a central figure in discussions about how science fiction can interrogate determinism, theology, and the social consequences of innovation. His work is frequently studied in academic contexts and cited by both writers and technologists, highlighting his significance at the intersection of speculative literature and contemporary debates about science and society.
