## Biography
Harlan Jay Ellison (1934–2018) was an American writer whose fierce intellect, prolific output, and combative personality made him one of the most distinctive voices in twentieth-century speculative fiction. Born in Cleveland, Ohio, to a Jewish family, he spent a restless youth working odd jobs across the United States before turning fully to writing. After a brief and contentious stint at Ohio State University and service in the U.S. Army, he moved into professional publishing, producing crime fiction, confessional pieces, and, increasingly, the incendiary short stories and novellas that defined his reputation.

Ellison became a central figure in the New Wave of speculative fiction, known for dense, morally charged tales such as "‘Repent, Harlequin!’ Said the Ticktockman," "A Boy and His Dog," and the collection "I Have No Mouth & I Must Scream," whose title story helped cement his status as a master of bleak, visionary science fiction. He was also a major force in television and film, writing influential scripts for series including "Star Trek" (notably "The City on the Edge of Forever"), "The Outer Limits," and "The Twilight Zone," and editing boundary-pushing anthologies like "Dangerous Visions" that showcased and shaped a generation of writers. Throughout his career he won numerous Hugo, Nebula, Edgar, and other awards.

Ellison’s life was as controversial as his work. Famous for his sharp tongue and refusal to compromise, he engaged in highly public disputes over artistic control and plagiarism, yet friends and collaborators also described him as fiercely loyal and principled. Married multiple times before a long final marriage to Susan Toth, he lived for decades in Los Angeles, where his home—nicknamed "The Lost Aztec Temple of Mars"—became a kind of shrine to his obsessions with books, film, and popular culture. His legacy lies in his relentless defense of writers’ rights and in a body of work that pushed science fiction and fantasy toward greater literary ambition and social engagement.
