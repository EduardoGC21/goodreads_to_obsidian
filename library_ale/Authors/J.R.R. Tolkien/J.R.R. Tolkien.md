## Biography
J.R.R. Tolkien (1892–1973) was an English philologist, academic, and writer whose imaginative world-building and invented languages reshaped modern fantasy literature. Born in Bloemfontein, South Africa, to English parents and raised in England, he developed an early passion for languages, mythology, and medieval literature. He studied at Exeter College, Oxford, served as a signals officer in the First World War, and later became a professor of Anglo-Saxon and English language and literature at the University of Oxford, where his scholarship on works such as Beowulf was highly influential.

While at Oxford, Tolkien was part of the informal literary group the Inklings, whose members included C.S. Lewis; their meetings and friendships fostered discussion of myth, faith, and storytelling that shaped his creative work. Out of this context grew the legendarium behind "The Hobbit," "The Lord of the Rings," and posthumously published works such as "The Silmarillion" and related tales like "The Children of Húrin" and "Beren and Lúthien," edited and organized in large part by his son Christopher Tolkien. His careful blending of invented histories, languages, and moral themes created a richly layered secondary world that has influenced generations of writers, artists, and readers, cementing Tolkien’s status as one of the central figures in twentieth-century literature.

Tolkien’s personal life was marked by deep Catholic faith and a long, devoted marriage to Edith Bratt, whose love story with him echoes through several of his fictional romances. A reserved but warm teacher, he was known for his meticulous approach to language and his perfectionism, often revising texts over many years. By the time of his death in 1973, Tolkien’s work had already begun to acquire a global readership, and in the decades since, his Middle-earth legendarium has become a cultural touchstone, underpinning modern high fantasy and inspiring extensive adaptations, scholarship, and fan communities.
