## Biography
Heather Morris (born 1953) is a New Zealand-born author of historical fiction whose work focuses on testimonies of survival, resilience, and love in the midst of genocide and war. Raised in Te Awamutu in New Zealand’s North Island, she moved between New Zealand and Australia from the early 1970s, eventually settling in Melbourne. There she balanced family life, work in the social work department of a major public hospital, and formal study in political science, sociology, and professional scriptwriting, nurturing a long-standing interest in how ordinary people navigate extraordinary trauma.

In 2003 Morris was introduced to Lale Sokolov, a Holocaust survivor who had been a tattooist at Auschwitz-Birkenau. Over several years of conversations, he entrusted her with the intimate details of his experiences and his enduring love story with his future wife, Gita. Morris first shaped his account into a screenplay and later reworked it into her debut novel, The Tattooist of Auschwitz, which became an international bestseller and the foundation of a broader body of work about Holocaust survivors and other victims of wartime brutality. Her subsequent books, including companion novels and later works on women in Japanese POW camps, have extended her exploration of memory, moral courage, and the bonds that help people endure persecution.

Morris’s writing is marked by close collaboration with survivors and their families, and she has become a prominent literary voice bringing personal wartime narratives to a global popular audience. Living in Australia while maintaining her New Zealand roots, she continues to write both historical and contemporary stories that foreground empathy and hope, often drawing directly on the lives of individuals whose experiences might otherwise remain unknown.
