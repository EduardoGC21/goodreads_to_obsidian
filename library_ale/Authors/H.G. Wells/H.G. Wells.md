## Biography
Herbert George Wells (1866–1946) was an English writer and public intellectual whose work helped define modern science fiction while also engaging deeply with the social and political tensions of his time. Born into a lower‑middle‑class family in Bromley, Kent, he rose from shop work and teaching to study science under T. H. Huxley, an experience that shaped his lifelong fascination with evolution, technology, and social change. His early struggles with class, health, and employment informed his sensitivity to inequality and progress, themes that recur throughout his writing.

Wells achieved lasting fame with pioneering scientific romances such as "The War of the Worlds," in which a Martian invasion of Earth becomes a vehicle for examining imperialism, vulnerability, and the fragility of human civilization. Alongside these visionary tales, he wrote realist novels, essays, and extensive works of social critique, arguing for education, rational planning, and global cooperation as means to prevent catastrophe. His relationships with leading thinkers, political figures, and fellow writers placed him at the center of early 20th‑century debates about socialism, world government, and the ethical use of science. Though his political forecasts and personal life were often controversial, Wells left a profound legacy as a writer who believed literature could confront the possibilities and dangers of the future.
