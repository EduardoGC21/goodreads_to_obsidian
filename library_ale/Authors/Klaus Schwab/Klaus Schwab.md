## Biography
Klaus Martin Schwab (born 1938) is a German engineer and economist best known as the founder and long‑time leader of the World Economic Forum (WEF), the organization behind the annual gathering of political, business, and civil‑society figures in Davos, Switzerland. Raised in postwar Europe and educated in both engineering and economics, he studied at ETH Zurich and the University of Fribourg, later adding a public administration degree from Harvard. In 1971 he organized a management symposium in Davos that evolved into the European Management Forum and, from 1987, the World Economic Forum, giving him a central role in debates about globalization, corporate responsibility, and the relationship between public and private power.

Alongside his work at the WEF, Schwab served for decades as a professor of business policy at the University of Geneva and, with his wife Hilde, co‑founded the Schwab Foundation for Social Entrepreneurship to promote socially oriented business leadership. He became widely known as a convener of heads of state and global CEOs, and as a prolific author on economic and technological change, including works on the “Fourth Industrial Revolution,” such as his book later translated as “La cuarta revolución industrial.” His agenda emphasized public‑private cooperation and stakeholder capitalism, drawing both admiration for fostering dialogue on global challenges and criticism over the concentration of influence among political and economic elites.

Schwab’s personal life has been closely tied to the institutions he created. He married Hilde Schwab in 1971, and they settled in Switzerland, raising two children who would also become involved in WEF‑related activities. Over more than half a century he shaped Davos into a symbol of international elite networking, leaving a complex legacy that combines institutional innovation, advocacy for business engagement in social issues, and controversy over governance, transparency, and the broader consequences of globalization.
