## Biography
Yuval Noah Harari (born 1976) is an Israeli historian and public intellectual whose work has reshaped popular understanding of human history and humanity’s future. Raised in a secular Jewish family in Kiryat Ata, Israel, he showed early intellectual promise, teaching himself to read at a young age and later studying in a program for gifted children. He read history and international relations at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem before completing a D.Phil. in history at the University of Oxford in 2002, specializing in medieval and military history.

After returning to Israel, Harari joined the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, where he became known for wide‑ranging courses in world history and macrohistorical processes. His research and writing moved from specialized military-history studies toward big‑picture questions about the relationship between biology and history, the distinctiveness of Homo sapiens, and the trajectories of human societies. The lecture series that inspired “Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind” and later “21 Lessons for the 21st Century” brought him global prominence, positioning him as a leading voice on how long-term historical forces, technology, and ideology shape contemporary and future life.

Harari is also notable for how his personal life and practices intersect with his intellectual outlook. Openly gay, he has spoken about how questioning inherited norms informed both his personal identity and his scholarly stance, and he lives with his husband and manager, Itzik Yahav, near Jerusalem. A dedicated practitioner of Vipassana meditation and a committed advocate of reducing harm to animals, he has said that intensive meditation retreats and confronting the realities of industrial farming significantly influenced his thinking about consciousness, suffering, and ethics—concerns that run through his reflections on the twenty‑first century and beyond.
