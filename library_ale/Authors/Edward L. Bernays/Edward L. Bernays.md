## Biography
Edward L. Bernays (1891–1995) was an Austrian-born American pioneer of public relations and modern propaganda, often described as “the father of public relations.” Born in Vienna to a Jewish family closely connected to Sigmund Freud—his mother was Freud’s sister and his father was brother to Freud’s wife—Bernays moved as a child to New York City, where he was raised and educated. After graduating from Cornell University in 1912, he turned from agriculture to journalism and publicity work, finding early success promoting the controversial play *Damaged Goods* and then serving on the U.S. Committee on Public Information during World War I, an experience that shaped his lifelong belief in the power of organized persuasion.

In the 1920s Bernays set himself up as one of the first professional “public relations counselors,” consciously applying insights from crowd psychology and psychoanalysis to influence public opinion. Through campaigns for corporations, trade groups, and governments, he helped popularize consumer products, shape social habits, and legitimize public relations as a field. His books, including *Crystallizing Public Opinion* (1923) and *Propaganda* (1928), articulated a theory of managing the “engineering of consent,” arguing that democratic societies inevitably rely on elites who guide mass opinion—ideas that have remained both influential and deeply controversial.

Bernays married writer and fellow public relations practitioner Doris E. Fleischman in 1922, and the couple became a notable professional partnership in New York’s interwar media and business circles. Throughout his long life he advised clients ranging from major corporations to non-profit organizations and political bodies, and he remained an energetic advocate for his profession into his nineties. His legacy is twofold: he helped establish public relations as a central feature of modern political and commercial life, and he became a symbol—and frequent target—of criticism for the ethical and democratic implications of systematic opinion management, particularly in light of campaigns such as those described in his book *Propaganda*.
