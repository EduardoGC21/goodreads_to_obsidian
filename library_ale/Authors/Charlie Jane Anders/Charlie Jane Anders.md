## Biography
Charlie Jane Anders is an American speculative fiction writer, critic, and cultural commentator whose work bridges science fiction, fantasy, and contemporary social issues. Born in rural Connecticut in 1969, she grew up in Mansfield and later studied English and Asian literature at the University of Cambridge, spending time in China before settling in San Francisco. Living with a sensory processing disorder, she has often credited a dedicated special-education teacher with nurturing her love of writing, turning an early struggle with literacy into a lifelong vocation.

In San Francisco, Anders became a central figure in queer and genre literary communities. With her partner, writer and journalist Annalee Newitz, she co-founded Other magazine, a pop‑culture and politics magazine for “new outcasts,” and later helped launch the influential science fiction and futurism blog io9, eventually serving as its editor in chief. She has long hosted the celebrated reading series “Writers with Drinks,” known for its eclectic mix of genres and surreal, free‑associative author introductions, and has organized events and tours highlighting transgender and genderqueer voices.

As an author, Anders has earned major awards and critical acclaim across multiple forms. Her early novel "Choir Boy" won a Lambda Literary Award, while later works such as "Six Months, Three Days" and the novel "All the Birds in the Sky"—a blend of magic, science, and relationships at the end of the world—received Hugo, Nebula, Sturgeon, Crawford, and Locus honors and helped establish her as a leading voice in contemporary speculative fiction. She went on to write the climate‑inflected space opera "The City in the Middle of the Night" and the young‑adult Unstoppable trilogy, and co‑created Escapade, a transgender mutant superhero for Marvel Comics. Beyond fiction, she has written about using creativity to survive difficult times and currently serves as a science fiction and fantasy book reviewer for the Washington Post, while continuing to co‑host the Hugo‑winning podcast "Our Opinions Are Correct" with Newitz.
