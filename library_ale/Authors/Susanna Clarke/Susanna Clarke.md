## Biography
Susanna Clarke (1959–) is an English novelist best known for her richly imaginative contributions to contemporary fantasy literature. Born in Nottingham, England, she grew up in a family that moved frequently due to her father’s work in the Methodist ministry, a childhood that exposed her to a variety of British landscapes and histories that later informed the atmosphere of her fiction. After studying philosophy, politics, and economics, she worked in publishing and teaching English abroad, gradually developing her voice as a writer while supporting herself with day jobs.

Clarke achieved international acclaim with her debut novel, "Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell" (2004), an alternative-history fantasy set in a meticulously realized 19th‑century England where magic has returned. The book’s intricate world‑building, scholarly footnotes, and Austen‑ and Dickens‑inflected prose earned her major awards and a reputation as a singular stylist in modern fantasy. After its success, she struggled for years with chronic illness, which significantly slowed her output but deepened the sense of solitude, interiority, and resilience that often suffuses her work.

Her second novel, "Piranesi" (2020), marked a celebrated return, winning the Women’s Prize for Fiction and further cementing her status as an author who blends the fantastical with emotional and philosophical depth. Clarke is frequently noted for her deft interweaving of myth, history, and everyday life, and for exploring themes of isolation, identity, and the costs and uses of power. She has lived much of her adult life in England, often in close partnership with fellow writers and editors who supported her long gestation periods between books, and she remains an influential, if deliberately low‑profile, figure in contemporary literature.
