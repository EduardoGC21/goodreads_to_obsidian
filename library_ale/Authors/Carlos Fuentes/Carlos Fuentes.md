## Biography
Carlos Fuentes (1928–2012) was a Mexican novelist, essayist, and diplomat who became one of the central figures of the Latin American Boom. Born in Panama City to Mexican parents, he spent his childhood following his diplomat father through capital cities across the Americas and Europe, gaining an early cosmopolitan outlook and fluency in multiple cultures and languages. As a teenager he returned to Mexico, studied law in Mexico City and Geneva, and soon turned to letters, cofounding influential literary journals and entering public service in Mexico’s foreign ministry.

Fuentes’s fiction explored Mexican history, identity, and social stratification with formal experimentation and political urgency. Early works such as “Where the Air Is Clear” and “Las buenas conciencias” dissected the moral compromises of the Mexican middle class, while “Aura” and later novels fused the fantastic and the historical to probe memory, power, and myth. “The Death of Artemio Cruz” secured his global reputation, and books like “Terra Nostra,” “The Old Gringo,” and essays such as those later collected in “Nuevo tiempo mexicano” confirmed his role as a major interpreter of Mexico and the Hispanic world.

Alongside his literary career, Fuentes served as Mexico’s ambassador to France and taught at leading universities in Europe and the United States, becoming an intellectual bridge between Latin America and the wider world. Widely honored with prizes including the Cervantes Prize and the Prince of Asturias Award, he was often mentioned as a likely Nobel laureate. His personal life included two marriages and three children, and he remained an engaged public intellectual until his death in Mexico City in 2012, leaving a body of work that continues to shape understandings of Mexican and Latin American modernity.
