## Biography
Ocean Vuong (born 1988) is a Vietnamese American poet, novelist, and essayist whose work explores war, displacement, family, and queer identity. Born in Saigon, Vietnam, he immigrated to the United States as a child with his family, who came from a background marked by the Vietnam War and refugee experience. Raised primarily by women in a working-class household, Vuong has often drawn on his close relationship with his mother and grandmother, as well as his experience of learning English as a second language, to shape his lyrical and formally inventive writing.

Vuong first gained wide recognition as a poet, winning major literary prizes for collections that examine memory, violence, tenderness, and the inheritance of trauma. His novel "On Earth We're Briefly Gorgeous" is written as a letter from a son to his illiterate mother, blending autobiography and fiction to trace the complexities of immigrant life, masculinity, and love in the United States. In subsequent work such as "Time Is a Mother," he continued to address grief—especially after his mother’s death—while deepening his exploration of language and identity.

Widely regarded as one of the most important contemporary voices in American letters, Vuong has received numerous fellowships and awards, and he has also taught writing at the university level, mentoring a younger generation of poets and writers. His life and career embody a bridge between Vietnamese and American histories, and between personal vulnerability and literary innovation.
