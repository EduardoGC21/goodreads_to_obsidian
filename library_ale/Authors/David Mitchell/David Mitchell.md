## Biography
David Stephen Mitchell (born 1969) is an English novelist and screenwriter whose fiction is known for its intricate structures, global settings, and lyrical prose. Born in the seaside town of Southport and raised in Malvern, Worcestershire, he studied English and American literature and later comparative literature at the University of Kent. A formative period of his life was spent abroad—first in Sicily and then in Hiroshima, Japan, where he taught English for several years and began to write seriously, experiences that strongly shaped the international scope and cultural hybridity of his work.

Mitchell’s debut, "Ghostwritten" (1999), introduced his taste for interlinked narratives, but "Cloud Atlas" (2004) made his reputation, confirming him as one of the most formally adventurous British novelists of his generation. Its nested, centuries-spanning stories exemplify his interest in how individual lives echo across time and place, and it brought him major prizes, a Booker Prize shortlisting, and later a high-profile film adaptation, which he helped adapt for the screen. Other acclaimed novels, including "number9dream," "Black Swan Green," "The Thousand Autumns of Jacob de Zoet," and "The Bone Clocks," have reinforced his status as a key contemporary voice in Anglophone fiction, and he has also worked as a translator and co-writer on projects about autism and on large-scale screen works such as "The Matrix Resurrections."

Mitchell lives in Ireland with his Japanese-born wife, Keiko Yoshida, and their children. His family life, including parenting a severely autistic son, has informed his advocacy and his translation of Japanese writing on autism into English. Having grown up with a stammer and periods of solitude, he has spoken about how reading and language became both refuge and vocation, and his fiction often reflects a deep ethical preoccupation with empathy, interconnectedness, and the moral consequences of human ambition.
