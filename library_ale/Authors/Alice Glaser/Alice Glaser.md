## Biography
Alice Glaser (1928–1970) was an American writer and magazine editor best known for her influential dystopian short story "The Tunnel Ahead" (1961). Raised on Long Island, she studied at Radcliffe College, where she completed a senior thesis on Joseph Conrad, and became part of the postwar generation of highly educated women entering literary and journalistic careers. For a time she lived in Paris, absorbing the intellectual and artistic ferment of the period before returning to the United States.

From 1958 Glaser worked at Esquire magazine in New York, eventually serving as an associate editor under Harold Hayes. In that role she corresponded and collaborated with prominent cultural figures, including writers, civil-rights leaders, and photographers, and wrote essays and reportage that examined contemporary youth culture, travel, and social change. Her story "The Tunnel Ahead," depicting an overpopulated future United States in which random motorists are sacrificed to population control, became a frequently anthologized work of modern speculative fiction and later inspired the award-winning short film "The Tunnel" (2016), securing her a lasting place in science-fiction history despite her small fictional output.

Glaser’s life intersected with many of the central social and cultural movements of the 1950s and 1960s, from the Beat generation to second-wave reflections on women’s roles in professional life. She died in New York in 1970, at 41, after a fall that contemporaries reported as possibly a suicide, leaving behind a reputation as a sharp, socially engaged editor whose single major science-fiction work achieved enduring recognition.
