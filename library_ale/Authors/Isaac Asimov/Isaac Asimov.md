## Biography
Isaac Asimov (1920–1992) was a Russian-born American writer, biochemist, and one of the most influential authors in the history of science fiction. Born in Petrovichi, in what was then the Russian SFSR, he emigrated with his family to the United States as a small child and grew up in Brooklyn, New York, helping in his parents’ candy stores. A voracious reader with an early fascination for science and pulp magazines, he began writing stories in his teens and soon sold his work to the leading science-fiction magazines of the day.

Asimov earned a PhD in biochemistry from Columbia University and taught at Boston University, but he achieved global fame through his fiction and popular science writing. His “Foundation” series, including books such as “Foundation,” “Foundation and Empire,” and “Second Foundation,” helped define the grand-scale, idea-driven tradition of modern science fiction and shaped how generations imagined galactic empires and the future of human civilization. In parallel, novels like “The End of Eternity” and short stories such as “The Last Question” explored time travel, entropy, and humanity’s long-term destiny, showcasing his talent for turning abstract scientific and philosophical problems into compelling narratives.

Prolific and disciplined, Asimov wrote or edited hundreds of books across genres, from robot stories and detective fiction to histories and explanatory works on physics, chemistry, and the Bible. He was known for his clear, conversational style, skepticism, and humanistic outlook, as well as for his active participation in the science-fiction community and close friendships with fellow authors such as Arthur C. Clarke and Robert Heinlein. Until his death in 1992, he remained a central public advocate for science literacy and rational inquiry, leaving a legacy that continues to influence both literature and popular understanding of science and technology.
