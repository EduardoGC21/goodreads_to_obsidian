## Biography
Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley (1797–1851) was an English novelist and intellectual born into a prominent radical literary family in London. She was the daughter of the pioneering feminist writer Mary Wollstonecraft and the philosopher William Godwin, though her mother died shortly after her birth. Raised in an environment that valued political debate and literature, Shelley received an unconventional but rich education, absorbing ideas about reason, rights, and social reform that would influence her later work.

As a teenager she eloped with the Romantic poet Percy Bysshe Shelley, with whom she shared an intense, often difficult life marked by financial instability, frequent travel across Britain and the Continent, and the deaths of several children. Their circle included major Romantic figures such as Lord Byron. During a stay near Lake Geneva in 1816, a storytelling challenge among friends inspired her to conceive the novel "Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus," a groundbreaking work that blends Gothic fiction with reflections on scientific ambition, responsibility, and isolation. Published in 1818, it secured her lasting place in literary history and is often considered a foundational text of modern science fiction.

After Percy Shelley’s death by drowning in 1822, Mary Shelley supported herself and her surviving son through her writing and careful management of her late husband’s literary reputation. She produced additional novels, short stories, biographies, and travel writings, and quietly advocated for progressive causes within the constraints placed on women of her time. Her life was shaped by personal loss, intellectual ambition, and the effort to carve out a literary career as a woman in the early nineteenth century. She died in London in 1851, leaving a legacy that continues to influence literature, philosophy, and cultural understandings of creation and responsibility.
