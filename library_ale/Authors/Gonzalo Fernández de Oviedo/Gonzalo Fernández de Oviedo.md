## Biography
Gonzalo Fernández de Oviedo y Valdés (1478–1557) was a Spanish chronicler, soldier, and administrator whose writings became some of the earliest and most influential accounts of Spain’s expansion in the Americas. Born in Madrid into a minor noble family, he served at the court of the Catholic Monarchs and later held various posts in Italy, where he was exposed to humanist learning and the culture of the Renaissance. This background shaped his approach as a historian, combining classical models with close observation and a desire to document the unprecedented events and environments of the New World.

Oviedo traveled repeatedly to the Caribbean and Central America, ultimately serving as royal chronicler of the Indies and as an official in Santo Domingo, where he observed colonial society, indigenous peoples, and the natural world at close range. His works, including the "Sumario de la natural historia de las Indias" and the much larger "Historia general y natural de las Indias," sought to describe American landscapes, plants, animals, and customs for a European audience, blending empirical detail with the biases of his time. Though often critical of indigenous cultures and aligned with imperial interests, his chronicles preserve invaluable information on the early decades of Spanish conquest and colonization, making him a key figure for understanding both the history of the Americas and the evolution of early modern ethnography and natural history.

He spent his later years refining his histories and defending their accuracy against critics, dying in Valladolid in 1557. Oviedo’s legacy is complex: he was at once a product of Spanish imperial power and a meticulous observer whose descriptions—sometimes the earliest written records—remain indispensable to historians of the early modern Atlantic world.
