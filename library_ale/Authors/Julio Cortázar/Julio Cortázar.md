## Biography
Julio Cortázar (1914–1984) was an Argentine writer, translator, and intellectual who became one of the central figures of the Latin American literary Boom. Born in Brussels to Argentine parents and raised mainly in Buenos Aires, he worked as a teacher and later as a translator for UNESCO, eventually settling in Paris in the early 1950s. His experience of exile, his deep engagement with European and Latin American culture, and his passion for jazz, surrealism, and experimental forms shaped a body of work that challenged conventional narrative and redefined the possibilities of the short story and the novel.

Cortázar’s fiction is marked by formal innovation, playful yet rigorous structures, and a constant blurring of the boundaries between the fantastic and the everyday. Collections such as “Bestiario” and “Final del juego,” and works like “El perseguidor” — a jazz-inflected exploration of genius, time, and self-destruction inspired by the figure of Charlie Parker — established his reputation as a master of the modern short story. His novel “Rayuela” (“Hopscotch”) became a landmark of experimental fiction, inviting readers to navigate its chapters in multiple orders and turning reading itself into a game.

Beyond his literary achievements, Cortázar was a committed public intellectual who supported leftist causes in Latin America, notably the Cuban Revolution and the Sandinista movement in Nicaragua. He cultivated friendships with fellow writers and artists, and his personal life, including his partnerships and friendships in Paris and Latin America, fed the emotional undercurrents of his work without overshadowing its intellectual ambition. By the time of his death in Paris in 1984, Cortázar had secured a lasting place in world literature as a writer who fused political commitment, emotional intensity, and radical experimentation in language and form.
