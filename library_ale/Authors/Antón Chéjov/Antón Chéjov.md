## Biography
Anton Chekhov (1860–1904) was a Russian playwright, short-story writer, and practicing physician whose work transformed modern literature and drama. Born in Taganrog to a lower-middle-class family with a harsh, religious father and a long-suffering, artistic mother, he supported his parents and siblings through his medical studies by writing humorous sketches and short pieces for newspapers and magazines. This double life as a doctor and writer, and his keen, clinical observation of people, shaped his unsentimental yet deeply humane view of everyday life.

Chekhov’s mature short stories and novellas, later collected in many volumes including selections like “Cinco novelas cortas” in translation, broke with conventional plotting in favor of subtle, psychologically rich portraits of ordinary people facing disappointment, moral compromise, and fleeting moments of grace. Parallel to his prose, he revolutionized theater with plays such as “The Seagull,” “Uncle Vanya,” “Three Sisters,” and “The Cherry Orchard,” written in close collaboration with directors and actors who emphasized quiet, implied action over melodrama. His long relationship with the actress Olga Knipper, whom he married late in life, connected him closely to the Moscow Art Theatre.

Despite chronic ill health and advancing tuberculosis, Chekhov undertook demanding trips, including a famous journey to the penal colony on Sakhalin Island, where he investigated and documented the harsh conditions of prisoners and exiles. He died in 1904 in Badenweiler, Germany, but his influence on fiction and drama—through his understated style, focus on subtext, and compassionate attention to ordinary lives—has remained central to world literature.
