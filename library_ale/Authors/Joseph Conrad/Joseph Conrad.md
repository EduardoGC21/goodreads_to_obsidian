## Biography
Joseph Conrad (1857–1924) was a Polish-born British novelist whose life as a seaman and émigré deeply shaped his fiction. Born Józef Teodor Konrad Korzeniowski in Berdychiv to Polish parents under Russian rule, he was orphaned young and raised by relatives in partitioned Poland. As a teenager he left for France and then joined the British merchant navy, spending nearly twenty years at sea and eventually becoming a British citizen; this experience provided the raw material, settings, and moral dilemmas that animate much of his work.

Conrad is best known for psychologically and morally complex novels and tales that explore imperialism, isolation, loyalty, and the ambiguity of human motives. Works such as "Heart of Darkness"—in which the journey into the African interior doubles as a journey into the darkest regions of the human psyche—made him a key precursor to literary modernism and an enduring critic of European colonialism. Writing in his third language, English, he developed a dense, allusive style and intricate narrative structures that influenced later writers including T.S. Eliot, William Faulkner, and Graham Greene.

Though he often struggled financially and was not always widely read in his own time, Conrad gradually gained recognition and spent his later years in England as an established man of letters. He married Jessie George and had two sons, and maintained friendships and correspondence with other prominent writers and intellectuals. By the time of his death in 1924, his reputation as one of the major English-language novelists of the early twentieth century was secure, and his work has since remained central to debates about modernism, empire, and moral responsibility.
