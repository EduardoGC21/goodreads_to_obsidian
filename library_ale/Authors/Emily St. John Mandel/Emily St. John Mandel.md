## Biography
Emily St. John Mandel (born 1979) is a Canadian novelist and essayist whose work bridges literary fiction, speculative narratives, and crime-inflected storytelling. Raised on Denman Island off the coast of British Columbia in a homeschooling environment that encouraged daily writing, she later studied contemporary dance at The School of Toronto Dance Theatre and spent time in Toronto and Montreal before settling in New York City. Her early working life included administrative roles and collaborations with independent choreographers, experiences that informed her interest in work, performance, and the hidden structures of modern life.

Mandel’s novels often explore interconnected lives, the aftermath of catastrophe, and the moral ambiguities of global capitalism, themes that come to the fore in books such as “Station Eleven,” “The Glass Hotel,” and “Sea of Tranquility.” “Station Eleven” brought her international recognition, earning major literary awards and a television adaptation, while “Sea of Tranquility” continued her interest in nonlinear timelines and the echoes between pandemics, art, and memory. She frequently reuses characters and settings across otherwise self-contained novels, creating a loose network of overlapping stories that reward attentive readers.

Beyond her fiction, Mandel has written essays and criticism, notably as a staff writer for the literary site The Millions, where she has examined publishing trends and the representation of women in contemporary book marketing. She has lived for many years in New York City (and later also Los Angeles), balancing an international literary career with family life, and is regarded as one of the most influential contemporary Canadian-born novelists working in English.
