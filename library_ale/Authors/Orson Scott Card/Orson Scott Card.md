## Biography
Orson Scott Card (1951–) is an American author best known for his science fiction and fantasy, particularly the Ender series that began with "Ender’s Game." Born in Richland, Washington, he grew up primarily in Utah and other parts of the western United States in a Latter-day Saint (Mormon) family, an upbringing that has strongly influenced the ethical, religious, and community themes in his work. Before becoming a full-time writer, he studied at Brigham Young University and the University of Utah, worked in theater, and wrote scripts and plays, experiences that helped shape his focus on dialogue, character interiority, and moral conflict.

Card’s breakthrough came with "Ender’s Game" and its sequels, including the core Ender Quartet later collected in box sets alongside related works such as "Ender in Exile." These novels combined military science fiction with psychological and ethical questions about leadership, empathy, and the cost of warfare, earning him major awards and a lasting reputation within the genre. Beyond Ender’s story, he has written extensively in fantasy, contemporary fiction, and religious commentary, often exploring family relationships, responsibility, and the tensions between personal conscience and institutional authority. Though some of his public views have been controversial, his influence on late 20th- and early 21st-century science fiction—especially in portraying gifted children, intercultural understanding, and the moral ambiguities of power—has been substantial.
