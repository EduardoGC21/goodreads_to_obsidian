## Biography
Marcus Aurelius (121–180) was a Roman emperor and Stoic philosopher whose life combined military leadership, political responsibility, and inner spiritual discipline. Born in Rome into a prominent aristocratic family, he was adopted by the emperor Antoninus Pius as heir, which placed him at the center of imperial power from a young age. As emperor, he ruled during a time of frontier wars, plague, and internal tensions, spending much of his reign on military campaigns along the Danube. Despite the burdens of rule, he sought to live according to Stoic virtues—reason, justice, self-control, and acceptance of fate—and to embody the ideal of a philosopher-king.

His private reflections, later known as "Meditations" (originally written in Greek and known in Spanish as "Meditaciones"), reveal a man wrestling with duty, mortality, and the moral use of power. These writings were not intended for publication, but they became one of the most influential texts in the history of philosophy, shaping later understandings of Stoicism and personal ethics. Marcus Aurelius’s legacy rests on the rare combination of supreme political authority with a serious, introspective philosophical life, as well as on his attempt to maintain compassion and integrity while navigating war, illness, and family challenges within the complex world of the Roman imperial court.
