## Biography
Philip K. Dick (1928–1982) was an American writer whose prolific body of work reshaped science fiction through its exploration of reality, identity, and perception. Born in Chicago and raised mainly in California by a single mother after his parents’ divorce, he led a financially precarious and emotionally turbulent life that deeply informed his fiction. Recurrent themes of paranoia, altered states of consciousness, and unstable realities reflected his own struggles with mental health, drug use, and intense religious or visionary experiences he reported in the 1970s.

Although he struggled for years on the margins of the pulp science-fiction market, Dick gradually became one of the genre’s most influential and philosophically ambitious voices. His novels and stories often questioned the nature of authenticity and humanity, famously in works like "Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?" which interrogates empathy and personhood, and "The Man in the High Castle," which imagines an alternate history to probe power, fascism, and moral ambiguity. Frequently at odds with publishers, the government he believed might be surveilling him, and even his own perceptions, Dick nevertheless built close relationships within the California literary and counterculture scenes, and his work gained a devoted readership.

Posthumously, Dick’s reputation expanded dramatically as film and television adaptations brought his ideas to global audiences. Though he never enjoyed great wealth or mainstream acclaim in his lifetime, he is now regarded as one of the central figures of modern speculative fiction, influencing not only writers but also filmmakers, philosophers, and technologists who draw on his anxious, visionary portraits of a world where the boundaries between human and machine, real and unreal, are always in doubt.
