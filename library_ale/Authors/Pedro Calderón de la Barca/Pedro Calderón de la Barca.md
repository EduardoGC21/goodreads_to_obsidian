## Biography
Pedro Calderón de la Barca (1600–1681) was a Spanish playwright, poet, and priest, and one of the central figures of the Spanish Golden Age of literature. Born in Madrid to a family connected with the court, he received a humanist and Jesuit education and briefly studied law at the universities of Alcalá and Salamanca before devoting himself to letters. His early success in theatrical competitions and at the royal court quickly established him as the leading dramatist to succeed Lope de Vega.

Calderón wrote extensively for the public theaters of Madrid and for court festivals, cultivating both secular and religious drama. His philosophical tragicomedy "La vida es sueño" (Life Is a Dream) is widely regarded as his masterpiece, reflecting his interest in free will, honor, and the illusory nature of worldly existence. He also became the preeminent author of autos sacramentales—allegorical Eucharistic plays—exemplified by works such as "El gran teatro del mundo," which present human life as a divine drama of salvation.

Over the course of his life, Calderón was closely linked to the Spanish monarchy, serving in the army in his youth, later being appointed a knight of the Order of Santiago, and eventually becoming honorary chaplain to King Philip IV. Ordained as a priest in middle age, he balanced his religious duties with continued writing for royal occasions. His work shaped Spanish baroque theater, influencing generations of dramatists across Europe, and he died in Madrid as a celebrated national author whose plays remain central to the canon of early modern drama.
