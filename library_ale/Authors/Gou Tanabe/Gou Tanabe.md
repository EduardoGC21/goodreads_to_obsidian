## Biography
Gou Tanabe is a Japanese manga artist born in Tokyo in 1975, known for his meticulous, atmospheric adaptations of classic literature, especially the weird fiction of H. P. Lovecraft. He debuted in 2001 with the story "Sunakichi," which won a Special Jury Prize in Kodansha's Afternoon Magazine Shiki Awards, and soon after gained further attention with his adaptation of Maxim Gorky's "Twenty-Six Men and a Girl." From early in his career he showed a strong interest in literary sources, reworking prose narratives into visually rich, psychologically intense comics.

Tanabe's encounter in the mid‑2000s with Lovecraft's Cthulhu Mythos reshaped his artistic direction. Struck by Lovecraftian protagonists who "lose all hope and appetite for life," he began a sustained project of adapting these stories into manga, including "The Outsider," "The Call of Cthulhu," "At the Mountains of Madness" and other major works, such as the two‑volume version of "At the Mountains of Madness" that helped solidify his international reputation. His careful pacing, heavy use of shadow, and adherence to the tone of the original texts have been praised by readers and fellow creators, and his books have been translated into numerous languages, introducing Lovecraft to new audiences through a distinctly Japanese visual style.

Although a relatively private figure, Tanabe has become an important bridge between Western horror literature and contemporary manga, showing how prose steeped in cosmic dread can be reimagined for comics without losing its philosophical weight. His work has influenced how other artists think about literary adaptation in manga, and he is widely regarded as one of the foremost interpreters of Lovecraft in any medium.
