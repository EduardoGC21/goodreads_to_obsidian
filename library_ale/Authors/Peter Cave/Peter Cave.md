## Biography
Peter Cave is a British philosopher, writer, and broadcaster known for presenting complex philosophical ideas through engaging puzzles, paradoxes, and thought experiments. Educated in philosophy at University College London and King’s College, Cambridge, he has held lectureships at institutions including University College London, the University of Khartoum in Sudan, and City University London, while also teaching for the Open University and New York University’s London program. His work spans academic and popular philosophy, combining logical rigor with an accessible, often humorous style that invites general readers to reflect on ethical, logical, and existential questions.

Beyond teaching, Cave has been active in public philosophy and humanist thought. He chairs the Humanist Philosophers’ Group in Britain, sits on the Council of the Royal Institute of Philosophy, and is associated with humanist organizations such as the British Humanist Association. His books, including the puzzle-based collections such as "Can a Robot be Human?: 33 Perplexing Philosophy Puzzles," along with works on paradoxes and humanism, have helped popularize philosophical reflection on topics like religion, morality, and the challenges of modern life.

Cave has also contributed extensively to magazines, journals, and radio. He has scripted and presented philosophy programs for the BBC, written both serious and humorous philosophical papers, and participated in public debates across Europe on religion, ethics, and socio‑political issues. His career reflects a commitment to bringing philosophy into everyday life, encouraging audiences to question assumptions and to engage critically with both timeless dilemmas and contemporary controversies.
