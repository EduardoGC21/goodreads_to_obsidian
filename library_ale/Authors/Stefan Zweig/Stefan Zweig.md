## Biography
Stefan Zweig (1881–1942) was an Austrian writer, biographer, and essayist, born into a wealthy Jewish family in Vienna. Educated in philosophy and literature, he quickly became part of the city’s vibrant cultural life, befriending leading intellectuals and artists. His elegant, psychologically acute prose and cosmopolitan outlook made him one of the most widely read authors in Europe between the two World Wars, with works ranging from novellas and biographies to essays and plays.

Zweig’s writing often explored themes of moral conflict, emotional extremity, and the fragility of human relationships, as seen in works like “Beware of Pity,” which examines guilt, compassion, and responsibility in the shadow of a collapsing society. A committed humanist and pacifist, he was deeply troubled by rising nationalism and antisemitism. After the Nazi takeover and the annexation of Austria, his books were banned, and he went into exile—living in the United Kingdom, the United States, and ultimately Brazil.

Cut off from his European readership and devastated by the destruction of the cultural world that had shaped him, Zweig fell into despair. In 1942, while living in Petrópolis, Brazil, he and his second wife Lotte Altmann died by joint suicide. His work, which captures both the brilliance and the breakdown of European civilization in the early 20th century, has since undergone a major revival, and he is now regarded as a key chronicler of the psychological and moral crises of his era.
