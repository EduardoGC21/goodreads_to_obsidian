## Biography
Jack London (1876–1916) was an American novelist, journalist, and social critic whose adventurous life at sea, in the Klondike, and on the American West Coast deeply shaped his fiction. Born John Griffith Chaney in San Francisco and raised in working-class poverty, he held a series of demanding jobs—oyster pirate, sailor, factory worker, and gold prospector—before turning to writing as a route out of hardship. These experiences furnished the raw material for his vivid depictions of survival, class struggle, and the harshness of nature.

London rose to international fame with works such as "The Call of the Wild" and "White Fang," which combined gripping adventure with sharp examinations of instinct, environment, and power. A committed socialist, he wrote extensively about labor, inequality, and the fate of individuals under capitalism, themes that are central to the semi-autobiographical novel "Martin Eden," where a self-taught writer’s rise and disillusionment reflect London’s own conflicted feelings about literary success and social status. Throughout his life he maintained complex personal relationships, including two marriages and numerous literary and political friendships, while struggling with health issues and heavy alcohol use. Despite his relatively short life, London became one of the most widely read authors of his era and a key figure in early 20th-century American literature.
