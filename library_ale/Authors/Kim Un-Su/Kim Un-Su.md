## Biography
Kim Un-su (also romanized as Un-su Kim) is a South Korean novelist born in 1972 in the port city of Busan. He studied Korean literature at Kyung Hee University, where he also completed graduate-level coursework, and initially aspired to be a poet before turning to fiction. After working various jobs and completing his mandatory military service, he debuted in 2002 with short fiction and gained national recognition in 2003 by winning the Dong-A Ilbo New Writer’s Award for his novella “Breaking Up with Friday.”

Kim’s first novel, The Cabinet (2006), established him as a major literary voice when it won the 12th Munhakdongne Novel Award, one of South Korea’s most prestigious literary prizes, and helped launch a career marked by formally inventive and often surreal narratives. He went on to publish the thriller The Plotters (2010), the story collection Jab (2013), and the Busan-set novel Hot Blood (2016), the latter earning the Hahn Moo-Sook Literary Prize and later being adapted into a feature film. His work frequently portrays lonely or marginal characters—assassins, small-time gangsters, office workers—set against contemporary Korean society, blending dark humor, violence, and philosophical reflection.

From the 2010s onward Kim achieved significant international prominence, especially after The Plotters was translated into French and English and was shortlisted for the Grand Prix de la Littérature Policière and selected by Time magazine as one of the 100 Best Mystery and Thriller Books of All Time. The Cabinet, first published in Korean in 2006, followed into English translation and broadened his readership further. With his books now translated into numerous languages and a television adaptation of The Plotters in development, Kim is regarded as one of the most globally visible Korean genre-bending novelists of his generation.
