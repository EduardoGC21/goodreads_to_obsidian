## Biography
Voltaire (François-Marie Arouet, 1694–1778) was a French writer, philosopher, and one of the central figures of the Enlightenment. Born in Paris to a middle-class family, he was educated by Jesuits but quickly developed a sharp, skeptical attitude toward religious dogma and absolute authority. His satirical wit and criticism of the French monarchy and the Catholic Church led to imprisonment in the Bastille and periods of exile, experiences that deepened his commitment to civil liberties, freedom of thought, and judicial reform. A formative stay in England exposed him to constitutional monarchy, religious toleration, and the scientific culture of Newton and Locke, which strongly influenced his later work.

Voltaire became famous across Europe for his prolific output—plays, poems, philosophical tales, histories, and polemical pamphlets—that used irony and narrative to challenge fanaticism and injustice. Works such as his philosophical tales, including those collected in volumes like “Cuentos completos en prosa y verso” or stories such as “Cándido / Micromegas / Zadig,” combined imaginative fiction with pointed critiques of war, religious persecution, and irrational optimism. He maintained important intellectual and personal relationships, notably with the mathematician and physicist Émilie du Châtelet and with Frederick the Great of Prussia, whose court he briefly joined. Spending his later years at Ferney near the Swiss border, Voltaire became a symbol of the fight against intolerance, famously intervening in legal cases to defend victims of religious injustice, and his legacy endures as that of a foundational advocate for reason, individual rights, and secular governance.
