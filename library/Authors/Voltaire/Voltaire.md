## Biography
Voltaire (born François-Marie Arouet, 1694–1778) was a French writer, philosopher, and polemicist who became one of the central figures of the Enlightenment. Raised and educated in Paris, he quickly gained fame for his sharp wit and his attacks on religious intolerance, arbitrary power, and censorship. His biting satires and outspoken views led to repeated clashes with authorities, including imprisonment in the Bastille and periods of exile, experiences that shaped his lifelong commitment to civil liberties and freedom of thought.

After a formative exile in England, where he studied British constitutionalism, science, and religious pluralism, Voltaire incorporated these influences into his own work, helping to popularize Newtonian science and liberal political ideas on the Continent. He maintained an important intellectual and romantic partnership with the mathematician and physicist Émilie du Châtelet, with whom he lived and worked for many years. Later in life, he settled in Ferney near the Swiss border, turning it into a hub for European intellectuals while using his wealth and reputation to support victims of injustice and religious persecution.

Voltaire’s legacy rests on a vast and varied body of work—plays, poems, essays, historical studies, philosophical tales, and countless letters—in which he used humor, irony, and narrative to challenge dogma and advocate for reason and tolerance. Short philosophical fictions such as "Candide" and other tales, including those collected alongside "Micromegas" and "Zadig", exemplify his method of using imaginative stories to question optimism, fanaticism, and unexamined authority. His motto-like call to “écrasez l’infâme” (crush the infamous thing) captured his determination to combat superstition and oppression, and his influence on modern conceptions of free speech, religious tolerance, and human rights remains profound.
