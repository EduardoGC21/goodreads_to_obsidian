## Biography
Alejandro Ortiz Rescaniere (often cited as José Alejandro Ortiz Rescaniere) was a Peruvian anthropologist and scholar whose work focused on Andean and Amazonian indigenous cultures, particularly their mythologies, oral traditions, and conceptions of the person and community. Trained in both anthropology and linguistics, he combined structural, historical, and ethnographic approaches to study Quechua- and Aymara-speaking peoples and broader Amerindian thought, producing influential analyses of myth, ritual, and indigenous religious imagination.

Over several decades as a professor of anthropology at the Pontificia Universidad Católica del Perú, Ortiz Rescaniere helped shape multiple generations of Peruvian anthropologists. He was a founding figure of the journal "Anthropologica" and directed the university’s master’s program in anthropology, while also conducting extensive fieldwork that yielded major collections of Andean oral tradition and music. Elected an active member of the Société des Américanistes in 1967, he gained international recognition through works such as "Set Theory and Linguistics," "De Adaneva a Inkarrí," "El quechua y el aymara," and his editorial role in volumes like "Mitologías amerindias," which synthesized and interpreted Amerindian mythic corpora across the continent.

Ortiz Rescaniere maintained important intellectual and personal ties with figures such as the writer José María Arguedas, about whom he later published reflective studies on friendship and literary vision. His scholarship is widely regarded as a key bridge between formal linguistic and anthropological theory and the lived experiences, narratives, and religious worlds of indigenous communities in Peru and Latin America.
