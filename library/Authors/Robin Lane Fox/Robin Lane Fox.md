## Biography
Robin James Lane Fox (born 1946) is an English classicist and ancient historian whose work has helped shape modern understanding of the Greek and Roman worlds. Educated at Eton College and Magdalen College, Oxford, he rose quickly through the academic ranks, becoming a fellow at Magdalen in 1970 and later a long‑standing fellow and tutor in ancient history at New College, Oxford. Over decades of teaching Greek and Latin literature and ancient history, he influenced generations of students while also engaging wider publics through lucid, often provocative writing on antiquity, early Christianity, and the Bible.

Lane Fox first came to international prominence with his 1973 study of Alexander the Great, which won major literary prizes and established him as a leading authority on the Macedonian conqueror. He has since produced a series of widely read books on the ancient world, including broad surveys such as "The Classical World: An Epic History from Homer to Hadrian" and interpretive works on Homer and the Iliad, as well as studies of paganism and early Christianity. His expertise has extended beyond print: he served as historical adviser and even cavalry extra for Oliver Stone’s film "Alexander," and he wrote and presented television documentaries on Greek myth and history.

Alongside his academic career, Lane Fox has maintained a parallel public role as gardening correspondent for the Financial Times since 1970, an unusual combination that reflects his interest in both classical landscapes and practical horticulture. He is the father of entrepreneur and life peer Martha Lane Fox and of Henry Lane Fox, linking his scholarly life to contemporary business and public service. Celebrated for his erudition, independence of mind, and ability to connect ancient texts—from Homer’s epics to early Christian writings—to modern concerns, he remains one of the most distinctive voices in the study of classical antiquity.
