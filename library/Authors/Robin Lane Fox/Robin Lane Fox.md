## Biography
Robin Lane Fox (born 1946) is a British historian and classicist best known for his influential work on the ancient Greek and Roman worlds. Educated at Eton and New College, Oxford, he became a longstanding fellow of New College and later Reader in Ancient History at Oxford University, shaping generations of students with his teaching on Greek history, classical literature, and early Christianity. His scholarship combines philological rigor with vivid narrative, making complex ancient sources accessible to a wide audience.

Lane Fox first gained major recognition with his biography of Alexander the Great, which established him as a leading authority on Macedonian and Greek history and later informed his role as historical adviser for Oliver Stone’s film "Alexander," in which he famously rode as a cavalryman in the battle scenes. He has continued to interpret the ancient world for both scholars and general readers through works such as "The Classical World: An Epic History from Homer to Hadrian" and his studies of Homer, where he explores how epic poetry reflects the societies that produced it. Alongside his academic work, he is also known as a gardening writer and long‑time gardening columnist, an unusual combination that underscores the breadth of his interests.

Throughout his career, Lane Fox has been noted for his independence of mind, sometimes challenging prevailing scholarly orthodoxies, and for his ability to relate the ancient past to enduring questions about leadership, belief, and cultural identity. His writings and public lectures have made him a prominent public intellectual in the field of classics, and his work continues to influence how both specialists and lay readers understand figures like Alexander the Great and the world of Homeric epic.
