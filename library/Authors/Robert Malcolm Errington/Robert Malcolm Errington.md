## Biography
Robert Malcolm Errington (born 5 July 1939 in Howdon-on-Tyne, England) is a British ancient historian and epigrapher whose work has significantly shaped modern understanding of the Greek and Roman worlds. Educated at Durham University, where he studied Classics from 1958 to 1963 and earned a first-class BA and later a PhD in Ancient History in 1966, he went on to teach at Queen’s University Belfast before moving to the Philipps-Universität Marburg in Germany, where he served as Professor of Ancient History until his retirement. His long career in Marburg placed him at the center of German and international scholarship on the ancient Mediterranean, and he became a full member of the German Archaeological Institute.

Errington’s research ranges widely across the political and institutional history of ancient Greece and Macedonia, the Hellenistic monarchies, Roman expansion, Roman–Greek relations, Roman legal and administrative history, and the Christian late antique world. He is known for studies that emphasize state structures and constitutional frameworks rather than purely biographical or military narratives, an approach visible in works such as his histories of Macedonia and of the Hellenistic world, including “A History of the Hellenistic World: 323–30 BC.” His leadership of the Inscriptiones Graecae project from 2001 to 2007 reflects his deep engagement with epigraphy and documentary sources, and he has supervised and influenced a generation of scholars in ancient history and late antiquity through his teaching and mentorship.

Over several decades, Errington has contributed to international scholarly networks, including visiting memberships at institutions such as the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, and has published widely in both English and German. His work is frequently cited for its clear organization, close reading of inscriptions, and careful reconstruction of the interplay between cities, kings, and imperial powers. Through his books, articles, and editorial work on Greek inscriptions and interstate treaties, he remains an important figure in the study of how Greek and Roman political structures evolved from the classical polis world to the imperial systems of Rome.
