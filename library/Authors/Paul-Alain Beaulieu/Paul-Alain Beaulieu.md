## Biography
Paul-Alain Beaulieu is a Canadian Assyriologist whose work has significantly shaped modern understanding of ancient Mesopotamia, especially the political, religious, and intellectual history of Babylonia in the first millennium BC. Born and raised in Quebec, he first trained in law and history at the Université de Montréal before turning fully to the study of the ancient Near East. He went on to complete his MA and PhD in Assyriology at Yale University, where he worked closely with major figures in the field and gained extensive experience with the Yale Babylonian Collection’s cuneiform tablets.

After his doctorate, Beaulieu held research and teaching appointments at Yale, then joined the faculty of Harvard University, and later the University of Notre Dame, before moving in 2006 to the University of Toronto. At Toronto he became Professor of Assyriology and later Chair and Graduate Chair of the Department of Near and Middle Eastern Civilizations, mentoring generations of students in Akkadian language, Babylonian history, and the broader intellectual world of Mesopotamia. His election as a Fellow of the Royal Society of Canada in 2020 reflects his standing as a leading scholar in the humanities.

Beaulieu’s research combines close reading of cuneiform archives with a broad, historically nuanced approach. He has written influential monographs on Nabonidus and Neo-Babylonian religion, as well as the synthetic volume "A History of Babylon, 2200 BC – AD 75," which presents Babylon’s long history to both specialists and non-specialists. His work on temple archives from cities such as Uruk has illuminated the workings of Babylonian cult, economy, and scholarship, making him a central figure in the modern reconstruction of Mesopotamian civilization.
