## Biography
Howard Phillips Lovecraft (1890–1937) was an American writer of weird and cosmic horror whose work profoundly reshaped 20th‑century speculative fiction. Born in Providence, Rhode Island, he experienced a difficult childhood marked by the early deaths and mental illnesses of his parents, fragile health, and financial instability. Despite these hardships, he became a voracious self‑taught reader in science, history, and literature, which deeply informed the archaic style, elaborate mythology, and cosmic pessimism of his later fiction.

Lovecraft is best known for developing what is now called the Cthulhu Mythos, a shared universe populated by ancient, indifferent cosmic entities that render humanity insignificant—reflected in stories like “The Call of Cthulhu.” Collections such as “Necronomicon: The Best Weird Tales” and “Eldritch Tales: A Miscellany of the Macabre” bring together many of these works and highlight his influence on themes of cosmic dread, forbidden knowledge, and unstable reality. Although he lived in relative obscurity and poverty, publishing mostly in pulp magazines like Weird Tales, he maintained an extensive correspondence with other writers, mentoring and collaborating with figures such as Robert E. Howard and August Derleth, who helped preserve and expand his mythos after his death.

Personally, Lovecraft held deeply troubling racist views, which were explicit in his letters and sometimes in his fiction, and these have become an important part of modern reassessment of his legacy. Nonetheless, his imaginative world‑building, creation of a modern shared mythos, and blending of horror with science and philosophy have made him a foundational figure in horror and weird fiction, inspiring generations of writers, filmmakers, game designers, and artists long after his death.
