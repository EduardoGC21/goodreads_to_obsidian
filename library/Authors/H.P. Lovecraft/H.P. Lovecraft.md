## Biography
Howard Phillips Lovecraft (1890–1937) was an American writer of weird fiction whose work profoundly shaped modern horror and cosmic fiction. Born in Providence, Rhode Island, he spent most of his life in New England, a region that heavily influenced his settings and atmosphere. Lovecraft’s childhood was marked by instability and illness: his father suffered a mental breakdown and was institutionalized, and Lovecraft himself struggled with fragile health, anxiety, and social isolation. Raised primarily by his mother and extended family in a declining but proud New England household, he developed an early fascination with astronomy, history, and classical literature, all of which would inform the tone and detail of his later writing.

Lovecraft’s literary significance rests on his creation of a distinctive form of cosmic horror, in which human beings confront vast, indifferent, and often incomprehensible forces. Stories such as "The Call of Cthulhu" and other tales later gathered in collections like "Necronomicon" and "Eldritch Tales" introduced a shared mythos of ancient entities, forbidden texts, and decaying towns, influencing generations of writers, filmmakers, and game designers. Despite his creativity, Lovecraft lived in relative poverty, wrote much of his work for pulp magazines, and relied on extensive correspondence with a circle of fellow writers for intellectual companionship. His personal views included pronounced racism and xenophobia, which have become a serious and necessary part of modern reassessments of his legacy.

In his later years, Lovecraft faced worsening financial hardship and health problems, and he remained largely unknown outside pulp circles during his lifetime. He died in Providence in 1937, likely unaware of the lasting impact his ideas would have on literature and popular culture. After his death, friends and admirers preserved and promoted his work, establishing the so‑called Cthulhu Mythos and ensuring that his influence extended far beyond the modest circumstances of his life. Today, Lovecraft is recognized both for his imaginative power and for the troubling aspects of his worldview, making him a central, if contentious, figure in the history of speculative fiction.
