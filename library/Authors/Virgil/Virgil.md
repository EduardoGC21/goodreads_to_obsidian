## Biography
Virgil (Publius Vergilius Maro, 70–19 BC) was a Roman poet whose works became foundational texts of Latin literature and later Western culture. Born near Mantua in northern Italy to a modestly prosperous family, he received a thorough education in rhetoric, philosophy, and poetry in Cremona, Milan, and Rome. Although trained for a legal career, his reserved temperament and ill health led him away from public pleading and into a quieter life devoted to study and writing, often under the patronage of cultivated Roman elites.

His earliest major works, the pastoral "Eclogues" and the didactic "Georgics" (closely related in theme to the agricultural focus of works like the "Geórgicas"), explore rural life, political upheaval, and humanity’s relationship with nature, reflecting the turbulent transition from the Roman Republic to the Principate. These poems attracted the support of influential figures, most notably Maecenas, a key adviser to Octavian (the future Augustus), which secured Virgil’s position within the literary and political circles of his time.

Virgil’s epic "Aeneid"—the work for which he is best known—recast Rome’s origins through the legendary journey of Aeneas, offering both a national myth and a meditation on duty, suffering, empire, and divine will. Left technically unfinished at his death in 19 BC, it was nonetheless preserved at Augustus’s insistence and quickly became a central text for Roman education, ethics, and identity. In later centuries, Virgil was revered as a master poet, moral guide, and even semi-prophetic figure, influencing authors from Dante to modern poets and securing his reputation as one of antiquity’s most significant and enduring literary voices.
