## Biography
Virgil (70–19 BC), born Publius Vergilius Maro near Mantua in northern Italy, was the preeminent poetic voice of the late Roman Republic and the early Augustan age. Educated in rhetoric and philosophy, he gravitated toward poetry and became associated with literary circles that supported the new political order under Augustus. His early works, the "Eclogues" (or "Bucolics"), introduced refined pastoral themes into Latin literature, while the "Georgics" blended didactic farming lore with reflections on labor, nature, and Roman destiny, establishing him as a master craftsman of the Latin hexameter.

Virgil’s greatest work, the "Aeneid," composed in the final decade of his life, reshaped earlier Greek and Roman myth into a national epic that traced Rome’s origins to the Trojan hero Aeneas. Through this poem he provided the Augustan regime with a powerful cultural and ideological foundation, while simultaneously exploring questions of duty, suffering, empire, and personal sacrifice. Though he reportedly wished to have the unfinished "Aeneid" destroyed at his death, Augustus ordered it preserved, and it quickly became a core text of Roman education.

For centuries after his death, Virgil was revered not only as Rome’s finest poet but also as a quasi-prophetic moral authority: his verses were memorized in schools, consulted for guidance by means of bibliomancy (the "Sortes Vergilianae"), and later, in the Middle Ages, he was cast as Dante’s guide through the afterlife. His influence on Western literature, from epic structure to poetic language and political imagination, has been immense and enduring.
