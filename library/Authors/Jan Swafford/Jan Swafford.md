## Biography
Jan Swafford (born 1946 in Chattanooga, Tennessee) is an American composer, music historian, and biographer whose work has significantly shaped modern understanding of the classical canon. Trained at Harvard and the Yale School of Music, he studied composition with figures such as Earl Kim and Jacob Druckman before building a career that bridges creative work, scholarship, and public music education. His own music, often described as lyrically expressive and “New Romantic” in character, moves freely between tonality and atonality and draws inspiration from nature, world music traditions, jazz, and blues.

Alongside his compositional career, Swafford has become one of the leading biographers of major Western composers, writing substantial lives of Charles Ives, Johannes Brahms, Ludwig van Beethoven, and later Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart. His Beethoven biography, published in English as “Beethoven: Anguish and Triumph,” earned wide critical acclaim and reached the New York Times bestseller list, cementing his reputation for combining meticulous archival research with vivid narrative. Earlier, his Ives biography was shortlisted for the National Book Critics Circle Award and won the PEN/Winship prize, while his studies of Brahms and his “Vintage Guide to Classical Music” have become reference points for both scholars and general readers.

Swafford has taught composition, theory, and music history at institutions including the Boston Conservatory, Tufts University, and Boston University, and is a familiar voice to wider audiences through his program notes for leading orchestras, as well as commentary on NPR, the BBC, and in publications such as Slate. Over the decades he has balanced composing with writing and teaching, serving as an important mediator between the concert hall, the academy, and the general public, and helping contemporary listeners enter more deeply into the worlds of composers like Beethoven whose lives he has chronicled.
