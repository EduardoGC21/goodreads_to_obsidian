## Biography
Émile Zola (1840–1902) was a French novelist, journalist, and critic who became one of the central figures of literary naturalism and a powerful voice in the public life of the Third Republic. Born in Paris and raised partly in Aix-en-Provence after his father’s early death, Zola experienced financial hardship that shaped his sensitivity to class, poverty, and social injustice. After modest beginnings as a clerk and a journalist, he turned to fiction and conceived the vast Rougon-Macquart cycle, a 20-novel series exploring how heredity and environment shape individuals across generations in the Second Empire. Works like "Germinal" displayed his commitment to documenting working-class life with unflinching realism and helped cement his reputation as a bold and controversial observer of industrial society.

Zola’s influence extended beyond literature into politics and public morality. He formed close relationships within artistic and intellectual circles in Paris, championing Impressionist painters and engaging with debates over science, determinism, and social reform. His most famous intervention came during the Dreyfus Affair, when his open letter "J’Accuse…!" accused the French government and military of antisemitism and judicial corruption. This act of conscience led to his prosecution and temporary exile in England, but also transformed him into an international symbol of the engaged writer willing to risk his career and safety in defense of truth and justice. Zola died suddenly in 1902 from carbon monoxide poisoning—likely accidental, though some have suspected foul play—and remains a seminal figure whose life bridged literature, politics, and the struggle for a more just society.
