## Biography
Émile Zola (1840–1902) was a French novelist, journalist, and critic who became the leading figure of literary naturalism and one of the most influential writers of the late 19th century. Born in Paris to an Italian father and French mother, he grew up in relative poverty after his father’s early death, an experience that sharpened his sensitivity to social injustice. Zola conceived the monumental twenty-novel cycle Les Rougon-Macquart as a kind of laboratory of fiction, using a single extended family under the Second Empire to explore how heredity and environment shape human lives. Within that series, works such as "Germinal" powerfully depicted the harsh conditions of miners and the working class, cementing his reputation as a writer deeply engaged with the realities of industrial society.

Beyond literature, Zola played a decisive public role in French political life. In 1898, at great personal risk, he published his famous open letter "J’accuse…!" defending Captain Alfred Dreyfus, a Jewish army officer falsely convicted of treason, thereby helping to expose antisemitism and corruption at the highest levels of the French state. Forced into temporary exile in England during the Dreyfus Affair, Zola continued to write and to influence public opinion. His sudden death in 1902, likely caused by carbon monoxide poisoning, led to national mourning, and he was later interred in the Panthéon, symbolizing his status as both a major literary innovator and a moral voice of the French Republic.
