## Biography
Joshua Frydman is a scholar of ancient Japan and an assistant professor of Japanese in the Department of Modern Languages, Literatures and Linguistics at the University of Oklahoma in the United States. He completed his BA and subsequent graduate degrees in Japanese language and literature at Yale University, earning his PhD in 2014, and went on to postdoctoral and teaching appointments at institutions such as Harvard, Brown, and Brandeis before taking up his current position. His exact birth year is not widely public, but he is a contemporary academic whose career has been shaped within the international field of Japanese studies.

Frydman’s research centers on the origins of writing in Japan and the development of early Japanese literature, especially inscribed poetry from the seventh to tenth centuries CE, and he is recognized as an expert in ancient Japanese literature and material culture. He has authored the monograph "Inscribed Objects and the Development of Literature in Early Japan" and the widely read introduction to Japanese mythology "The Japanese Myths: A Guide to Gods, Heroes and Spirits," which has appeared in translation, including as "Los mitos japoneses: Una guía de sus dioses, héroes y espíritus." Through his teaching on premodern literature, classical Japanese, poetry, and popular culture (including anime), as well as his public-oriented work on myth, Frydman has helped bridge specialized scholarship and a broader readership, highlighting how Japan’s ancient myths and early texts continue to shape cultural imagination today.
