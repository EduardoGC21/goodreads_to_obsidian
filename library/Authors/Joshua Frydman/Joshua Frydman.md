## Biography
Joshua Frydman is a scholar of Japanese literature and culture whose work focuses on the deep historical roots of Japan’s myths and writing systems. An expert on ancient Japan, he serves as Assistant Professor of Japanese in the Department of Modern Languages, Literatures and Linguistics at the University of Oklahoma, where he teaches and writes about the intersections of language, religion, and storytelling in premodern East Asia. His research often traces how ideas, scripts, and stories moved across regions, and how those exchanges shaped the development of early Japanese society.

Frydman has written both for academic audiences and for general readers. His monograph on inscribed objects and the emergence of literature in early Japan explores how writing on artifacts and everyday materials helped give rise to new forms of narrative and cultural memory. In more accessible works such as *The Japanese Myths: A Guide to Gods, Heroes and Spirits* (also available in Spanish as *Los mitos japoneses: Una guía de sus dioses, héroes y espíritus*), he presents Japan’s mythological traditions in ways that connect ancient texts to contemporary media, from religion and court culture to manga, anime, and video games. Through this combination of rigorous scholarship and clear public-facing writing, he has become a notable voice in explaining how Japanese myths continue to inform both Japan’s self-understanding and global perceptions of Japanese culture.
