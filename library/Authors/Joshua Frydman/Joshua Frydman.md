## Biography
Joshua Frydman is a scholar of Japanese literature and culture, specializing in the myths, religions, and literary traditions of ancient Japan. He serves as an Assistant Professor of Japanese in the Department of Modern Languages, Literatures and Linguistics at the University of Oklahoma, where his teaching and research focus on the development of writing systems in East Asia and the ways literary and material culture illuminate premodern societies. His academic work examines how texts and inscribed objects shape cultural memory, political authority, and religious practice across the Japanese archipelago.

Frydman is widely recognized for making complex aspects of Japanese mythology accessible to general readers. In books such as his guide to Japanese myths—published in Spanish as "Los mitos japoneses: Una guía de sus dioses, héroes y espíritus"—he not only retells foundational stories but also explains how they connect to Shinto, Buddhism, and regional folklore, and how those myths continue to influence modern media like manga, anime, and film. Alongside this more public-facing work, he is the author of specialized studies such as "Inscribed Objects and the Development of Literature in Early Japan," which explore how early Japanese texts and artifacts help us understand the formation of literature and identity in East Asia.

Through his dual role as a researcher and a communicator of Japanese culture, Frydman has contributed to deepening international understanding of Japan’s religious and mythological heritage. His work highlights the dynamic relationship between ancient narratives and contemporary culture, showing how myths are continually reinterpreted as they move across time, media, and global audiences.
