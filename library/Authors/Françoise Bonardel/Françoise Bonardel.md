## Biography
Françoise Bonardel (b. 1947) is a French philosopher and essayist known for her work at the crossroads of religious studies, esotericism, and contemporary European culture. Born in Joigny, she trained as a philosopher, becoming agrégée in 1975 and later earning a doctorate in letters and human sciences with a thesis on alchemical thought in modern culture. Her academic career was largely spent at the University Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne, where from 1990 to 2010 she taught philosophy of religion and contributed to shaping French debates on spirituality, symbolism, and cultural identity.

Bonardel’s research has focused on hermetism, alchemy, gnosis, and Buddhism, exploring how these ancient traditions illuminate modern psychological and cultural crises. Works such as “Philosophie de l’alchimie,” “La Voie hermétique” (also known in Spanish as “La vía hermética: Introducción a la filosofía de Hermes”), and her studies on Jung and gnosis examine “transmutation” of matter, psyche, and values as a way of rethinking Western modernity. She has been active in the Institut d’Études Bouddhiques, teaching on the acculturation of Buddhism in Europe and engaging in dialogue between Eastern spiritualities and Western philosophy.

Beyond the university, Bonardel has taken part in public intellectual life through lectures, essays, and participation in colloquia, notably on the crisis of European cultural identity and the spiritual stakes of contemporary culture. Her work situates esoteric and religious traditions within a broader reflection on Europe’s heritage, modern individualism, and the search for wisdom, making her a significant figure in late 20th- and early 21st-century French thought on religion and culture.
