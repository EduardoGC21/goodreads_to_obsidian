## Biography
Jorge Luis Borges (1899–1986) was an Argentine writer, poet, essayist, and librarian whose work reshaped 20th‑century literature. Born in Buenos Aires to a book‑loving family of mixed Argentine and European heritage, he grew up bilingual in Spanish and English, steeped in classical literature and philosophy. His early years in Palermo and later stays in Geneva and Spain exposed him to diverse intellectual currents, from symbolism to ultraísmo, which fed into his distinctive blend of metaphysics, detective logic, and fantastical invention.

Borges became internationally renowned for his short stories and essays—especially collections like "Ficciones" and "El Aleph"—which explore labyrinths, mirrors, infinite libraries, and the nature of time, authorship, and identity. He worked as a municipal librarian and later as director of the National Library of Argentina, a role he reached just as he was going blind, a biographical irony he often reflected on. Despite his failing eyesight, he continued to write and lecture, dictating many late works such as those gathered in volumes like "Siete noches" and collaborations with close friends, including Adolfo Bioy Casares. His personal life was marked by a long, intense relationship with his mother, Leonor Acevedo, who supported and accompanied him well into his old age, and by a circle of literary friendships that anchored his intellectual life.

Borges’s significance lies not in large novels but in concentrated, idea‑rich pieces that blurred the boundaries between fiction, essay, and philosophical parable. His inventive use of metafiction, unreliable texts within texts, and imaginary books influenced writers across Latin America, Europe, and North America, making him a key precursor to postmodern literature and an essential figure in the Latin American literary canon.
