## Biography
Jorge Luis Borges (1899–1986) was an Argentine writer, poet, and essayist whose innovative blend of philosophy, metaphysics, and fiction reshaped twentieth-century literature. Born in Buenos Aires to a bookish, bilingual family, he grew up surrounded by English and Spanish books, and his father’s library became his real childhood world. After spending part of his youth in Europe during World War I, he returned to Argentina in the 1920s and quickly became a central figure in Buenos Aires’s literary avant-garde, contributing essays, reviews, and poems that explored identity, time, and the shifting nature of reality.

Borges is best known for his short stories, especially those collected in “Ficciones” and “El Aleph,” where he created labyrinths of ideas involving infinite libraries, alternate worlds, and paradoxical time, often using the form of scholarly notes, invented sources, and imagined books. These works, along with his extensive essays and lectures, helped shape modern conceptions of metafiction and influenced writers across Latin America, Europe, and the United States. Although he gradually lost his sight and became completely blind by his mid-fifties, he continued to dictate stories, poems, and erudite lectures, many later gathered in volumes such as “Siete noches,” and he also collaborated closely with friends and fellow writers, turning his disability into a different way of seeing literature and memory.

Throughout his life Borges held positions at libraries and universities, notably serving as director of the National Library of Argentina, a role he regarded as both an honor and a metaphysical irony given his blindness. He traveled widely, forged important intellectual friendships, and became an international symbol of literary cosmopolitanism, drawing on sources from world religions, philosophy, and obscure encyclopedias as readily as from his native Buenos Aires. Though he never received the Nobel Prize, his work is often cited as among the most important of the twentieth century, and his explorations of dreams, mirrors, and infinite regress—echoed in collections like “Libro de sueños”—continue to shape how readers and writers think about fiction itself.
