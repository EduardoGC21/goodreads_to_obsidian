## Biography
Yuval Noah Harari (1976–) is an Israeli historian and public intellectual best known for his wide-ranging syntheses of human history and possible futures. Born in Kiryat Ata, Israel, he studied history at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and completed his doctorate at the University of Oxford, specializing in medieval and military history. He later joined the faculty of the Hebrew University, where he became known for his clear, integrative lectures on the history of humankind. Harari’s breakthrough came when his popular Hebrew lecture course evolved into the book "Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind," which reframed human history in terms of cognitive, agricultural, and scientific revolutions and reached a global audience.

Building on that success, Harari wrote "Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow," exploring how biotechnology, artificial intelligence, and data-driven systems might reshape human societies, and "21 Lessons for the 21st Century," which examines the political, technological, and existential challenges of contemporary life. His work is characterized by its ability to connect long-term historical patterns with current and future dilemmas, making him influential in debates about globalization, ethics, and the impact of technology. Harari is openly gay; his husband, Itzik Yahav, is also his personal manager, and Harari often credits this partnership and his disciplined meditation practice with helping him sustain his intense writing and speaking schedule.
