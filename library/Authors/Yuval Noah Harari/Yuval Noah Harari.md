## Biography
Yuval Noah Harari (born 1976) is an Israeli historian and public intellectual best known for his wide-ranging syntheses of human history and the future of humankind. Trained as a medieval military historian, he earned his PhD from the University of Oxford and later became a professor at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem. Over time, his work shifted from narrow academic topics to sweeping narratives about the evolution of Homo sapiens, the development of complex societies, and the forces shaping our technological future.

Harari gained international prominence with books such as "Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind" and "Homo Deus: A Brief History of Tomorrow", in which he combines history, philosophy, and science to explore how cognitive, agricultural, and scientific revolutions shaped human societies and may lead to new forms of social and biological engineering. His writing, interviews, and lectures have influenced public debates on topics like artificial intelligence, data privacy, nationalism, and global cooperation. Harari is also known for his ability to communicate complex ideas to broad audiences, and for his candid reflections on his own life, including his identity as a gay man and his long-term partnership with Itzik Yahav, who also serves as his manager.

Throughout his career, Harari has emphasized the power of stories, myths, and shared fictions in organizing human societies, arguing that modern institutions—from nations and corporations to human rights—depend on collective belief. He frequently engages with political leaders, technologists, and cultural figures, positioning himself as a kind of philosopher-historian of the 21st century who warns of the ethical and social challenges posed by rapid technological change, while urging more global, long-term thinking about humanity’s future.
