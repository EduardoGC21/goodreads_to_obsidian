## Biography
Steven H. Strogatz (1959– ) is an American mathematician and professor renowned for his work in dynamical systems and for making advanced mathematics accessible to broad audiences. Born in Torrington, Connecticut, he studied at Princeton University, the University of Cambridge, and Harvard University before joining the faculty at Cornell University, where he has spent much of his career in the Department of Mathematics. His research has explored synchronization in coupled oscillators, nonlinear dynamics, chaos, and complex networks, including the influential Watts–Strogatz model of “small-world” networks that helped shape modern network science.

Alongside his research contributions, Strogatz is widely celebrated as a gifted expositor of mathematics. He has written popular books and essays that explain deep ideas in calculus, dynamical systems, and everyday mathematics to non-specialists; “Infinite Powers: How Calculus Reveals the Secrets of the Universe” is one of his signature works in this vein, tracing how calculus underpins modern science and technology while also reflecting on its historical development. He has also written columns for major newspapers and produced widely viewed lectures and courses, earning awards for both teaching and communication.

Throughout his career, Strogatz has mentored students and collaborators across mathematics, physics, biology, and engineering, illustrating how mathematical thinking illuminates phenomena from fireflies flashing in unison to the spread of opinions in society. His combination of rigorous research, cross-disciplinary collaborations, and exceptional public outreach has made him a central figure in contemporary applied mathematics and in the broader project of sharing the power and beauty of mathematics with the public.
