## Biography
Steven H. Strogatz (born 1959) is an American mathematician and one of the leading figures in the study and public communication of dynamical systems and complex networks. Raised in Torrington, Connecticut, he studied mathematics at Princeton University, then continued as a Marshall Scholar at Trinity College, Cambridge, and completed a PhD in applied mathematics at Harvard on the dynamics of the human sleep–wake cycle. After postdoctoral work at Harvard and Boston University, he joined the faculty at MIT and later moved to Cornell University, where he became the Susan and Barton Winokur Distinguished Professor for the Public Understanding of Science and Mathematics.

Strogatz’s research ranges across applied mathematics, including mathematical biology, chaos, and complex network theory. He is especially known for foundational work on synchronization phenomena and for co-developing the Watts–Strogatz model of small‑world networks with Duncan J. Watts, a contribution that helped shape modern thinking about how real-world networks—from social ties to technological systems—organize and evolve. Over the years he has mentored students who themselves became influential researchers, and has collaborated widely with experimentalists to connect mathematical theory with observed phenomena such as firefly synchrony, crowd-induced bridge oscillations, and other collective behaviors in nature and society.

Parallel to his research, Strogatz has become a prominent public expositor of mathematics. Through textbooks like “Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos” and popular works such as “Sync,” “The Calculus of Friendship,” “The Joy of x,” and “Infinite Powers: How Calculus Reveals the Secrets of the Universe,” as well as New York Times columns and the podcast “The Joy of Why,” he has introduced broad audiences to the power and beauty of mathematical ideas. His ability to make advanced topics accessible has earned him major honors, including election to the National Academy of Sciences and multiple awards for science communication, and has positioned him as a central figure in contemporary efforts to connect mathematics with everyday life.
