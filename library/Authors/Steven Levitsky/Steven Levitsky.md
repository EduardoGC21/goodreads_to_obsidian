## Biography
Steven Robert Levitsky (born 1968) is an American political scientist whose work has reshaped the study of democracy, authoritarianism, and political parties, particularly in Latin America. Raised in Ithaca, New York, he studied political science at Stanford University (B.A., 1990) and earned his Ph.D. from the University of California, Berkeley (1999). After a visiting fellowship at the Kellogg Institute at the University of Notre Dame, he joined Harvard University, where he became David Rockefeller Professor of Latin American Studies and Professor of Government and later director of the David Rockefeller Center for Latin American Studies. His scholarship has focused on how democracies erode from within, the role of informal institutions, and the dynamics of party systems and competitive authoritarian regimes.

Levitsky is best known to general audiences as co-author, with Daniel Ziblatt, of "How Democracies Die," a widely translated best seller that draws on comparative historical cases to explain how elected leaders and mainstream parties can undermine democratic norms and institutions from the inside. Beyond that book, he has authored or edited numerous influential academic works, including studies of Argentine Peronism, competitive authoritarianism, and the durability of revolutionary regimes, making him a central figure in comparative politics. He has also written extensively for public venues such as major newspapers and magazines, bringing academic insights about democracy’s fragility and resilience into broader political debates.

Throughout his career, Levitsky has maintained close ties to Latin America, conducting fieldwork and engaging in public discussion, especially on Argentine and Peruvian politics. His personal and professional life reflect this regional focus: he is married to Peruvian journalist Liz Mineo, and his research and teaching have helped shape generations of students’ understanding of Latin American politics and global democratic backsliding. His work continues to influence scholars, policymakers, and citizens concerned with the health of democracy around the world.
