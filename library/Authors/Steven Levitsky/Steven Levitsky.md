## Biography
Steven Robert Levitsky (born 1968) is an American political scientist whose work has reshaped contemporary understanding of how democracies erode and how authoritarian regimes endure. Raised in Ithaca, New York, he became interested in Latin America as a student and went on to earn a B.A. from Stanford University and a Ph.D. in political science from the University of California, Berkeley. His early fieldwork in countries such as Argentina and Peru grounded his later theories about political parties, informal institutions, and hybrid regimes that blur the line between democracy and dictatorship.

Levitsky joined Harvard University in 2000 and is now David Rockefeller Professor of Latin American Studies and Professor of Government, as well as Director of the David Rockefeller Center for Latin American Studies. Internationally, he is recognized for his work on “competitive authoritarianism” and on the informal rules that shape political life, especially in Latin America. His co‑authored book "How Democracies Die" brought his comparative research to a broad public, using historical and global cases to illuminate contemporary threats to democratic norms in the United States and beyond, and helped frame public debates about democratic backsliding.

Beyond his books and academic articles, Levitsky has been an influential public intellectual, writing for major newspapers and magazines and advising civic and policy organizations concerned with the health of democracy. He has received multiple scholarly and public‑impact awards for his work and continues to collaborate with colleagues on the study of authoritarianism, democratic resilience, and the challenges facing multiracial democracy. He lives with his family in the Boston area and remains deeply engaged with political developments across the Americas.
