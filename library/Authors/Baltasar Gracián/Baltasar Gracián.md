## Biography
Baltasar Gracián (1601–1658) was a Spanish Jesuit priest, moral philosopher, and Baroque prose stylist whose reflections on prudence, power, and human conduct made him one of the most influential thinkers of Golden Age Spain. Born in Belmonte de Gracián in Aragon, he entered the Society of Jesus as a young man and spent much of his life teaching, preaching, and serving as a confessor while also cultivating a reputation as a sharp observer of courtly and social life. His religious vocation brought him into contact with both ecclesiastical authorities and educated elites, shaping a worldview in which spiritual concerns, political realism, and ethical self-discipline were tightly interwoven.

Gracián’s writings, often published under pseudonyms to avoid conflict with Jesuit superiors, explore the complexities of reputation, friendship, and power within a highly competitive society. Works such as "El Criticón" and his aphoristic collections present life as a perilous journey requiring discernment, restraint, and strategic insight, ideas that later resonated with European moralists and, centuries afterward, with modern readers interested in self-mastery and practical wisdom. Although his frank analyses of human weakness and institutional hypocrisy eventually led to tensions with his order and periods of censure, he remained within the Jesuit community until his death. Today, Gracián is remembered as a major figure in Spanish literature and a precursor to modern psychological and political realism, whose concise maxims and allegorical narratives continue to influence thinkers, writers, and students of ethics and strategy.
