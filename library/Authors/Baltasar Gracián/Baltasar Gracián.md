## Biography
Baltasar Gracián (1601–1658) was a Spanish Jesuit priest, writer, and moral philosopher, born in Belmonte de Gracián in the Kingdom of Aragon. Educated within Jesuit schools, he became known both as a preacher and as a teacher of theology, while cultivating a highly compressed, conceptist prose style that made him one of the most distinctive voices of Spain’s Baroque Golden Age. His works were often published under the name of friends or relatives to avoid trouble with his religious superiors, reflecting the tension between his intellectual independence and the strict discipline of the Society of Jesus.

Gracián’s significance lies in his exploration of prudence, disillusionment, and the art of living wisely in a complex and often hostile world. In works such as "El Criticón," an allegorical novel that meditates on human life from youth to old age, and in his maxims and handbooks of worldly wisdom, he offered readers a highly influential analysis of character, power, and moral strategy. His thought later shaped European moral and political reflection, influencing figures such as Schopenhauer and Nietzsche, and securing his reputation as one of the most penetrating psychological and ethical observers of early modern Europe.
