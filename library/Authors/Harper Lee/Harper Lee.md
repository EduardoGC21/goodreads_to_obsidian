## Biography
Harper Lee (1926–2016) was an American novelist best known for her landmark work "To Kill a Mockingbird," first published in 1960. Born Nelle Harper Lee in Monroeville, Alabama, she was the youngest of four children in a small-town lawyer’s family, an upbringing that deeply informed her depiction of the fictional Maycomb. Lee studied law at the University of Alabama but left before completing her degree, moving to New York City to pursue writing while supporting herself with various clerical and airline jobs.

Lee’s close friendships, including with writer Truman Capote, connected her to the literary and journalistic worlds, and she assisted Capote with research for his nonfiction book "In Cold Blood". "To Kill a Mockingbird" quickly became both a popular and critical success, winning the Pulitzer Prize and becoming a central text in discussions of race, justice, childhood, and moral courage in the United States. Despite her fame, Lee chose a largely private life, publishing little else and spending much of her later years back in Monroeville, where she remained a significant, if somewhat enigmatic, literary figure until her death in 2016.
