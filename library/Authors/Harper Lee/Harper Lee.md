## Biography
Harper Lee (1926–2016) was an American novelist best known for her seminal work *To Kill a Mockingbird*, first published in 1960. Born Nelle Harper Lee in Monroeville, Alabama, she grew up in a small Southern town that strongly influenced the fictional Maycomb of her novel. Lee was the youngest of four children in a family headed by a lawyer and state legislator, and she developed an early interest in literature and storytelling. She studied law at the University of Alabama but left without completing her degree, eventually moving to New York City to pursue writing while working various jobs to support herself.

In New York, Lee maintained close friendships, including with childhood friend Truman Capote, whom she assisted as a research partner on his nonfiction work *In Cold Blood*. The publication of *To Kill a Mockingbird* brought her immediate acclaim, earning the Pulitzer Prize for Fiction in 1961 and establishing her as a major voice on issues of race, justice, and moral conscience in the American South. Despite the novel’s success and its enduring influence on American culture and civil rights discourse, Lee largely withdrew from public life and published very little afterward, valuing her privacy and quiet life back in Monroeville. She remained an important, if elusive, literary figure until her death in 2016, and her work continues to shape conversations about empathy, childhood, and social inequality.
