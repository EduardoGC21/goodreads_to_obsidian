## Biography
Robert Graves (1895–1985) was an English poet, novelist, and classical scholar whose life spanned both World Wars and much of the turbulent twentieth century. Born in London to a literary and scholarly family, he served as an officer in the First World War, where severe wounds and the trauma of trench fighting profoundly shaped his outlook and early poetry. His war memoir "Goodbye to All That" became a defining account of the conflict and his disillusionment with the social and cultural order that produced it.

After the war Graves lived a peripatetic and often financially precarious life, teaching, translating, and writing across genres. His relationships—especially with the American poet and muse Laura Riding—were central to his emotional and creative world, influencing both his experimental poetry and his later mythological and critical works. He gained wide recognition with the historical novels "I, Claudius" and "Claudius the God", but he also devoted great energy to reinterpreting ancient religions and myth in books such as "The White Goddess" and his retellings of Greek and Hebrew myths, where he argued for the centrality of goddess worship and poetic inspiration in human culture.

Graves spent many years in Deià, on the island of Mallorca, which became both a refuge and a literary salon, attracting writers and artists from across Europe and America. There he continued to write poetry, criticism, and fiction into old age, becoming a revered yet sometimes controversial figure for his unorthodox theories and fiercely independent views on poetry, myth, and history. His work left a lasting mark on modern war poetry, historical fiction, and the popular understanding of classical and mythological traditions.
