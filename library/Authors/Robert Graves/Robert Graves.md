## Biography
Robert Graves (1895–1985) was an English poet, novelist, and scholar of myth and classical literature. Born in Wimbledon, London, he served as an officer in the British Army during World War I, experiences that shaped his early poetry and later his acclaimed memoir "Goodbye to All That." After the war he became a central figure in early 20th‑century English poetry, associated with writers such as Siegfried Sassoon, and developed a reputation for technical precision, emotional restraint, and skepticism toward patriotic mythmaking.

Over his long career Graves wrote historical novels, most famously "I, Claudius" and its sequel, as well as influential studies of myth and religion, including "The Greek Myths" and "The White Goddess," where he advanced provocative theories about goddess worship and poetic inspiration. His personal life was marked by intense literary and romantic relationships, particularly with the poet Laura Riding, with whom he collaborated closely before settling in Deià, Majorca, where he lived and worked for decades. Widely honored in his lifetime, Graves is remembered both for his war poetry and for reshaping popular and scholarly views of classical myth and ancient history.
