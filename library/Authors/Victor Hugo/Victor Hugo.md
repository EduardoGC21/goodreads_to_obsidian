## Biography
Victor Hugo (1802–1885) was a French novelist, poet, and dramatist who became one of the central figures of 19th‑century European literature and a leading voice of French Romanticism. Born in Besançon to a military father and a royalist mother, he grew up amid political upheaval, which sharpened his awareness of power, injustice, and the shifting fortunes of ordinary people. His early success as a poet and playwright made him a celebrated literary figure in Paris, and he quickly gained influence in cultural and political circles.

Hugo’s novels, including "Les Misérables" (known in Spanish as "Los miserables"), combined sweeping historical backdrops with intimate portrayals of moral struggle, poverty, and redemption, helping to shape modern ideas about social justice and the responsibilities of the state toward the most vulnerable. A committed republican, he served in politics, spoke out forcefully against the death penalty and authoritarian rule, and spent years in exile after opposing Napoleon III. During this exile, he produced some of his most powerful works, turning personal separation from France into a moral vantage point from which to critique tyranny.

Widely admired in his lifetime, Hugo’s relationships with other writers, artists, and political activists positioned him at the crossroads of literature and public life. His home became a gathering place for intellectuals, and he used his fame to advocate for humanitarian causes across Europe. By the time of his death in Paris, he was regarded not only as a master storyteller but as a national conscience, and his funeral drew enormous crowds, cementing his status as a symbol of the French Republic and a champion of the oppressed.
