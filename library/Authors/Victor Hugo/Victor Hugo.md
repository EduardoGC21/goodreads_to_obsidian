## Biography
Victor Hugo (1802–1885) was a French novelist, poet, and playwright who became one of the central figures of European Romanticism and a major public voice in 19th‑century France. Born in Besançon to a military officer who served Napoleon and a royalist mother, he grew up amid political turbulence, which deeply shaped his lifelong engagement with questions of power, justice, and social inequality. Early celebrated as a poet and dramatist, he quickly became a leading literary figure in Paris.

Hugo’s novels, especially "The Hunchback of Notre-Dame" and "Les Misérables", used vivid characters and dramatic plots to critique oppression, poverty, and the failings of institutions, helping to shift public conscience on issues like crime, punishment, and the plight of the poor. Politically active, he was elected to the National Assembly and opposed the rise of Napoleon III, which led to nearly two decades of exile in Belgium, Jersey, and Guernsey; this period produced some of his most powerful works and solidified his reputation as a moral and political conscience of his time.

In his personal life, Hugo married Adèle Foucher and had several children, enduring profound tragedy with the drowning of his daughter Léopoldine, which marked his poetry and inner life. He also maintained a long, complex relationship with actress Juliette Drouet, who was both companion and muse. Returning from exile after the fall of the Second Empire, he was revered as a national hero; his state funeral in 1885 drew massive crowds, and he was interred in the Panthéon, symbolizing his lasting significance to French literature, politics, and cultural identity.
