## Biography
Ray Bradbury (1920–2012) was an American writer whose imaginative storytelling bridged science fiction, fantasy, and literary fiction, helping to legitimize speculative literature in the mainstream. Born in Waukegan, Illinois, he grew up during the Great Depression, developing an early passion for reading and for the pulps, comics, and classic authors that shaped his vivid, poetic style. His family eventually moved to Los Angeles, where he educated himself largely in public libraries rather than formal universities, often describing libraries as his true alma mater.

Bradbury’s work is marked by a deep concern for humanity, memory, and the consequences of technology and authoritarianism. In "The Martian Chronicles," he used Mars and interplanetary colonization as a lens to explore colonization, loss, and the fragility of civilization, while "Fahrenheit 451" offered a powerful warning about censorship, conformity, and the erosion of critical thought. Beyond his novels and short stories, he wrote for film, television, and theatre, and his influence reached generations of readers, writers, and filmmakers.

Throughout his life Bradbury maintained close, supportive relationships with librarians, booksellers, and fellow authors, and he was a vocal advocate for literacy, creativity, and the preservation of public libraries. He received numerous honors, including a National Medal of Arts, and remained an active speaker and essayist into his later years. His legacy endures as that of a humanist visionary who used speculative worlds to illuminate the emotional and ethical contours of real life.
