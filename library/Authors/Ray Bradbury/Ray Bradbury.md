## Biography
Ray Bradbury (1920–2012) was an American author whose imaginative blend of science fiction, fantasy, and social commentary made him one of the most influential writers of the twentieth century. Born in Waukegan, Illinois, he grew up during the Great Depression, steeped in small-town Midwestern life, pulp magazines, and classic films, all of which shaped his sense of wonder and nostalgia. Largely self-educated, he spent countless hours in libraries, developing a disciplined writing routine that eventually produced hundreds of short stories, novels, plays, and screenplays.

Bradbury’s breakthrough came with works like "The Martian Chronicles," which used stories of human colonization of Mars to explore themes of colonialism, racism, and cultural fragility, and "Fahrenheit 451," a dystopian novel about censorship and the suppression of ideas that became a cornerstone of modern literature and a standard in schools. Beyond their speculative elements, his stories often focused on human relationships, memory, and the tensions between technology and humanity. Over his long career he received numerous honors, including a National Medal of Arts, and he maintained close relationships with editors, filmmakers, and fellow writers, helping bridge the worlds of genre and literary fiction. Bradbury died in Los Angeles, California, leaving a legacy that continues to shape contemporary science fiction and broader literary culture.
