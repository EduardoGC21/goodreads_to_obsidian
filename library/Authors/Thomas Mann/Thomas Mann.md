## Biography
Thomas Mann (1875–1955) was a German novelist, essayist, and public intellectual whose work probed the tensions between art and bourgeois life, individual desire and social duty, and spiritual illness and health. Born into a prosperous merchant family in Lübeck, he transformed his experience of the North German bourgeoisie into literature, first achieving fame with “Buddenbrooks,” a family saga that won him the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1929. Mann became known for combining psychological depth with ironic distance, often embedding philosophical and cultural reflection into carefully constructed, symbolically rich narratives.

His novel “The Magic Mountain” (“Der Zauberberg”), set in a tuberculosis sanatorium in the Swiss Alps, uses the enclosed world of the clinic and its international cast of characters to stage debates about time, illness, politics, and the fate of European civilization on the eve of World War I. Mann’s life was deeply marked by the upheavals of the 20th century: initially conservative during World War I, he later became a vocal critic of fascism. As an opponent of National Socialism, he went into exile—first in Switzerland, then the United States—where he delivered influential anti-Nazi radio addresses to Germany and continued to write major works, including the “Joseph and His Brothers” tetralogy and “Doctor Faustus.”

Mann’s personal life, including complex emotional attachments and a long marriage to Katia Mann, provided a rich if often veiled subtext to his portraits of conflicted artists and intellectuals. After the war, he lived between the U.S. and Switzerland, remaining a central moral and literary voice in postwar Europe. His exploration of decadence, morality, and the artist’s role in society has made him one of the defining figures of modern European literature, with a lasting influence on the novel as a form.
