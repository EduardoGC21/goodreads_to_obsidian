## Biography
Thomas Mann (1875–1955) was a German novelist, essayist, and public intellectual whose work probed the moral, psychological, and spiritual crises of modern European society. Born into a prosperous merchant family in Lübeck, he transformed his observations of bourgeois life into literature with his early masterpiece "Buddenbrooks," which established his reputation and later contributed to his receiving the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1929. Mann’s fiction often explores the tension between artistic or intellectual contemplation and the demands of practical, social life, as well as themes of illness, decadence, and the collapse of traditional values.

In novels such as "The Magic Mountain" (known in Spanish as "La montaña mágica"), Mann used richly symbolic settings and introspective characters to reflect on questions of time, death, and the fate of Europe on the eve of catastrophe. He married Katia Pringsheim, with whom he had six children, many of whom became notable writers or thinkers in their own right, and his family life frequently informed his portraits of complex, cultured households. An outspoken critic of Nazism, Mann went into exile in the 1930s, living in Switzerland and the United States, where he lectured widely and used his prestige to defend democratic and humanist values. He spent his final years back in Switzerland, dying in Zurich in 1955, and remains one of the central figures of twentieth-century literature.
