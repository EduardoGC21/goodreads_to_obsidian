## Biography
Herman Melville (1819–1891) was an American novelist, short story writer, and poet whose work explored the sea, obsession, morality, and the limits of human knowledge. Born in New York City to a once-prosperous merchant family, he experienced early financial instability after his father’s business failed and his father died, which forced him into various clerical and teaching jobs before he went to sea. His time as a sailor on whaling ships and naval vessels in the 1840s supplied the raw material and technical detail for many of his later writings.

Melville achieved early popularity with adventure tales drawn from his South Pacific experiences, but his reputation waned when he turned to more experimental and philosophically dense works. "Moby-Dick" (1851), now considered his masterpiece, initially puzzled readers with its blend of epic narrative, symbolic depth, and encyclopedic whaling lore. The novel’s later recognition in the 20th century transformed Melville from a relatively obscure writer who died in modest circumstances into a central figure of American literature, celebrated for his probing treatment of authority, individuality, race, and fate.

Throughout his life, Melville maintained significant literary and personal relationships, including a complex friendship with fellow writer Nathaniel Hawthorne, whose influence helped deepen the psychological and moral dimensions of his fiction. After his major novels failed commercially, Melville worked as a customs inspector in New York for decades while continuing to write poetry and shorter prose largely unnoticed. Only posthumously was his broader achievement recognized, positioning him as one of the most important and innovative voices in the 19th-century American canon.
