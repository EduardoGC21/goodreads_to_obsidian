## Biography
Herman Melville (1819–1891) was an American novelist, short story writer, poet, and essayist whose work explored the moral, psychological, and metaphysical depths of human experience. Born in New York City to a once-prosperous merchant family, he experienced financial instability and limited formal education after his father’s death, which pushed him into a series of jobs, including work as a schoolteacher and clerk. Seeking adventure and livelihood, Melville went to sea on merchant and whaling ships, experiences that profoundly shaped his imagination and later became the raw material for much of his fiction.

Drawing on these maritime years, Melville wrote a series of sea narratives, culminating in "Moby-Dick; or, The Whale," a novel that transformed a whaling voyage into a vast allegory about obsession, authority, faith, and the elusiveness of truth. Though underappreciated in his own lifetime, this and other works such as "Typee," "Benito Cereno," and "Bartleby, the Scrivener" later established him as a central figure in American literature. Melville married Elizabeth Shaw, with whom he had four children, and maintained friendships and intellectual exchanges with contemporaries such as Nathaniel Hawthorne, whose influence he deeply admired.

In later life Melville turned increasingly to poetry and more introspective prose, even as his readership dwindled and he returned to more routine work as a customs inspector in New York to support his family. He died in relative obscurity in 1891, but a revival of interest in the early twentieth century elevated him to the canon of great American writers. Today he is recognized for his ambitious narrative structures, psychological depth, and probing engagement with questions of democracy, empire, race, and the limits of knowledge.
