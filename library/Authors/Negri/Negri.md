## Biography
Antonio (Toni) Negri (1933–2023) was an Italian political philosopher and militant whose work profoundly influenced contemporary Marxist, autonomist, and post-structuralist thought. Born in Padua, he became a leading figure in the Italian extra‑parliamentary left during the 1960s and 1970s, associated with operaismo (workerism) and later autonomia, movements that sought to rethink class struggle in the context of advanced capitalism. He held academic posts, notably at the University of Padua, while also engaging in intense political organizing that brought him into conflict with the Italian state.

In 1979 Negri was arrested and accused, controversially and largely on circumstantial grounds, of involvement with the Red Brigades and the kidnapping of Aldo Moro, charges that he and many observers regarded as politically motivated. He spent years in prison and in exile in France, where he taught and collaborated with major French thinkers. After returning to Italy and serving additional time, he emerged as a central theorist of global capitalism and resistance, co‑authoring influential works such as "Empire" and "Multitude" with Michael Hardt, which helped recast debates about sovereignty, globalization, and political subjectivity.

Earlier, in "The Savage Anomaly: The Power of Spinoza's Metaphysics and Politics," Negri offered a seminal reinterpretation of Baruch Spinoza, reading Spinoza’s metaphysics as a radical philosophy of immanence and collective power. This work connected his historical studies to his political project, highlighting how ideas of multitude, desire, and constituent power could ground new forms of emancipatory politics. Throughout his life, Negri’s blend of rigorous scholarship, political engagement, and theoretical innovation made him a controversial yet pivotal figure in late 20th‑ and early 21st‑century critical theory.
