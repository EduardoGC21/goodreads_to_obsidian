## Biography
Antonio "Toni" Negri (1933–2023) was an Italian political philosopher and militant whose work reshaped late-20th-century Marxist theory. Born in Padua, he became involved in left-wing politics at a young age and later taught political philosophy at the University of Padua. Negri was associated with operaismo (workerism) and Autonomia, currents that emphasized workers’ autonomy and direct action, and he became a prominent, polarizing figure in Italy’s turbulent political landscape of the 1960s and 1970s.

In the late 1970s, Negri was arrested and charged in connection with political violence, including accusations—later dropped—of involvement with the Red Brigades. He spent years in prison and in exile in France, where he taught and continued to develop his philosophical work, engaging with thinkers like Spinoza, Marx, and Foucault. "The Savage Anomaly: The Power of Spinoza's Metaphysics and Politics" is a key example of how he reread classic philosophy to illuminate contemporary struggles over power, subjectivity, and democracy.

Negri’s later collaboration with Michael Hardt, especially on the books "Empire", "Multitude", and "Commonwealth", brought him wide international recognition beyond specialist academic circles. These works argued that globalization had produced a new, networked form of sovereignty and a correspondingly new terrain of resistance. Throughout his life, Negri remained a controversial but influential figure whose ideas linked radical theory, social movements, and debates about democracy, globalization, and the commons.
