## Biography
Edgar Allan Poe (1809–1849) was an American writer, poet, editor, and literary critic whose work helped shape modern horror, detective fiction, and psychological suspense. Born in Boston and orphaned at a young age, he was taken in by the Allan family in Richmond, Virginia, from whom he later became estranged. His life was marked by financial hardship, unstable employment in journalism and editing, and personal tragedy, including the early death of his wife, Virginia Clemm. These experiences deeply informed the emotional intensity and macabre themes of his writing.

Poe gained fame for his poetry and short stories, particularly for works that explored madness, guilt, and the uncanny. “The Raven” made him widely known in his lifetime and exemplified his musical language, careful use of rhythm, and fascination with grief and loss. His tales of ratiocination introduced one of the earliest fictional detectives, influencing later crime and mystery writers, while his Gothic stories set a template for modern horror. Despite his troubled life and mysterious death in 1849, Poe’s innovations in form, mood, and narrative technique secured his status as a foundational figure in American and world literature.

Poe’s critical essays were also significant; he argued for unity of effect in short fiction and for precision and economy in poetic composition, ideas that shaped later literary theory. His complex relationships—with his foster family, his literary contemporaries, and the public—often involved conflict and misunderstanding, yet they also placed him at the center of antebellum American literary culture. Today, Poe is recognized not only for his dark imagination but for his technical mastery and lasting impact on genres ranging from symbolist poetry to science fiction and detective stories.
