## Biography
Edgar Allan Poe (1809–1849) was an American writer, poet, critic, and editor whose work helped shape modern literary horror, detective fiction, and psychological suspense. Born in Boston and orphaned at a young age, he was taken in by the Allan family in Richmond, Virginia, from whom he took his middle name. His life was marked by financial instability, strained family relationships, and periods of illness, all of which fed into the dark, introspective tone of his writings.

Poe gained lasting fame with poems like "The Raven," which showcased his mastery of rhythm, sound, and mood, and with tales such as those collected in his various story volumes, where he refined the short story as a serious literary form. He is also credited with pioneering the modern detective story through characters like C. Auguste Dupin, influencing generations of crime and mystery writers. Despite his troubled personal life and early death under mysterious circumstances, Poe’s work profoundly shaped Gothic literature and popular conceptions of horror, and he remains a central figure in both American and world literature.
