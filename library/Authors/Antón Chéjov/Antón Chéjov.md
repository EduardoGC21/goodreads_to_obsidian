## Biography
Anton Chekhov (1860–1904) was a Russian playwright, short-story writer, and practicing physician, widely regarded as one of the most important figures in modern literature. Born in Taganrog in the Russian Empire to a struggling merchant family, he financed his medical studies in Moscow by writing humorous sketches and short pieces for newspapers and magazines. This early need to earn money sharpened his eye for everyday details and the quiet tragedies of ordinary life, traits that would define his mature work.

While working as a doctor—a profession he famously described with the phrase “medicine is my lawful wife and literature my mistress”—Chekhov produced an extraordinary body of short stories and novellas that transformed the form, favoring subtle psychological insight and open endings over moral lessons or melodrama. His trips across Russia, including a difficult journey to the penal colony on Sakhalin Island, deepened his social awareness and informed his compassionate but unsentimental view of human suffering.

In his later years Chekhov became equally influential as a dramatist, writing plays such as "The Seagull," "Uncle Vanya," "Three Sisters," and "The Cherry Orchard," which revolutionized theatre through their understated action, ensemble focus, and attention to atmosphere rather than plot. His personal life was marked by close friendships in literary circles and a complex, tender relationship with the actress Olga Knipper, whom he married late in life. Despite chronic ill health from tuberculosis, Chekhov continued to write until shortly before his death in Badenweiler, Germany, leaving a legacy that shaped twentieth-century fiction and drama across the world.
