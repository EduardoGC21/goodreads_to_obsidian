## Biography
Mircea Eliade (1907–1986) was a Romanian historian of religions, philosopher, and writer whose work profoundly shaped the academic study of myth and religious experience in the twentieth century. Born in Bucharest, he showed precocious interest in literature, philosophy, and spirituality, traveling to India in the late 1920s to study Sanskrit and Indian philosophy, an experience that deeply influenced his later thought. After returning to Romania, he became part of an influential intellectual circle, writing novels, essays, and scholarly works that explored the symbolic, mythical, and sacred dimensions of human existence.

During the Second World War Eliade served in Romanian diplomatic posts, and after the communist takeover he remained in exile, living first in France and later in the United States. He eventually settled at the University of Chicago, where he held a prominent chair in the history of religions and trained generations of scholars. Eliade’s major works, including "The Myth of the Eternal Return," advanced the idea that myths and ritual structures express a fundamental human longing to re-enter sacred time and recover primordial meanings. His synthesis of comparative religion, phenomenology, and history made him one of the most influential and controversial scholars of religion of his era, praised for the breadth and creativity of his vision even as his political entanglements and interpretive methods continue to invite debate.
