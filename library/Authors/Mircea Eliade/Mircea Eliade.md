## Biography
Mircea Eliade (1907–1986) was a Romanian historian of religions, philosopher, novelist, and essayist whose work profoundly shaped the modern academic study of myth and the sacred. Born in Bucharest, he showed precocious intellectual talent, studying philosophy at the University of Bucharest before traveling to India in the late 1920s, where he immersed himself in Sanskrit, yoga, and Hindu philosophy. This formative period led him to see religious symbols and myths as expressions of a universal human search for meaning, a view he later developed in influential works such as "The Myth of the Eternal Return" ("El mito del eterno retorno") and "The Sacred and the Profane."

Eliade’s life was marked by both scholarly achievement and political controversy. In interwar Romania he was associated, at least for a time, with far-right and nationalist circles, including sympathy toward the Iron Guard, a connection that later drew criticism and raised questions about his political and moral legacy. After World War II, he lived in exile, first in France and then in the United States, where he joined the University of Chicago in the 1950s and became a central figure in building the "history of religions" as an academic field.

At Chicago, Eliade mentored students from around the world and collaborated with scholars across disciplines, promoting a comparative approach to mythology and religious symbolism. He argued that myths and rituals reveal an encounter with the sacred that structures human experience, influencing not only religious studies but also anthropology, literature, and philosophy. Despite ongoing debates about his politics, Eliade remains a major—if contested—figure in twentieth‑century intellectual history, remembered for his ambitious attempts to understand the patterns of religious life across cultures and eras.
