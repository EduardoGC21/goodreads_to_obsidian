## Biography
Ivan Turgenev (1818–1883) was a Russian novelist, short story writer, and playwright whose work helped shape the development of the modern European novel. Born into a wealthy landowning family in Oryol Province, he was raised primarily by a domineering mother, an experience that deeply influenced his portrayals of family life and social hierarchy. Educated in Moscow, Saint Petersburg, and Berlin, Turgenev absorbed Western philosophical and literary ideas, which later informed his nuanced depictions of Russia’s clash between tradition and modernity.

Turgenev’s fiction is best known for its subtle psychology, liberal humanism, and attention to generational conflict, most famously dramatized in “Fathers and Sons,” where he explores tensions between older liberals and younger radicals in mid-19th-century Russia. He spent much of his adult life in Western Europe, particularly in France, and maintained close relationships with leading cultural figures, including the singer Pauline Viardot, with whom he had a lifelong intimate attachment. His position between Russia and the West allowed him to serve as a cultural bridge: he introduced Russian literature to European audiences and championed fellow Russian writers such as Tolstoy and Dostoevsky, even when he disagreed with them. By the time of his death in 1883, Turgenev was recognized as one of the foremost realist writers of his century, admired for his elegant style and humane, often melancholic, vision of social change.
