## Biography
Ivan Sergeyevich Turgenev (1818–1883) was a Russian novelist, short story writer, and playwright, born into a wealthy landowning family in the Oryol region of the Russian Empire. Educated in Moscow, St. Petersburg, and Berlin, he absorbed Western European philosophical and liberal ideas, which later shaped his nuanced portrayals of Russian society and the tensions between tradition and modernity. His early fame came from the story cycle A Sportsman’s Sketches, whose sympathetic depiction of peasants helped influence public sentiment against serfdom.

Turgenev became internationally renowned for his psychologically subtle novels and novellas, marked by restrained style and deep compassion for his characters. Fathers and Sons (known in Spanish as Padres e hijos) is his most celebrated work, capturing the generational conflict between liberal fathers and nihilist sons on the eve of great social change in Russia. Throughout his life he maintained complex relationships with contemporaries such as Leo Tolstoy and Fyodor Dostoevsky, and he spent long periods in Western Europe, especially in the circle of the singer Pauline Viardot, with whom he had a devoted lifelong attachment.

Regarded as a key figure in 19th‑century realism, Turgenev acted as a cultural bridge between Russia and Western Europe, introducing Russian literature to foreign audiences and advocating moderate reform over revolutionary upheaval. His works of short fiction and novellas, including the kinds of shorter pieces collected in volumes of "novelas cortas," influenced later European and American writers with their elegant prose, emotional restraint, and humane insight into love, family, and social change.
