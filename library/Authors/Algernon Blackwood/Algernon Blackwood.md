## Biography
Algernon Blackwood (1869–1951) was an English writer, journalist, and adventurer best known as one of the great masters of the supernatural short story. Born in Shooter’s Hill, Kent, into a strict religious household, he grew up fascinated by nature and the unseen, interests that would later permeate his fiction. As a young man he led a restless, exploratory life, working variously as a hotel manager, farmer, bartender, and journalist in Canada and the United States before returning to Britain. These years abroad deepened his sense of vast landscapes and elemental forces, which became central to his imaginative world.

Back in England, Blackwood turned seriously to writing and rapidly became a prominent figure in early twentieth‑century weird fiction. Stories such as “The Willows” and “The Wendigo” exemplify his distinctive approach, in which wilderness and natural settings themselves seem to awaken an impersonal, cosmic awe rather than simple horror. He was deeply influenced by mysticism, the occult, and the teachings of the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn and later the Theosophical Society, and he wove ideas of spiritual evolution, psychic experience, and the porous boundary between human consciousness and the natural world into his tales.

Beyond his fiction, Blackwood was a well‑known public personality in Britain, becoming a popular radio and early television storyteller for the BBC, where his dramatic readings introduced a wide audience to the ghost story as a serious art form. He never married, sustaining instead a network of friendships within literary and esoteric circles, and he remained intellectually curious and spiritually inclined throughout his life. By the time of his death in 1951, he was widely regarded as a key figure in supernatural literature, admired by contemporaries and later writers alike for his ability to convey mystery, wonder, and terror through encounters with a living, almost sentient natural world.
