## Biography
Algernon Henry Blackwood (1869–1951) was an English writer, journalist, and later radio and television broadcaster, widely regarded as one of the foremost masters of the supernatural and weird tale. Born in Shooter’s Hill, Kent, into a strict and deeply religious Victorian household, he developed an early love of the natural world that contrasted sharply with the narrow piety of his upbringing. Educated in England and briefly in Germany, he became fascinated by Eastern philosophies and occult ideas, interests that would later permeate his fiction.

In his twenties and thirties Blackwood led a notably wandering life, working a succession of jobs in Canada and the United States—farmer, hotel keeper, bartender, newspaper reporter, and private secretary—before returning to England in 1899 to devote himself to writing. This varied experience, combined with his mystical outlook and passion for remote landscapes, shaped stories such as “The Willows” and “The Wendigo,” in which rivers, forests, and wilderness spaces become sites of awe as much as terror. Over several decades he produced numerous short‑story collections, novels, children’s books, and plays, and he joined organizations like the Theosophical Society and The Ghost Club, building friendships with literary and occult figures while maintaining a largely solitary personal life; he never married but was remembered as genial and contemplative company.

From the 1940s onward Blackwood gained a new audience as a charismatic teller of ghost stories on British radio and television, and he was appointed a Commander of the Order of the British Empire in recognition of his contributions to broadcasting and literature. His work influenced later writers of horror and fantasy, and critics have often singled out his ability to convey a sense of the numinous in nature and to suggest realities just beyond human perception. Even within a rich field of early twentieth‑century supernatural fiction, Blackwood stands out as a writer whose tales blend adventure, metaphysical speculation, and psychological insight into experiences of the unknown.
