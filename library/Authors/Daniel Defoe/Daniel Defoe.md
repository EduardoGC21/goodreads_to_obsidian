## Biography
Daniel Defoe (1660–1731) was an English writer, trader, and political commentator, best known as one of the early shapers of the English novel. Born in London to a family of Dissenters, he was originally intended for the Presbyterian ministry but instead pursued business, which led him through a series of ventures in trade, manufacturing, and speculation. His early life was marked by the turbulence of late 17th‑century England—plague, the Great Fire of London, and the political and religious conflicts surrounding the Glorious Revolution—all of which informed his sharp interest in social and economic issues.

Before turning to long-form fiction, Defoe gained prominence as a prolific pamphleteer and journalist, writing on politics, religion, finance, and international affairs. His satirical and polemical works brought him both influence and trouble, including prosecution and time in the pillory for seditious libel. Defoe later worked, somewhat controversially, as an intelligence agent and propagandist for the government, navigating complex loyalties in an era of shifting power between crown and parliament.

Defoe’s enduring fame rests largely on "Robinson Crusoe" (1719), often cited as a foundational English novel and a landmark in realistic prose narrative. The book’s focus on individual survival, enterprise, and spiritual reflection reflects Defoe’s lifelong engagement with commerce, morality, and the experience of the self in a rapidly changing world. Over his career he produced a vast body of work, including other novels, travel narratives, and economic treatises, influencing later novelists and helping to define the possibilities of fiction in the 18th century.
