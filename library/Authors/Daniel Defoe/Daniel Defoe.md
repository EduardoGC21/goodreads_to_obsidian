## Biography
Daniel Defoe (c.1660–1731) was an English writer, journalist, and political pamphleteer, widely regarded as one of the early masters of the English novel. Born in London to a family of Protestant Dissenters, he was educated outside the Anglican establishment and initially pursued a career as a merchant, experiencing both prosperity and financial ruin. His sharp pen led him into political controversy; satirical works such as his attack on religious intolerance brought prosecution, the pillory, and imprisonment. Defoe later worked as a government agent and propagandist, moving in the complex world of early 18th‑century politics while writing prolifically for newspapers and periodicals.

Defoe’s significance in literary history rests above all on his innovative use of realistic detail, first-person narration, and the presentation of fiction as if it were authentic memoir or report. "Robinson Crusoe" (1719), his best-known work, exemplifies this approach, blending adventure, spiritual reflection, and economic self-sufficiency in a way that resonated with the emerging modern middle class and imperial Britain. He went on to write other influential prose narratives such as "Moll Flanders" and "A Journal of the Plague Year," which further developed his documentary style and keen interest in social, economic, and moral life. Defoe’s combination of political engagement, commercial experience, and narrative experimentation helped shape the development of the English novel and left a lasting mark on later fiction.
