## Biography
Franz Kafka (1883–1924) was a German‑language writer born in Prague to a middle‑class Jewish family in what was then the Austro‑Hungarian Empire. Trained as a lawyer, he spent most of his working life in a bureaucratic job at an insurance company, writing fiction privately and publishing only a small portion of it during his lifetime. His difficult relationship with his domineering father, his sense of alienation as a German‑speaking Jew in a predominantly Czech city, and his fragile health, including the tuberculosis that eventually killed him, all fed into his work and self‑understanding. The famous "Letter to His Father" exposes the emotional conflict at the heart of his family life and helps explain the recurring themes of guilt, fear, and powerlessness that mark his fiction.

Kafka’s posthumous reputation rests on the singular, unsettling vision expressed in works such as "The Trial" and "The Castle," where individuals confront opaque, impersonal systems they can neither comprehend nor escape. Although he requested that his manuscripts be destroyed after his death, his friend and literary executor Max Brod defied these wishes and arranged their publication, making Kafka one of the most influential figures in twentieth‑century literature. His exploration of anxiety, bureaucracy, and the absurd has shaped modern understandings of existential crisis and totalitarianism, and the adjective “Kafkaesque” has entered many languages to describe situations that echo the nightmarish logic of his stories.
