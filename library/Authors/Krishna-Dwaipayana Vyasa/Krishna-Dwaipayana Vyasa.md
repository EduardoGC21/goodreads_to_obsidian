## Biography
Krishna-Dwaipayana Vyasa, commonly known as Vyasa or Veda Vyasa, is a central and revered sage in Hindu tradition, traditionally credited with composing or compiling several foundational Sanskrit texts. He is regarded as the author of the Mahabharata, within which the Bhagavad Gita appears as a key philosophical dialogue, and as the compiler who arranged the Vedas into their four canonical collections. In Hindu mythology he is often described as the son of the sage Parashara and the fisherwoman Satyavati, born on an island in the Yamuna River, which gives rise to his epithet “Dwaipayana” (island-born). His figure stands at the intersection of history and legend, with many details of his life considered more theological than strictly historical.

Vyasa’s significance lies not only in authorship but also in his role as a transmitter and organizer of sacred knowledge. He is portrayed as a rishi who both participates in and narrates the events of the Mahabharata, and as a spiritual teacher whose insights shape later Hindu philosophy, devotion, and law. The Bhagavad Gita, embedded within the Mahabharata, is often associated with his redaction of the epic and has become one of the most influential works in Indian religious and philosophical history. Through these texts, Vyasa is seen as a foundational figure for multiple Hindu traditions, including Vedanta and various bhakti movements.

Traditional accounts place Vyasa in the Dvapara Yuga, prior to the beginning of the current age (Kali Yuga), making his historical dates uncertain and the subject of scholarly debate. Within the tradition, he is sometimes described as a chiranjivi (long-lived or deathless being), further complicating attempts to assign precise birth and death years. As a result, modern historians treat him as a possibly composite or symbolic figure representing the work of several sages and redactors over time, while religious communities continue to venerate him as a single, supremely important author and seer.
