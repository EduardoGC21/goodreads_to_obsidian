## Biography
Juan Nepomuceno Carlos Pérez Rulfo Vizcaíno (1917–1986) was a Mexican writer, screenwriter, and photographer whose small but revolutionary body of work transformed 20th‑century Latin American literature. Born in Jalisco to a family of landowners ruined by the Mexican Revolution and the Cristero War, he lost both parents in childhood and was raised by relatives and in boarding schools and an orphanage. These early experiences of violence, dispossession, and rural hardship became the emotional landscape of his fiction, which often portrays impoverished villages marked by war, religious conflict, and silence.

Rulfo spent much of his life working as a government employee and traveling across Mexico as an immigration agent and later as an editor at the National Institute for Indigenous People, while writing in his spare time. With the short‑story collection “El llano en llamas” (1953) and the novel “Pedro Páramo” (1955), he created a world where memory, the supernatural, and everyday rural life intertwine, influencing the Latin American Boom and writers such as Gabriel García Márquez. He married Clara Aparicio, with whom he had four children, and cultivated close relationships with contemporaries like Juan José Arreola and other Mexican writers, even as he remained personally reserved and published little.

In his later years Rulfo largely abandoned book‑length writing but continued to shape Mexican culture through his editorial work and his stark, poetic photography of rural landscapes and communities. Celebrated with major honors, including the Mexican National Prize for Literature and Spain’s Prince of Asturias Prize, he came to be regarded as a foundational figure of modern Mexican narrative, proving that two major books and a handful of other texts could be enough to redefine a literary tradition.
