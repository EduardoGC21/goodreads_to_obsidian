## Biography
Juan Rulfo (1917–1986) was a Mexican writer and photographer whose small but powerful body of work reshaped 20th‑century Latin American literature. Born Juan Nepomuceno Carlos Pérez Rulfo Vizcaíno in Apulco, Jalisco, and raised in nearby towns, he lost both parents at a young age during the violent aftermath of the Mexican Revolution and the Cristero War. This experience of orphanhood, displacement, and rural violence marked his imagination and later became central to the desolate landscapes, ghosts, and silences that populate his fiction.

Rulfo spent much of his adult life working in government and publishing offices while writing fiction on the side, which partly explains the brevity of his literary output. His collection "El llano en llamas" and the novel "Pedro Páramo" gained him international recognition for their condensed, poetic prose, complex narrative voices, and blending of the living and the dead, profoundly influencing the Latin American Boom and authors such as Gabriel García Márquez. Beyond his literary prestige, Rulfo was also an accomplished photographer who documented rural Mexico, further testifying to his deep engagement with the country’s people, landscapes, and historical wounds.

Reserved and self‑critical, Rulfo published little after his early successes, which only added to his aura as a legendary, almost mythical figure in Mexican letters. He married Clara Aparicio in 1948, and they had several children; their family life remained largely private, in contrast with his public stature as a national literary icon. By the time of his death in Mexico City in 1986, Juan Rulfo was widely regarded as one of Mexico’s greatest writers and a crucial precursor to modern Latin American narrative.
