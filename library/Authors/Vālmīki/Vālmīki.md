## Biography
Vālmīki is revered in the Sanskrit literary and religious tradition as the sage-poet traditionally credited with composing the epic Ramayana, one of the two great classical epics of India. According to later legends, he began life as a common man—often portrayed as a hunter or bandit—who underwent a profound spiritual transformation after an encounter with the sage Narada. Through intense penance and devotion, he is said to have attained the status of a rishi (seer), turning his life toward contemplation, teaching, and poetic inspiration.

Vālmīki’s significance lies not only in his role as the putative author of the Ramayana but also as a foundational figure in the development of Sanskrit kavya (poetic literature). The Ramayana’s refined language, moral reflection, and exploration of dharma (righteous conduct) have led later tradition to honor him as the “ādikavi,” or the first poet. Within the narrative, he appears as a character who shelters Sita and educates her twin sons, Lava and Kusha, indicating his role as both spiritual guide and cultural transmitter.

Although historical details of Vālmīki’s life, including exact dates and biographical facts, remain uncertain and are largely preserved through legend, his influence on South Asian religious life, ethics, and aesthetics has been profound. Through the Ramayana and the traditions surrounding his name, Vālmīki stands as a symbol of moral transformation, poetic creativity, and the power of narrative to shape collective ideals of virtue, kingship, and devotion.
