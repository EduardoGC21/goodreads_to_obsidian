## Biography
Vālmīki is revered in the Hindu tradition as the legendary sage and poet who composed the Sanskrit epic Ramayana, one of the foundational works of Indian and South Asian literature. Little is known about him as a historical figure, and most details of his life come from the Ramayana itself and later devotional legends. These portray him as an ascetic rishi living in a forest hermitage, honored with titles such as Maharishi (great sage) and Ādi Kavi (first poet) for shaping the Ramayana into a long, unified poetic narrative whose influence has endured for millennia.

Traditional accounts describe Vālmīki’s life as a profound moral and spiritual transformation. Some narratives say he was originally a Brahmin who, through hardship or misguidance, became a robber or hunter, only to be confronted by the sage Nārada and led to self-reflection and repentance. Immersed in long meditation and the recitation of a mantra associated with Rāma, he is said to have remained motionless until an anthill formed around him; emerging from it, he received the name Vālmīki, derived from the Sanskrit word for anthill. In these stories, his journey from violence to contemplative wisdom exemplifies the power of inner change and devotion.

Within the Ramayana’s own narrative frame, Vālmīki appears as both author and character. He asks Nārada who embodies the ideal of virtue and is directed to Rāma, then receives inspiration to cast Rāma’s life into poetic form. In the later part of the epic, his hermitage becomes a refuge for Sītā after her banishment; there he raises and educates the twins Lava and Kuśa, teaching them the Ramayana so they can later recite it to their father, Rāma. Through his poetic achievement, his role as teacher and protector, and his enduring status as the first great poet of Sanskrit epic, Vālmīki occupies a central place in the religious, literary, and ethical imagination of India, even though his exact historical dates remain unknown.
