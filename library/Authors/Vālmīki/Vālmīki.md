## Biography
Vālmīki is traditionally revered as the sage-poet who composed the Sanskrit epic Ramayana, one of the foundational texts of Indian literature and religious culture. Often called the “Ādikavi,” or the first poet, he is credited with shaping the classical form of Sanskrit epic poetry, using the śloka meter that would influence generations of poets and storytellers across South Asia. In later tradition, his life story itself becomes emblematic of moral transformation, with many accounts describing him as a former outlaw or bandit who, through spiritual instruction and penance, became an enlightened rishi and a guide to kings and commoners alike.

Within the narrative world of the Ramayana, Vālmīki appears as both author and character: he provides shelter to Sita during her exile and raises her twin sons, Lava and Kusha, whom he teaches to sing the epic of their father, Rāma. This dual role underscores his significance as both a guardian of dharma and a transmitter of cultural memory. Over centuries, his portrayal has inspired devotional communities, literary retellings, and artistic representations, and he is honored as a foundational figure for Sanskrit kavya (poetic) tradition and for the broader ethical and spiritual imagination of Hindu civilization.
