## Biography
Alfred William Pollard (1859–1944) was an English bibliographer, literary scholar, and editor whose work helped shape modern understanding of early English printed books and Renaissance literature. Educated at King’s College London and St John’s College, Oxford, he joined the staff of the British Museum (now the British Library) in the Department of Printed Books, where he eventually became Keeper. Pollard’s meticulous cataloguing, descriptive bibliography, and historical analyses made him a leading authority on incunabula and early modern printing practices.

Beyond technical bibliography, Pollard played an important role in Shakespearean and medieval studies, challenging dubious attributions and promoting more rigorous textual standards. He was a long‑time editor of the scholarly journal *The Library* and contributed to major reference works. His retellings and editorial work on medieval and Arthurian material, including versions related to stories such as those in *El Rey Arturo y los caballeros de la Tabla Redonda*, helped introduce wider audiences to classic narratives through careful, accessible prose. Over a long and productive career, Pollard’s combination of archival expertise and literary sensitivity left a lasting impact on how scholars and readers approach the history of the book and English literature.
