## Biography
Alfred William Pollard (1859–1944) was a British bibliographer, literary scholar, and editor whose work helped shape modern understanding of early English printing and literature. Born in London, he studied at King’s College and at the University of Oxford before joining the Department of Printed Books at the British Museum in 1883, where he spent over four decades and eventually became Curator of Printed Books. A gifted linguist and meticulous scholar, he became one of the key authorities on incunabula, early illustrated books, and the history of the English Bible, producing influential catalogues and historical studies that are still referenced today.

Pollard’s reputation rests in large part on his pioneering contributions to Shakespearean and medieval English studies. His bibliographical analysis of Shakespeare’s texts—including the formulation of the “bad quarto” theory—reframed debates about textual corruption and transmission in Elizabethan drama and remains a cornerstone of textual criticism. He also edited important works such as Chaucer’s “Canterbury Tales” and helped bring medieval drama and devotional literature to modern readers. His adaptation of Thomas Malory’s Arthurian material, published in English and later in Spanish as “El Rey Arturo y los caballeros de la Tabla Redonda,” exemplifies his talent for mediating classic texts for contemporary audiences.

Beyond his own writing, Pollard played a central role in the institutional life of bibliography. From 1893 to 1934 he served as honorary secretary of the Bibliographical Society, fostering collaboration among scholars and collectors and helping to professionalize the field. He maintained long-standing relationships with publishers and fellow scholars, and his careful, historically grounded methods influenced generations of editors and literary historians. Pollard’s legacy is that of a bridge between book history and literary criticism, showing how the physical forms and printing histories of books illuminate the meanings of the texts they transmit.
