## Biography
Lao Tzu (also rendered Laozi or Lao-tze) is the legendary Chinese sage traditionally regarded as the author of the Tao Te Ching and the founding figure of philosophical Taoism. Very little is known with certainty about his life, and many accounts blend history and myth. Traditional stories place him in the Zhou dynasty, where he is said to have served as an archivist or keeper of records at the royal court, a position that would have given him broad exposure to history, ritual, and philosophy. According to these tales, he grew disillusioned with political corruption and social decay, choosing a life of withdrawal and reflection.

In later accounts, Lao Tzu’s departure from civilization becomes a defining episode: as he rode westward to leave the realm, a border guard recognized his wisdom and begged him to write down his teaching. The text he composed in response—known today as the Tao Te Ching—presents a vision of the Way (Tao) as the underlying principle of reality and advocates humility, simplicity, non‑contention, and rule by minimal interference. Although historians debate whether Lao Tzu was a single historical person, a composite of several thinkers, or a symbolic figure, his attributed work profoundly shaped Chinese thought, influencing not only Taoism but also Confucianism, Buddhism in East Asia, and later global philosophy.

Over the centuries, Lao Tzu has been revered as a sage, a spiritual master, and even a deity within religious Taoism. His supposed relationship to other early thinkers, including Confucius, is the subject of numerous anecdotes that contrast his emphasis on effortless harmony and naturalness with more formal moral and ritual systems. Regardless of the uncertainties surrounding his biography, Lao Tzu’s image as an old, wise wanderer who turned away from worldly ambition to articulate a timeless vision of balance and harmony has become central to how later generations understand the Tao and its place in human life.
