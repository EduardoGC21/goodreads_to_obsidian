## Biography
Lao Tzu (also spelled Laozi or Lao-Tze) is the semi-legendary Chinese sage traditionally regarded as the author of the *Tao Te Ching* and the founding figure of philosophical Taoism. Very little is known with certainty about his life; most accounts come from later tradition and blend history with myth. Classical sources describe him as an older contemporary of Confucius who served as an archivist or historian at the Zhou royal court, renowned for his wisdom, humility, and insight into the nature of the Way (Tao).

According to legend, disillusioned by political decline and moral decay, Lao Tzu left the Zhou capital riding an ox toward the western frontier. There, a gatekeeper recognized his stature and asked him to set down his teachings before departing into obscurity. Lao Tzu is said to have composed the brief but profound *Tao Te Ching* in response, outlining a vision of reality grounded in harmony with the Tao, naturalness, and non-coercive action (wu wei). Whether or not this story is historically accurate, the text and the figure of Lao Tzu have profoundly shaped Chinese philosophy, religion, and later East Asian culture.

Over centuries, Lao Tzu became venerated not only as a philosopher but also as a spiritual and even divine figure within religious Taoism. His image has come to symbolize wisdom, quiet strength, and the value of aligning human life with the larger patterns of the natural world. The scarcity of reliable biographical details has allowed successive generations to reinterpret him in light of their own concerns, ensuring his continued influence across philosophy, religion, literature, and political thought.
