## Biography
Lao Tzu (often dated c. 6th–4th century BCE) is traditionally regarded as the semi-legendary founder of philosophical Taoism and the reputed author of the Tao Te Ching. According to later historical accounts, he served as an archivist or historian at the Zhou royal court, where his deep knowledge of ritual, history, and classic texts shaped his reflections on the nature of reality, virtue, and governance. Disillusioned with the moral and political decay of his time, he is said to have departed the court and ridden west on a water buffalo, where a border guard persuaded him to write down his teachings before disappearing from history.

The Tao Te Ching, attributed to Lao Tzu, became a foundational work of Chinese philosophy and religion, exerting enormous influence on later Confucian, Legalist, Buddhist, and Daoist thinkers. It emphasizes living in harmony with the Tao (the Way), valuing humility, non-contention, simplicity, and non-coercive leadership. Lao Tzu’s thought shaped Chinese ideas of rulership, personal cultivation, and cosmology, and he is venerated within religious Daoism as a sage and sometimes as a deified figure. Because sources about his life were written centuries after his supposed era and mix history with legend, many details of his biography, including exact dates, remain uncertain.
