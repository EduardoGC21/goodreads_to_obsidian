## Biography
Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish moral philosopher and political economist whose ideas laid much of the intellectual groundwork for modern economic thought. Born in Kirkcaldy, Scotland, he studied at the University of Glasgow and Balliol College, Oxford, later becoming a professor of logic and then moral philosophy at Glasgow. His early work focused on ethics, human behavior, and sympathy, reflecting his belief that economic life is embedded in broader moral and social relations.

Smith’s most influential contributions came through his analysis of markets, specialization, and the division of labor, culminating in his landmark work often known in English as "The Wealth of Nations." In it he examined how self-interest, operating within a framework of laws and social norms, could generate prosperity and complex economic order without centralized direction. Smith was closely connected to other Enlightenment figures—particularly in the Scottish Enlightenment—maintaining friendships and intellectual exchanges with thinkers such as David Hume. He spent part of his career as a tutor and later as a customs commissioner, roles that exposed him both to elite European society and to the practical workings of trade and regulation. Smith’s legacy is that of a foundational theorist of political economy whose nuanced view of markets, morality, and the state continues to shape debates about capitalism, public policy, and social welfare.
