## Biography
Adam Smith (1723–1790) was a Scottish moral philosopher, political economist, and key figure of the Scottish Enlightenment. Born in Kirkcaldy, he studied at the University of Glasgow under Francis Hutcheson and later at Balliol College, Oxford, developing a lifelong interest in ethics, jurisprudence, and the forces that shape commercial society. His close friendship with David Hume and his involvement in Edinburgh and Glasgow’s intellectual circles placed him at the center of 18th‑century debates about human nature, freedom, and markets.

Smith first gained prominence with "The Theory of Moral Sentiments" (1759), where he argued that sympathy and the impartial spectator shape moral judgment. His later work, "An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations" (1776)—widely known and translated, including into Spanish as "La riqueza de las naciones"—became foundational for classical economics, articulating ideas such as the division of labor, free trade, and the “invisible hand” of the market. Beyond his writings, Smith served as a commissioner of customs in Scotland, lived a relatively private and scholarly life, and left a legacy that profoundly influenced economic thought, public policy, and liberal political philosophy.
