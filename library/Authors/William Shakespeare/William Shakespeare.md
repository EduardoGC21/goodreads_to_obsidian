## Biography
William Shakespeare (1564–1616) was an English playwright, poet, and actor whose works have become foundational to English literature and world drama. Born in Stratford-upon-Avon, he likely attended the local grammar school, where he gained a strong grounding in Latin language and classical authors. In his late teens he married Anne Hathaway, with whom he had three children, and by his late twenties he had moved to London and established himself in the city’s vibrant theatrical world.

Shakespeare became a shareholder and leading dramatist for the Lord Chamberlain’s Men (later the King’s Men), writing comedies, histories, tragedies, and romances that explored power, love, ambition, jealousy, and the complexities of human character. Plays such as "Hamlet," "Romeo and Juliet," "Macbeth," "The Merchant of Venice," "Coriolanus," and "Titus Andronicus" show his range from intimate psychological drama to large-scale political conflict. His mastery of blank verse and his inventive use of the English language helped shape modern English, and many of his phrases and characters have entered common cultural reference.

In his later years, Shakespeare returned to Stratford-upon-Avon as a prosperous landowner, maintaining ties with his theater company and collaborators in London. He died there in 1616 and was buried in Holy Trinity Church. Over the centuries, his works have been edited, translated, and performed in countless languages and settings, securing his reputation as one of the most influential writers in history and a central figure in the development of Western drama and poetry.
