## Biography
Samuel Taylor Coleridge (1772–1834) was an English poet, critic, and philosopher, and one of the central figures of early British Romanticism. Born in Ottery St Mary, Devon, he was the youngest of ten children of a clergyman. After his father’s death, Coleridge was educated at Christ’s Hospital in London and later attended Jesus College, Cambridge, though he left without taking a degree. His intense intellectual curiosity, religious questioning, and wide reading in philosophy and theology shaped his lifelong engagement with ideas as much as with poetry.

Coleridge’s close friendship and creative partnership with William Wordsworth produced *Lyrical Ballads* (1798), a landmark volume often seen as inaugurating the Romantic movement in English literature. In this context he wrote some of his most famous poems, including “The Rime of the Ancient Mariner,” which exemplifies his gift for superstition-haunted narrative, psychological depth, and musical language. His critical writings—especially *Biographia Literaria*—introduced and interpreted German philosophy and literary theory for English readers, and his lectures on Shakespeare had a lasting influence on literary criticism.

Coleridge’s life was marked by chronic ill health and a long struggle with opium dependence, which complicated his relationships and curtailed his productivity but also fed into his exploration of the imagination and the unconscious, as seen in visionary poems like “Kubla Khan.” Though he often failed to complete large projects and lived a financially precarious life, his poetic and critical work profoundly shaped Romantic and later Victorian thought, and his collected poems, such as in editions like *The Complete Poems*, continue to be read for their psychological insight and imaginative power.
