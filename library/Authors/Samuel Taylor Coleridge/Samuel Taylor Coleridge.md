## Biography
Samuel Taylor Coleridge (1772–1834) was an English poet, critic, philosopher, and theologian, and one of the central figures of early British Romanticism. Born in Ottery St Mary, Devon, he was the youngest of ten children of a clergyman and schoolmaster. A precocious and sensitive child, Coleridge excelled academically but struggled with health and anxiety. His early adult years were marked by intense intellectual ambition, radical political ideals inspired by the French Revolution, and complicated personal relationships, including an unhappy marriage to Sara Fricker and a profound, lifelong emotional attachment to Sara Hutchinson.

Coleridge’s literary significance rests primarily on a relatively small body of poetry and a large, often fragmentary body of prose. His collaboration with William Wordsworth on "Lyrical Ballads" (1798), which included his most famous poem "The Rime of the Ancient Mariner," helped inaugurate the Romantic movement by shifting poetry toward common language, intense emotion, and the supernatural. Alongside his visionary poems, Coleridge developed influential ideas about imagination, symbolism, and criticism in works like "Biographia Literaria," shaping modern literary theory and philosophy of art.

Coleridge’s life was also marked by opium addiction, deteriorating health, and periods of guilt and religious introspection. Despite these struggles, he became an important public lecturer and conversationalist, influencing a circle of younger writers and thinkers. His blend of metaphysical speculation, psychological insight, and poetic innovation left a lasting impact on English literature, theology, and philosophy, and he is remembered as one of the most original and searching minds of his era.
