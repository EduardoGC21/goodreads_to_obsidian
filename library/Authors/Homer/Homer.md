## Biography
Homer is the name traditionally given to the ancient Greek poet credited with composing the epic poems the Iliad and the Odyssey, foundational works of Western literature. Little is known with certainty about his life, and even in antiquity his biography was a subject of debate and legend. Ancient sources variously placed his birth in different cities of Ionia or mainland Greece, and stories about him often emphasize his status as a wandering, possibly blind bard who recited epic songs for aristocratic audiences.

Modern scholars generally agree that the poems associated with Homer emerged from a long oral tradition of storytelling and performance, and that they were likely shaped and refined over generations before being written down. The Iliad and the Odyssey (known in Spanish as “La Ilíada” and “La Odisea”) not only preserved mythic narratives of the Trojan War and its aftermath but also transmitted values, social norms, and religious ideas central to ancient Greek culture. Because of this, “Homer” has come to represent both an individual poet and a broader cultural process through which oral epic was transformed into canonical texts.

Homer’s significance reaches far beyond his own era: his epics have influenced countless authors, philosophers, and artists from classical Athens through the Roman Empire, the European Renaissance, and into the modern world. Questions about his existence—whether he was a single historical person, a composite of many poets, or a symbolic figure—remain unresolved, but the impact of the works attributed to him on narrative art, conceptions of heroism, and the study of language and culture is undisputed.
