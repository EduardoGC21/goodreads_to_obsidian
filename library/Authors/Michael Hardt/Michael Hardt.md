## Biography
Michael Hardt (born 1960) is an American political philosopher and literary theorist whose work has reshaped contemporary debates on globalization, power, and democracy. Raised near Washington, D.C. (often cited as Bethesda or Washington, DC as his birthplace), he initially trained as an engineer at Swarthmore College before turning toward the humanities and completing a PhD in comparative literature at the University of Washington. Early in his life he combined technical work in alternative energy and solar power with political engagement in Central America, experiences that convinced him that the deepest transformations had to be pursued by rethinking politics, subjectivity, and collective action at a global scale rather than only through technical aid projects.

Hardt’s international reputation rests above all on the trilogy he co‑authored with Italian philosopher Antonio Negri—*Empire* (known in Spanish as *Imperio*), *Multitude: War and Democracy in the Age of Empire*, and *Commonwealth*. In this long-term collaboration, which grew out of his translations of Negri and their first meetings in Paris in the mid‑1980s, Hardt helped articulate a new account of empire, capitalism, and the “multitude” as a potential democratic subject spanning borders. Beyond these books, he has written influential studies on Gilles Deleuze and co-edited volumes on radical political thought, while teaching literature and political theory at Duke University and participating in intellectual networks such as the European Graduate School. His work is marked by its effort to link rigorous philosophical inquiry with the possibilities of joy, love, and collective creativity in political life.

Throughout his career, Hardt has worked in close dialogue with other critical theorists and with social movements, especially those contesting neoliberal globalization and new forms of empire. His writings aim to provide conceptual tools for understanding how contemporary forms of domination might also generate new capacities for democratic organization and common life. By bridging literary theory, continental philosophy, and radical political thought, he has become a central reference point for scholars, activists, and students seeking to imagine forms of politics adequate to an increasingly interconnected world.
