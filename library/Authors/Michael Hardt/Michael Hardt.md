## Biography
Michael Hardt (b. 1960) is an American political philosopher and literary theorist whose work has reshaped contemporary debates on empire, democracy, and global capitalism. Trained initially as an engineer at Swarthmore College, he became politically engaged through work in alternative energy and solidarity projects in Central America before turning to comparative literature and philosophy. His graduate studies at the University of Washington, including a dissertation on Gilles Deleuze, brought him into contact with Italian philosopher Antonio Negri, whose writings he translated and with whom he forged a long-term intellectual partnership.

Hardt is best known for the collaborative project he developed with Negri, especially the books "Empire" (including its Spanish edition "Imperio"), "Multitude: War and Democracy in the Age of Empire," and "Commonwealth," which offer a radical account of contemporary forms of sovereignty, labor, and resistance. Often described as a kind of twenty‑first‑century counterpart to the Communist Manifesto, this work places Hardt at the center of discussions about globalization, social movements, and the politics of the common. Beyond these major texts, he has written on Deleuze, co-edited influential collections in critical theory, and contributed to debates on love, joy, and collective political life.

Over the course of his career, Hardt has held academic positions in the United States and Europe, most prominently as a professor of literature and romance studies at Duke University and as a professor of philosophy or political literature at the European Graduate School. His intellectual trajectory reflects a consistent effort to link theoretical inquiry with emancipatory political practice, maintaining close ties to leftist social movements while engaging in rigorous philosophical reflection on power, subjectivity, and democratic transformation.
