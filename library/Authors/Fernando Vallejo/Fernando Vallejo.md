## Biography
Fernando Vallejo (1942– ) is a Colombian-born writer, filmmaker, and polemicist known for his uncompromising style, autobiographical fictions, and fierce public interventions in cultural and political debates. Born in Medellín, he studied philosophy and literature in Colombia before moving to Italy and later Mexico, where he trained in film. His work often returns to the violence, hypocrisy, and corruption he associates with Colombian society and the broader Latin American political order, blending personal memory with historical and social critique.

Vallejo gained prominence through novels like the "El río del tiempo" cycle and "La virgen de los sicarios", in which he explores Medellín’s drug-fueled violence and the moral collapse he perceives around him. "La puta de Babilonia" extends his iconoclastic stance into a scathing historical attack on the Catholic Church, reflecting his atheism and distrust of institutional power. Across his essays, novels, and films, he is noted for a voice that fuses lyrical intensity with invective, and for his willingness to alienate authorities and readers alike in the pursuit of intellectual and moral consistency.

Though he later became a naturalized Mexican citizen and lives primarily in Mexico, Vallejo’s work remains deeply rooted in his Colombian origins, his family history, and his fraught relationship with his homeland. He has become a significant and controversial figure in contemporary Spanish-language literature, admired for stylistic brilliance and moral courage while criticized for his extremism and pessimism. His life and oeuvre occupy a distinctive place in Latin American letters, standing at the intersection of autobiography, political denunciation, and philosophical reflection.
