## Biography
Fernando Vallejo (1942–) is a Colombian writer, filmmaker, and polemicist born in Medellín, whose work has become emblematic of late-20th- and early-21st-century Latin American dissent. Trained initially in law and music before studying film in Italy and Mexico, he developed a sharp, uncompromising voice that mixes autobiography, philosophical skepticism, and social critique. His life has been marked by movement between Colombia and Mexico, as well as by an often tense relationship with his homeland’s political and religious institutions.

Vallejo is best known for his fierce criticism of the Catholic Church, Colombian politics, and social hypocrisy, a stance summed up in works like "La puta de Babilonia," where he launches a sweeping historical and theological attack on the Church’s role in Latin America and the world. Across his novels, essays, and memoirs, he addresses themes such as violence in Medellín, the fragility of life, and his own homosexuality, often blurring boundaries between narrator and author. Widely awarded and frequently controversial, Vallejo has become a central figure in contemporary Spanish-language literature, admired for the intensity of his prose and his refusal to temper his convictions, even as his views have sparked debate and estrangement in both literary and political circles.
