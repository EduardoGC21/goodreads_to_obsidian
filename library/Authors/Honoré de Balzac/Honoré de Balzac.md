## Biography
Honoré de Balzac (1799–1850) was a French novelist and playwright, best known for his vast cycle of interlinked novels and stories collectively titled "La Comédie humaine." Born in Tours to a middle-class family, he moved to Paris as a young man, briefly studied law, and then turned to writing, enduring years of poverty and commercial failure as a printer and publisher before committing fully to literature. His intense work habits, fueled by endless cups of coffee, became legendary and contributed to a relatively short but extraordinarily productive life.

Balzac’s fiction offers a panoramic, often unsparing portrait of French society after the fall of Napoleon, examining class ambition, financial speculation, family relationships, and the corrosive power of money. He created hundreds of recurring characters who move across different works, giving his universe a remarkable sense of depth and realism. Although he never fully achieved the social standing he desired, his work influenced later writers such as Marcel Proust, Émile Zola, and Charles Dickens, and he is now regarded as one of the founders of literary realism in Europe.

Balzac’s personal life was marked by intense romantic relationships and financial difficulties. For many years he maintained a long-distance correspondence with Ewelina Hańska, a wealthy Polish countess, whom he finally married shortly before his death. Despite chronic debt and declining health, he continued to write at a frenetic pace until the end of his life in Paris, leaving behind one of the most ambitious and influential bodies of work in modern literature.
