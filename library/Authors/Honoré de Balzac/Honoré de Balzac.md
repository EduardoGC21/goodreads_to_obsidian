## Biography
Honoré de Balzac (1799–1850) was a French novelist and playwright who became one of the central architects of 19th‑century European realism. Born in Tours, he moved to Paris as a child, briefly studied law, and worked as a legal clerk before deciding to pursue literature against his family’s wishes. After a series of early failures under pseudonyms and a disastrous stint as a publisher and printer that left him heavily in debt, Balzac committed himself to an extraordinary regime of work, writing through the night fueled by vast quantities of coffee.

Balzac’s major project, La Comédie humaine, is a vast interconnected cycle of novels and stories portraying French society after the fall of Napoleon, from the salons of Paris to the provinces and the emerging bourgeois world of finance and speculation. Works later grouped as his philosophical or metaphysical tales—akin to those represented in collections such as “Cuentos filosóficos” in Spanish—explore ideas about destiny, passion, and power alongside his more strictly realistic novels. His technique of recurring characters and meticulous social detail influenced writers as diverse as Charles Dickens, Émile Zola, Marcel Proust, and Henry James, and helped define the modern novel’s capacity to analyze social structures and individual ambition.

Balzac’s personal life was marked by financial strain, intense romantic entanglements, and chronic health problems. For many years he maintained a passionate correspondence with Ewelina Hańska, a Polish noblewoman whom he finally married in 1850, only months before his death in Paris. Despite dying relatively young, Balzac left behind more than 90 finished works and many fragments, securing his reputation as one of France’s greatest writers and an enduring observer of human desire, greed, love, and social aspiration.
