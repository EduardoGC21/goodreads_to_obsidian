## Biography
Quintus Horatius Flaccus (65–8 BCE), known in English as Horace, was a Roman poet born in Venusia, in southern Italy, to a freedman father who invested in his education and social advancement. He studied in Rome and later in Athens, where he absorbed Greek literature and philosophy, influences that would deeply shape his Latin verse. During the civil wars following Julius Caesar’s assassination, Horace briefly served as an officer on the losing side with Brutus, an experience that left him politically cautious and personally reflective.

After receiving a modest post as a treasury official, Horace gained entry into Rome’s literary and political elite through the patronage of Gaius Maecenas, a close adviser of Augustus. This relationship provided him financial security and a Sabine farm, allowing him to focus on his writing. In works such as his satires, epistles, and odes, Horace explored themes of moderation, friendship, love, politics, and the search for a balanced, contented life, often returning to the ideal of the "golden mean" and the brevity of human existence.

Horace’s lyric achievement in his odes and his reflective, conversational voice in the epistles secured his status as one of Rome’s greatest poets and a central figure in Augustan culture. His poems, including those later gathered under titles like *Satires. Epistles. Ars Poetica* and *Odes. Carmen Saeculare. Epodes*, influenced Western ideas of poetic craft and literary criticism for centuries. Admired by contemporaries and later generations alike, Horace became a model of polished Latin style and philosophical poise, shaping European lyric and didactic poetry well into the modern era.
