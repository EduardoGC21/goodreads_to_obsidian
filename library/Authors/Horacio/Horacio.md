## Biography
Horace (Quintus Horatius Flaccus) was a Roman poet who became one of the central literary figures of the Augustan age. Born in southern Italy to a freedman father who invested in his education, Horace studied in Rome and later in Athens, where he absorbed Greek philosophy and literature. He briefly served as an officer on the losing side in the civil war following Julius Caesar’s assassination, an experience that ended his political ambitions but helped shape his cautious, reflective view of life.

After returning to Rome, Horace secured a position as a minor treasury official and, more importantly, entered the literary circle around Maecenas, a powerful patron and adviser to Augustus. In this environment he produced refined poetry that combined Greek models with distinctly Roman concerns, writing about friendship, love, politics, and the pursuit of a balanced, modest life. His satires and epistles offered conversational moral reflection, while his odes—along with works like the “Carmen Saeculare” (secular hymn)—helped articulate the cultural ideals of the new regime. Horace’s nuanced stance toward power, his close relationship with Maecenas, and his mastery of Latin lyric secured his place as a foundational poet for later European literature.

Collections such as his satires, epistles, and poetic treatises on art, as well as his odes and epodes, have been read for centuries as models of style and moral insight. His phrases and themes—such as the call to seize the day and live within one’s means—became enduring touchstones in Western culture. Through a life lived at the intersection of political upheaval and literary innovation, Horace forged a body of work that continues to shape the way later writers think about poetry, philosophy, and public life.
