## Biography
Robert Morris Sapolsky (1957– ) is an American neuroscientist, primatologist, and writer whose work has reshaped understanding of stress, behavior, and moral responsibility. Born in Brooklyn, New York, to Soviet-immigrant parents and raised in an Orthodox Jewish household, he became fascinated with primates as a child, teaching himself Swahili and corresponding with primatologists. He studied biological anthropology at Harvard University, then completed a Ph.D. in neuroendocrinology at Rockefeller University, where he began the lifelong focus on how stress hormones affect the brain.

Sapolsky built a distinguished academic career at Stanford University, where he is the John A. and Cynthia Fry Gunn Professor of biology, neurology, and neurosurgery, while also serving as a research associate with the National Museums of Kenya. For decades he has split his time between laboratory work on neurons and fieldwork in East Africa, following wild baboon troops to understand how social hierarchies, personality, and environment shape health. His popular books, including “Behave: The Biology of Humans at Our Best and Worst” and “Determined: A Science of Life without Free Will,” synthesize neuroscience, psychology, primatology, and philosophy to argue for a deeply biological, largely deterministic view of human behavior.

Widely honored with awards such as a MacArthur “Genius Grant” and major science-writing prizes, Sapolsky is known for making complex science accessible while insisting on the ethical implications of biology for issues like crime, punishment, and compassion. He is married to neuropsychologist Lisa Sapolsky, with whom he has two children, and has written candidly about his own struggles with depression. Through his teaching, research, and public writing, he has become a leading voice in debates over how much control humans truly have over their actions and what that means for how societies treat one another.
