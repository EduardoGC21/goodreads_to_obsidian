## Biography
Robert M. Sapolsky (b. 1957) is an American neuroscientist, biologist, and author known for his integrative work on stress, behavior, and the biology of human and animal social life. Trained in biological anthropology and neuroendocrinology, he spent decades shuttling between Stanford laboratories and long-term fieldwork with wild baboons in East Africa, using this dual perspective to understand how hormones, brains, and environments interact over the lifespan. His research has been especially influential in explaining how chronic stress shapes health and behavior, and in challenging simplistic views of how biology and environment contribute to who we become.

Beyond his scientific publications, Sapolsky is widely recognized as a gifted communicator of complex ideas to general audiences, often blending rigorous science with humor and moral seriousness. In books such as “Behave: The Biology of Humans at Our Best and Worst,” he synthesizes findings from genetics, neuroscience, psychology, and social science to explore moral responsibility, aggression, empathy, and cooperation, while “Determined: A Science of Life without Free Will” extends his long-standing argument that behavior is fully caused by biological and environmental factors. As a teacher and public intellectual, he is known for his empathy toward human fallibility and for emphasizing the ethical implications of a deeply biological view of behavior, including how societies treat crime, mental illness, and difference.
