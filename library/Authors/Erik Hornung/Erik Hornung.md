## Biography
Erik Hornung (1933–2022) was a Latvian‑born German-Swiss Egyptologist whose work reshaped modern understanding of ancient Egyptian religion and funerary literature. Born in Riga on January 28, 1933, he left Latvia for Germany with his family in 1939, later studying Egyptology at the universities of Tübingen and Göttingen and earning his PhD in 1956. After research work in Cairo and an academic post in Münster, he became professor of Egyptology at the University of Basel in 1967, a position he held until his retirement in 1998, remaining closely associated with the Basel institute for the rest of his life.

Hornung became the leading authority on the Valley of the Kings and on New Kingdom royal funerary texts, producing definitive editions and studies of the Amduat, the Book of Gates, and related underworld books, as well as publications on tombs such as those of Horemheb, Ramses IV, Ramses VII, and Seti I. His influential synthesis “Der Eine und die Vielen” (in Spanish, “El uno y los múltiples”) and other works on Egyptian conceptions of the divine established him as one of the most important interpreters of Egyptian religion for both scholars and general readers. He was active in major exhibitions, editorial projects, and scholarly societies, and received international recognition, including membership in the German Archaeological Institute and the British Academy.

Beyond his scholarly achievements, Hornung was known for bridging academic Egyptology and wider intellectual currents, engaging with historians of religion, Jungian psychologists, and esoteric traditions interested in Egypt’s religious symbolism. He was married to Dorothea von Graevenitz and spent his later years in Basel, where he died on July 11, 2022. His work continues to shape how ancient Egyptian religious thought, kingship, and afterlife beliefs are studied and understood.
