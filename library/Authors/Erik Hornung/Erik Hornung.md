## Biography
Erik Hornung (1933–2022) was a Latvian‑born German‑Swiss Egyptologist whose work fundamentally reshaped modern understanding of ancient Egyptian religion and funerary thought. Born in Riga on January 28, 1933, he left Latvia for Germany in childhood and later studied Egyptology at the universities of Tübingen and Göttingen, completing his doctorate in 1956 and his habilitation in Münster in 1963. In 1967 he became professor of Egyptology at the University of Basel, where he taught and mentored generations of scholars until his retirement in 1998, remaining professor emeritus thereafter.

Hornung specialized in the royal tombs and funerary texts of the New Kingdom, particularly those in the Valley of the Kings. He produced authoritative editions of key underworld books such as the Amduat and published studies of tombs including those of Seti I and several Ramesside kings, becoming recognized as one of the foremost experts on the Theban necropolis and its religious imagery. His synthetic works—above all “Der Eine und die Vielen” (known in English as “Conceptions of God in Ancient Egypt: The One and the Many,” of which the Spanish translation “El Uno y los Múltiples” is a version)—offered a nuanced account of Egyptian notions of divinity and became standard references far beyond Egyptology. He engaged actively with broader intellectual currents, collaborating with Jungian psychologists and participating in major exhibitions and public outreach, which helped bring the symbolic and theological richness of ancient Egypt to a wide audience.

Over the course of his career Hornung received international recognition, including membership in the German Archaeological Institute and the British Academy, and he served in roles such as vice‑president of the Society of the Friends of the Royal Tombs of Egypt. He was married to Dorothea von Graevenitz, linking him to a prominent artistic family, and remained intellectually active well into retirement. Hornung died in Basel on July 11, 2022, leaving a lasting legacy as one of the leading interpreters of ancient Egyptian religion and afterlife beliefs.
