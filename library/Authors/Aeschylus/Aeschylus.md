## Biography
Aeschylus (c. 525/524–456/455 BCE) was one of the earliest and most influential tragedians of ancient Greece, often called the “father of tragedy.” Born in Eleusis, near Athens, he lived during the formative years of Athenian democracy and fought in the Persian Wars, including the battles of Marathon and possibly Salamis. These experiences of conflict, destiny, and civic duty deeply shaped the themes of his plays, which frequently explore the tension between human action and divine will, the evolution from personal vengeance to communal justice, and the moral responsibilities of rulers and citizens.

Aeschylus is credited with important innovations in theatrical form, such as expanding the number of actors on stage and developing more complex dialogue and dramatic structure, helping to transform simple choral performances into fully realized drama. His surviving works, including the trilogy "Oresteia," are central to the Western canon and influenced later tragedians like Sophocles and Euripides, as well as centuries of literature and philosophy. Revered in his own time and honored after his death, Aeschylus stands as a foundational figure whose tragedies shaped not only Greek theatre but the broader understanding of justice, fate, and the human condition.
