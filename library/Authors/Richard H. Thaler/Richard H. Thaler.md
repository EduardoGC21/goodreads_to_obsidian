## Biography
Richard H. Thaler (born 1945) is an American economist best known as one of the founders of behavioral economics and behavioral finance. Raised in East Orange, New Jersey, he studied at Case Western Reserve University (B.A., 1967) and the University of Rochester (M.A., 1970; Ph.D., 1974), where he began challenging the assumption that people are fully rational, self-controlled decision makers. His early academic career took him through positions at the University of Rochester and Cornell University, and a formative research year at Stanford University, where collaboration with psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky helped him connect observed economic “anomalies” with insights from cognitive psychology.

In 1995 Thaler joined the University of Chicago Booth School of Business, where he became the Charles R. Walgreen Distinguished Service Professor of Behavioral Science and Economics and directed the Center for Decision Research. His work on limited rationality, mental accounting, fairness, and self-control reshaped mainstream economics and influenced real-world policy design in areas such as retirement savings, health care, and consumer protection. The popularization of these ideas reached a wide audience through books such as “Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness” (with Cass R. Sunstein) and his intellectual memoir “Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics,” which trace how his research agenda helped move behavioral economics from the margins of the discipline to its center.

Thaler’s contributions have been recognized with numerous honors, most notably the 2017 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for his work incorporating psychologically realistic assumptions into economic analysis. Beyond academia, he co-founded Fuller & Thaler Asset Management and advised governments on the creation of “nudge units” that apply behavioral insights to public policy. He has three children from a previous marriage and is married to France Leclerc, a former marketing professor and photographer, reflecting a personal life intertwined with academic and professional collaboration.
