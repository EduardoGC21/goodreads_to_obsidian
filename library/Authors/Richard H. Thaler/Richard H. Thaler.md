## Biography
Richard H. Thaler (born 1945) is an American economist widely regarded as one of the founders of behavioral economics, a field that integrates insights from psychology into economic theory to better explain how people actually make decisions. Educated at Case Western Reserve University and the University of Rochester, he went on to teach at the University of Rochester, Cornell University, and ultimately the University of Chicago Booth School of Business, where he spent much of his career. Thaler’s work challenged the traditional view of perfectly rational agents, documenting the many ways in which human judgment departs from standard economic assumptions.

Thaler became particularly influential through his concepts of mental accounting, fairness, and self-control problems, as well as his advocacy of “nudges” – subtle changes in choice architecture that help individuals make better decisions without restricting their freedom. These ideas reached a broad audience through his co-authored book “Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness” and his more personal intellectual memoir “Misbehaving: The Making of Behavioral Economics.” His research has informed public policy worldwide, inspiring the creation of governmental “nudge units” and reshaping approaches to retirement savings, health behavior, and consumer protection. In recognition of his contributions, Thaler received the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences, solidifying his status as a central figure in modern economic thought.

Throughout his career, Thaler has collaborated with leading scholars such as Daniel Kahneman and Cass R. Sunstein, helping connect economics to psychology, law, and public policy. Known for his accessible writing style and willingness to confront mainstream assumptions, he has played a key role in making behavioral economics both academically rigorous and practically relevant. His work continues to influence how scholars, policymakers, and businesses think about human behavior, decision-making, and the design of institutions and markets.
