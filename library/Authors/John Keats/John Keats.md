## Biography
John Keats (1795–1821) was an English Romantic poet whose brief life produced some of the most influential and lyrically intense poetry in the language. Born in London to a stable-keeper and raised in modest circumstances, he was orphaned as a teenager and initially trained as a surgeon-apothecary. Despite a promising medical career, Keats abandoned medicine in favor of poetry, dedicating himself to writing within the vibrant but often fractious world of early 19th-century English letters.

Keats’s work is marked by rich, sensuous imagery and philosophical reflection on beauty, transience, and mortality. Living under the shadow of illness—both his own and that of his brother Tom, who died of tuberculosis—he developed a poetics that confronted suffering without losing a sense of wonder. Collections such as “Endymion” and, later, gathered volumes like “The Complete Poems” showcase his exploration of myth, nature, and emotional depth, often in dialogue with contemporaries like Leigh Hunt and, more distantly, Wordsworth and Shelley. Keats’s final years were spent in a passionate but frustrated love for Fanny Brawne and in declining health; he died of tuberculosis in Rome at just twenty-five. Posthumously, his reputation grew enormously, and he is now regarded as one of the central figures of English Romanticism, an emblem of artistic genius cut short by an early death.
