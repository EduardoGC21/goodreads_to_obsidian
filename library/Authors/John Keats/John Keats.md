## Biography
John Keats (1795–1821) was an English Romantic poet whose work, though produced over a brief career, became central to the canon of English literature. Born in London to a family of modest means, he was orphaned as a teenager and initially trained as a surgeon-apothecary. Drawn increasingly to poetry, he abandoned medicine to devote himself to writing, despite financial insecurity, ill health, and the disapproval of some guardians. Keats moved within the circle of younger Romantics and was deeply influenced by classical mythology, Shakespeare, and the sensory richness of the natural world.

During his short life, Keats developed a highly distinctive voice marked by vivid imagery, emotional intensity, and philosophical reflection on beauty, transience, and mortality. Early works like "Endymion" revealed both his ambition and the unevenness of a developing talent, attracting harsh criticism at first, yet they paved the way for the extraordinary sequence of odes and later poems that secured his reputation. His passionate, complicated love for Fanny Brawne, his close friendships with fellow writers, and his struggle with tuberculosis—ultimately leading to his death in Rome at just 25—fed the poignantly self-aware character of his poetry. Though he died believing himself a failure, Keats is now regarded as one of the most important and influential poets in the English language.
