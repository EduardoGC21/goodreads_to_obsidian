## Biography
John Milton (1608–1674) was an English poet, polemicist, and civil servant whose life was deeply entwined with the religious and political upheavals of seventeenth-century England. Born in London to a prosperous scrivener, he received a rigorous humanist education at St Paul’s School and Christ’s College, Cambridge, mastering Latin, Greek, and several modern languages. Early on he devoted himself to the ideal of the poet as a moral and intellectual guide, preparing for years through private study before attempting his greatest works.

Milton became an ardent supporter of the Puritan cause and the English Commonwealth, writing influential prose tracts defending freedom of the press, religious reform, and the execution of Charles I. He served as Latin Secretary to Oliver Cromwell’s government, drafting diplomatic correspondence in Latin. During this period he lost his sight, yet continued to work and later composed his major poetry entirely while blind, dictating to his daughters and amanuenses. His most famous work, the epic "Paradise Lost" (known in Spanish as "El paraíso perdido"), reshaped the literary imagination of the Fall of Man and secured his place as one of the central figures of English literature.

After the Restoration, Milton lived in relative obscurity and political danger, briefly imprisoned and then pardoned, but he persisted in exploring themes of liberty, conscience, and divine justice in later works such as "Paradise Regained" and "Samson Agonistes." His complex personal life—including troubled first marriage, later more harmonious unions, and his role as a demanding yet intellectually formative father—mirrored the tensions between authority and freedom that pervade his writings. Milton’s influence on later poets, political thinkers, and theologians has been immense, and he is widely regarded as one of the greatest English poets and a powerful voice for individual conscience and republican ideals.
