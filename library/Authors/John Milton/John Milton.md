## Biography
John Milton (1608–1674) was an English poet, polemicist, and civil servant whose life and work were deeply entwined with the religious and political upheavals of seventeenth-century England. Educated at St Paul’s School and Christ’s College, Cambridge, he immersed himself in classical literature and theology, developing the erudition and moral seriousness that would mark his later writings. After his studies, he spent years in private scholarship, mastering multiple languages and composing early poems that already showed his ambition to create a great Christian epic.

Milton played an active role in the English Revolution, writing influential prose tracts in defense of republican government, freedom of the press, and religious toleration, and later serving as Latin Secretary to Oliver Cromwell’s Council of State. Despite going completely blind by his early forties and suffering personal losses, he continued to dictate his major works, including the epic poem commonly known in English as "Paradise Lost," which reflects his profound engagement with questions of free will, obedience, and the nature of good and evil. His life of intellectual rigor, steadfast religious conviction, and political engagement made him one of the central figures of English literature and a key voice in the development of liberal political thought.
