## Biography
Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821–1881) was a Russian novelist and thinker whose intense, psychologically probing works reshaped modern literature. Born in Moscow to a military doctor and raised in a strict, religious household, he moved to St. Petersburg as a young man and trained as an engineer before turning to writing. His early success with “Poor Folk” brought him into radical intellectual circles, but in 1849 he was arrested for political discussion, subjected to a staged execution, and then sentenced to years of hard labor and exile in Siberia. This ordeal, combined with his lifelong epilepsy, deep religious searching, and experience of poverty and gambling addiction, gave his later fiction its distinctive blend of spiritual anguish, moral questioning, and compassion for society’s outcasts.

After returning from Siberia, Dostoevsky wrote a series of major novels that explore freedom, guilt, faith, and the struggle for meaning. Works such as “Notes from Underground,” “The Idiot,” “Demons” (often translated as “The Devils”), and his final masterpiece “The Brothers Karamazov” use intense interior monologues and conflicted characters to examine the limits of rationalism and the possibility of redemption through suffering and love. His second marriage to Anna Grigoryevna Snitkina, who served as his stenographer and later managed his finances, brought personal stability and helped him overcome crushing debts, allowing him to complete his greatest books. Dostoevsky’s influence has been profound, shaping psychology, philosophy, and theology as well as literature, and inspiring figures from Freud and Nietzsche to countless modern novelists.
