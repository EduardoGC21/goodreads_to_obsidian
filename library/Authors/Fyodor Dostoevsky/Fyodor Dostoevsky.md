## Biography
Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821–1881) was a Russian novelist, short story writer, and essayist whose work probed the deepest layers of human psychology, faith, and morality. Born in Moscow to a doctor working at a hospital for the poor, he grew up amid stories of suffering and hardship that left a lasting mark on his imagination. Early in his career he gained attention with socially conscious works about the urban poor, but he soon became involved with a circle of intellectuals critical of the autocracy, which led to his arrest in 1849, a staged mock-execution, and years of exile and hard labor in Siberia—an experience that reshaped his religious and philosophical outlook.

After his return from exile, Dostoevsky’s life was marked by financial struggle, a serious gambling addiction, and fragile health, yet these crises fed into the intensity of his writing. He married twice, and his second wife, Anna Grigoryevna, became a key emotional and practical support, helping to manage his debts and publish his works. In his later novels, including "Crime and Punishment," "The Idiot," "Demons" (often translated as "Devils"), and "The Brothers Karamazov," he explored themes of guilt, redemption, free will, and the clash between faith and rationalism, set against the ferment of 19th‑century Russian society. Today he is regarded as one of the central figures of world literature, whose psychologically rich characters and moral questioning influenced modern fiction, philosophy, and psychoanalysis.

Dostoevsky’s significance lies not only in his powerful depictions of individuals on the edge—criminals, visionaries, and spiritual seekers—but also in his nuanced portrayal of relationships: between parents and children, brothers, lovers, and spiritual mentors and disciples. His own experiences of humiliation, illness, and loss (including the deaths of several children) gave him a tragic sensibility and a compassionate understanding of human weakness. Through characters such as Prince Myshkin in "The Idiot" or the brothers of "The Brothers Karamazov," he tested the possibility of goodness and faith in a world haunted by suffering and doubt, leaving a body of work that continues to challenge and inspire readers and thinkers worldwide.
