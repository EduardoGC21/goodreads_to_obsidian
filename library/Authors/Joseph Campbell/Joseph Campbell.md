## Biography
Joseph Campbell (1904–1987) was an American mythologist, writer, and teacher whose comparative study of myths, religions, and symbols deeply influenced literary criticism, psychology, religious studies, and popular culture. Born in New York City to a middle-class Catholic family, he developed an early fascination with Native American lore and Arthurian legends, later broadening his interests to include Hinduism, Buddhism, and other world traditions. After studying at Columbia University and spending time in Europe, he undertook decades of wide-ranging reading and reflection, much of it in relative seclusion, which became the basis of his synthetic vision of myth as a shared human language of symbols and archetypes.

Campbell taught for many years at Sarah Lawrence College, where he was known as an inspiring, conversational lecturer who encouraged students to follow their own “bliss” as a guiding life principle. His seminal work, often known in Spanish as “El héroe de las mil caras,” articulated the “monomyth” or hero’s journey, a narrative pattern he argued recurs across cultures and eras, and which later shaped the storytelling of numerous writers and filmmakers. Collaborations and friendships with figures such as Jiddu Krishnamurti and, later, the widely viewed “The Power of Myth” interviews with Bill Moyers helped bring his ideas to a broad public. Though some scholars have critiqued his generalizations, Campbell remains a central, controversial, and highly influential figure in the modern understanding of myth and its role in individual and collective life.
