## Biography
Joseph Campbell (1904–1987) was an American mythologist, writer, and teacher whose work fundamentally reshaped modern understandings of mythology, religion, and narrative. Born in New York City to a middle‑class Catholic family, he developed an early fascination with Native American stories, later expanding his interests to the myths of ancient Greece, India, and the Far East. After studying at Columbia University, he spent formative years in Europe, where he absorbed the ideas of modernist writers, psychologists like Carl Jung, and comparative scholars, laying the foundation for his lifelong comparative approach to myth.

Campbell taught for decades at Sarah Lawrence College, where his broad, interdisciplinary lectures drew on literature, anthropology, psychology, and religious studies. His most influential work, often known in Spanish as "El héroe de las mil caras" ("The Hero with a Thousand Faces"), articulated the concept of the "monomyth" or hero’s journey—a recurring narrative pattern he believed underlay myths and stories worldwide. This idea deeply influenced later writers, filmmakers, and storytellers, especially in popular culture and Hollywood cinema.

Beyond his scholarly writing, Campbell became widely known through public television interviews that presented myth as a living guide to modern life, encouraging individuals to "follow their bliss" as a way of aligning personal experience with deeper symbolic patterns. His work, though sometimes criticized for overgeneralization, remains highly influential in fields ranging from religious studies to creative writing, shaping how many people think about stories, identity, and the role of myth in contemporary society.
