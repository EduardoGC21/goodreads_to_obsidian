## Biography
Wilhelm Robert Franz Gwinner (from 1908 Wilhelm von Gwinner) was a German jurist, man of letters, and intimate associate of the philosopher Arthur Schopenhauer. Born in Frankfurt am Main in 1825 into a respected bourgeois family, he received a classical education at the local Gymnasium before studying law at the universities of Jena and Heidelberg, where he joined the student Burschenschaft movement and completed a doctorate in jurisprudence. Returning to his native city, he practiced as an advocate and later entered the service of the Free City of Frankfurt, eventually serving as secretary of the appellate court and, in the 1860s, as a judge at the city court. His career combined legal service with a strong literary and philosophical interest.

Gwinner’s lasting significance lies in his close personal relationship with Schopenhauer and his role as the philosopher’s literary executor and first major biographer. After turning to the study of philosophy and theology in Tübingen and earning a second doctorate, Gwinner became part of Schopenhauer’s Frankfurt circle, gaining the philosopher’s trust to such an extent that he was appointed executor of his will. Drawing on years of personal acquaintance, private conversations, and access to papers and correspondence, Gwinner authored influential works such as “Arthur Schopenhauer aus persönlichen Umgange dargestellt” and later “Schopenhauers Leben,” which shaped early reception of Schopenhauer’s life and character. His writings, including commemorative addresses and polemical pieces defending Schopenhauer against critics, helped secure the philosopher’s posthumous reputation and documented his friendships and conflicts in detail.

Gwinner remained rooted in Frankfurt throughout his long life, witnessing the transformation of the city from a free city to part of the German Empire. Ennobled in 1908, he spent his later years as a respected elder statesman of Schopenhauer scholarship and of Frankfurt’s intellectual life. He died in Frankfurt in 1917 and was buried in the family grave at the city’s main cemetery, leaving behind a body of biographical and philosophical work that continues to be cited in studies of Schopenhauer and nineteenth‑century German intellectual history.
