## Biography
Johannes Grave (b. 1976) is a German art historian whose work has significantly shaped contemporary understanding of European painting from the Renaissance to Romanticism. Born in Thuine in the Emsland region, he studied art history, medieval Latin philology, medieval history, and philosophy at the University of Freiburg, including a formative study stay in Rome, and completed his doctorate at Friedrich Schiller University Jena. Early in his career he held research positions in Jena and at the University of Basel, where he later completed his habilitation, before serving as deputy director of the Deutsches Forum für Kunstgeschichte (German Center for Art History) in Paris.

Grave has held professorships at Bielefeld University and, subsequently, at Friedrich Schiller University Jena, where he is professor of modern art history. His research focuses on early Renaissance painting, art around 1800, image theory, and the temporality of viewing images. He is widely known for his studies of Caspar David Friedrich, including the major monograph "Caspar David Friedrich" and other works that situate the painter’s landscapes within broader intellectual, religious, and political contexts, as well as for influential books on Giovanni Bellini and on the theory of image reception. Through these publications, he has become one of the leading interpreters of Romantic landscape painting and its meanings.

Beyond his books, Grave has played an important role in the institutional and public life of art history in Germany. He has been closely involved with research centers and foundations in Weimar, has co-founded and edited Franco-German scholarly journals, and has served on boards and committees of professional associations and the German Research Foundation (DFG), eventually becoming a vice president of the DFG. As co-curator of major exhibitions such as "Caspar David Friedrich. Art for a New Age," he has helped bring new scholarly perspectives to a broad audience, reinforcing his standing as a central figure in current debates about how historical artworks are experienced and understood today.
