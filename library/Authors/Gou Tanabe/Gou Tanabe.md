## Biography
Gou Tanabe (1975– ) is a Japanese manga artist born in Tokyo, best known for his atmospheric adaptations of classic horror literature, especially the works of H. P. Lovecraft. After winning the Special Jury Prize at Kodansha’s Afternoon Shiki Awards in 2001 with his debut work “Sunakichi” and receiving an honorable mention at Enterbrain’s Entame Awards in 2002 for his adaptation of Maxim Gorky’s “Twenty-six Men and a Girl,” he gradually built a reputation as a specialist in dark, psychologically intense storytelling. Early in his career his editor introduced him to Lovecraft’s Cthulhu Mythos, a discovery that deeply influenced his artistic direction and led him to explore characters trapped in hopeless situations and confronted with incomprehensible cosmic forces.

From 2004 onward Tanabe dedicated much of his output to adapting Lovecraft, starting with “The Outsider” and later including major works such as “The Call of Cthulhu,” “The Shadow Over Innsmouth,” “The Colour Out of Space,” “At the Mountains of Madness” (including the two-volume edition known in English as “H.P. Lovecraft’s At the Mountains of Madness, Volume 1” and “Volume 2”), and several others. His meticulous linework, cinematic pacing, and faithful yet interpretive handling of the source material have earned him international acclaim, translations into many languages, and recognition from festivals and juries, including repeated selections at the Japan Media Arts Festival and nominations at Angoulême and the Eisner and Bram Stoker Awards. Within contemporary comics he is regarded as one of the foremost visual interpreters of Lovecraftian horror, bridging Western weird fiction and Japanese manga aesthetics for a global readership.

Although Tanabe tends to maintain a low personal profile, his career shows a consistent commitment to exploring fear, alienation, and the limits of human understanding through sequential art. By focusing on classical literary sources and treating them with seriousness and craft, he has become an important figure in both horror and adaptation comics, demonstrating how manga can reframe early twentieth‑century weird tales for modern audiences while preserving their mood of dread and existential awe.
