## Biography
Heinz Insu Fenkl (b. 1960) is an American writer, translator, and folklorist whose work bridges Korean myth, Asian American experience, and contemporary literature. Born in South Korea to a German father and Korean mother, he spent his childhood in Korea before moving to Germany and later the United States, growing up amid U.S. military communities and postwar Korean society. These cross-cultural, often liminal experiences deeply inform his autobiographical fiction and his scholarship on myth, shamanism, and narrative folklore.

Fenkl studied English at Vassar College and completed an M.A. in English and Creative Writing at the University of California, Davis, later pursuing doctoral work in cultural anthropology with a focus on shamanism and East Asian folklore. A longtime professor of English at the State University of New York at New Paltz, he has taught creative writing as well as courses in Asian and Asian American literature and film, and has helped shape the field through program leadership and editorial work on journals devoted to Korean literature and poetics. He is internationally recognized for collecting and translating Korean folktales and myths, and for his translations of contemporary Korean fiction and classical Buddhist texts.

As an author, Fenkl first gained wide attention with the autobiographical novel "Memories of My Ghost Brother," a PEN/Hemingway Award finalist, and later with the multi-stranded novel "Skull Water." His work, including collections of Korean folktales and studies such as "The Korean Myths: A Guide to the Gods, Heroes and Legends," has been influential in bringing Korean narrative traditions and the experiences of mixed-heritage Koreans to anglophone readers. He lives in New York’s Hudson Valley with his family, continuing to write, translate, and mentor new generations of writers and scholars.
