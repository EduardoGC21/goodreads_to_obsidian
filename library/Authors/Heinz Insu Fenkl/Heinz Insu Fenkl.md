## Biography
Heinz Insu Fenkl (born 1960) is a Korean American novelist, translator, folklorist, and scholar whose work bridges Korean mythic traditions and contemporary transnational experience. Born in Bupyeong, South Korea, to a German father and Korean mother, he spent his childhood in Korea before emigrating at twelve and later living in Germany and the United States. This mixed and mobile upbringing—among U.S. military communities, Korean neighborhoods, and European schools—deeply shaped his autobiographical fiction and his interest in stories of displacement, war’s aftermath, and the lives of those at cultural margins.

Fenkl studied English and creative writing at Vassar College and the University of California, Davis, and conducted Fulbright-funded research in Korea on shamanism, narrative folklore, and literary translation. As an academic, he has taught creative writing and Asian and Asian American literature at institutions including Vassar, Bard, Sarah Lawrence, Eastern Michigan, Yonsei University, and, most prominently, the State University of New York at New Paltz, where he is a professor of English and has helped build programs in creative writing and interstitial studies. Internationally recognized for his collections of Korean folktales and his translations of works such as the classic Buddhist novel “The Nine Cloud Dream,” he brings Korean narrative traditions to Anglophone readers while mentoring new generations of writers.

Across novels like “Memories of My Ghost Brother” and later work on Korean myths and legends, including “The Korean Myths: A Guide to the Gods, Heroes and Legends,” Fenkl explores themes of family, memory, mixed-race identity, and the enduring presence of myth in everyday life. His long engagement with Korean and Korean American literature, North Korean comics and fiction, and Buddhist and shamanic traditions has made him an important cultural mediator between Korea and the West. He lives in New York’s Hudson Valley with his family, continuing to write, translate, and teach.
