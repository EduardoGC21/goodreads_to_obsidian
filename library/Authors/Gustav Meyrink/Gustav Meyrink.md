## Biography
Gustav Meyrink (1868–1932), born Gustav Meyer in Vienna, was an Austrian writer and translator whose work fused urban realism with mysticism, satire, and occult philosophy. The illegitimate son of a government minister and an actress, he grew up on the margins of respectable society, an experience that sharpened his sense of social hypocrisy and outsiderhood. As a young man he worked in banking in Prague, where a personal crisis—often retold as a near-suicide interrupted by a chance encounter with a spiritual pamphlet—pushed him toward the study of theosophy, Kabbalah, Eastern philosophies, and esoteric traditions. Accusations of financial misconduct, from which he was ultimately cleared but not socially rehabilitated, ended his business career and forced him to live by his pen.

Meyrink became a central figure in early 20th‑century German‑language fantastic literature. His novel "Der Golem" ("The Golem"), set in the haunted atmosphere of Prague’s Jewish quarter, turned the legendary clay figure into a symbol of collective guilt, spiritual imprisonment, and the layered history of Central Europe, and it secured his international reputation. Over the following years he wrote further visionary novels and numerous short stories, often exploring themes of spiritual awakening, the illusory nature of everyday reality, and the possibility of inner transformation. He married and had children, spending his later life in Bavaria near Lake Starnberg, where he continued his literary and esoteric pursuits until his death in 1932. His work remains significant for its unique blend of grotesque humor, social critique, and metaphysical depth, influencing later horror, magical realist, and occult fiction.
