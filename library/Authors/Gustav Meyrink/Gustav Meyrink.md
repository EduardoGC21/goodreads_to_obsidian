## Biography
Gustav Meyrink (born Gustav Meyer, 1868–1932) was an Austrian writer, translator, and former banker whose work became a cornerstone of German‑language supernatural fiction. Born in Vienna as the illegitimate son of a Württemberg baron and an actress, he spent his youth between Munich, Hamburg, and Prague, where he co‑founded a private bank. A personal crisis in the early 1890s, when he reportedly contemplated suicide before being diverted by a pamphlet on life after death, led him away from conventional bourgeois ambitions and into a lifelong engagement with mysticism, theosophy, and Eastern religious thought.

After a fraud investigation abruptly ended his banking career, Meyrink reinvented himself as a satirist and storyteller, publishing sharp, anti‑bourgeois short fiction in magazines and later in collections. He married Philomene Bernt and settled eventually in Starnberg, Bavaria, supporting his family through an intense period of work as a literary translator and editor of occult texts, while at the same time developing the visionary fiction that made his name. His Prague years and his interest in Kabbalah, the occult, and esoteric philosophies all fed into novels such as “The Golem,” whose claustrophobic, dreamlike vision of the city helped secure his reputation.

Meyrink’s work placed him at the crossroads of Central European modernism, in dialogue—sometimes implicitly—with contemporaries like Franz Kafka, while maintaining a distinct mixture of social satire, metaphysical inquiry, and macabre fantasy. Opposed to the nationalism and militarism of his era, he attracted criticism from völkisch circles even as his books reached a wide readership. In his final years in Starnberg, he continued to write, translate, and pursue esoteric practices, formally adopting Mahayana Buddhism in the late 1920s. He died in 1932 in Starnberg, leaving behind a body of fiction that remains influential for its blend of occult imagination, psychological intensity, and darkly ironic scrutiny of Central European society.
