## Biography
Marc Van de Mieroop (1956– ) is a Belgian-born Assyriologist and Egyptologist whose work has reshaped the study of the ancient Near East and ancient Egypt. Born in Belgium into a prominent Flemish family, he took his BA at the Katholieke Universiteit Leuven before moving to the United States to earn his MA (1980) and PhD (1983) in Near Eastern studies at Yale University. He went on to teach at Yale and the University of Oxford and, from 1996, has served as professor of Ancient Near Eastern history at Columbia University, where he has also directed the Center for the Ancient Mediterranean and helped shape generations of specialists in the field.

Van de Mieroop is best known for his synthetic overviews of Mesopotamian, Near Eastern, and Egyptian history, including widely used surveys such as his history of the ancient Near East and his history of ancient Egypt, which present complex political, social, and intellectual developments in an accessible yet rigorous form. His scholarship ranges from urban and economic history to intellectual and literary traditions, culminating in influential studies of Babylonian thought and the role of writing, such as his work on “philosophy before the Greeks” and on writing as resistance in ancient empires. Awarded a Guggenheim Fellowship for his research, he has also played a central role in international scholarly networks and editorial projects, helping to connect Near Eastern history with broader debates in world history and historiography.

Across his career, Van de Mieroop has combined close engagement with cuneiform and other primary sources with a sustained interest in historical method, making him a key bridge between philology and historical interpretation. Through his teaching, his many articles and books in several languages, and his participation in editorial boards and research centers, he has significantly influenced how scholars and students understand the civilizations of Mesopotamia, the Levant, and Egypt, and their place in global history.
