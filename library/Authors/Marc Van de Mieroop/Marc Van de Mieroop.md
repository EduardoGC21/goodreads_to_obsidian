## Biography
Marc Van De Mieroop (born 1956) is a Belgian historian, Assyriologist, and Egyptologist whose work has helped shape modern understanding of the ancient Near East and Egypt. Raised in Belgium, he completed his early studies at the Katholieke Universiteit Leuven before moving to the United States to earn his M.A. and Ph.D. in history at Yale University. His training placed him at the intersection of philology, archaeology, and social history, giving him a broad foundation for interpreting cuneiform and other ancient sources.

After appointments at Yale and the University of Oxford, Van De Mieroop joined Columbia University, where he has long served as professor of Ancient Near Eastern history and as a central figure in the university’s community of ancient world scholars. He has directed Columbia’s Center for the Ancient Mediterranean and played a leading role in the creation and editorship of the Journal of Ancient Near Eastern History, helping to foster dialogue between specialists in Mesopotamian, Egyptian, and broader Mediterranean studies. His teaching and supervision of students have contributed to the training of a generation of historians of the ancient world.

Van De Mieroop is best known for a series of influential syntheses and monographs on Mesopotamian cities, political and socio-economic structures, and historical methodology, as well as accessible surveys such as his histories of the ancient Near East and of ancient Egypt. Books like “A History of Ancient Egypt” and his work on the ancient Near East have made complex scholarly debates available to wider audiences while remaining grounded in primary sources. He has also explored the intellectual history of Babylonia and its connections to Greek and biblical traditions, and has been recognized with honors such as a Guggenheim Fellowship for his contributions to the field.
