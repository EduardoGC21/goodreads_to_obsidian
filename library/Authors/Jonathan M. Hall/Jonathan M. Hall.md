## Biography
Jonathan M. Hall is a historian of ancient Greece whose work has reshaped how scholars understand Greek identity, community, and the formation of the polis. Trained as a classicist and historian, he has focused on the cultural and social history of the Greek world, especially questions of ethnicity, self-definition, and the relationship between texts, material culture, and historical interpretation. Hall has taught for many years at the University of Chicago, where he has held senior positions in the Departments of History and Classics and in the College, mentoring generations of students in Greek history and historiography.

Hall’s scholarship ranges from studies of ethnic identity in Greek antiquity to broader syntheses of archaic and classical Greek history. His books, including works on Greek ethnicity, the emergence of “Hellenicity,” and the interaction between archaeology and historical writing, have become standard points of reference for the field. A History of the Archaic Greek World, ca. 1200–479 BCE exemplifies his approach: combining close engagement with archaeological and textual evidence, challenging traditional periodizations, and inviting readers to think critically about how historical narratives are constructed. Recognized both for his research and his classroom teaching, Hall occupies a prominent place among contemporary historians of ancient Greece.
