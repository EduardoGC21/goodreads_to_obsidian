## Biography
Alexandre Dumas (1802–1870) was a French novelist and playwright whose adventurous life and prolific output made him one of the most widely read authors of the 19th century. Born in Villers-Cotterêts, he was the son of General Thomas-Alexandre Dumas, a celebrated French Revolutionary officer of mixed African and French descent, and Marie-Louise Labouret. Dumas grew up in modest circumstances after his father’s early death, moving to Paris as a young man, where he first gained fame as a dramatist before turning to the historical novel. His engaging storytelling, strong sense of drama, and gift for character made his works immensely popular across Europe and beyond in his own lifetime.

Dumas’s greatest achievements are his historical adventure novels, particularly “The Three Musketeers” and “The Count of Monte Cristo,” which combined meticulous historical settings with vivid characters and themes of loyalty, justice, and revenge. He often collaborated with assistants and researchers but shaped the narrative voice and grand design of his books, helping to define the modern serial novel and mass-market fiction. Despite periods of financial excess and political involvement—including support for liberal causes and time spent in exile—he remained a central figure in French cultural life. His mixed-race heritage, international success, and lasting influence on literature and popular culture have made him an enduring symbol of both the breadth of French identity and the power of accessible, compelling storytelling.
