## Biography
Alexandre Dumas (1802–1870) was a French novelist and playwright whose adventurous historical romances made him one of the most widely read authors of the 19th century. Born in Villers-Cotterêts, France, to a French mother and a father of mixed French and Afro-Caribbean descent who had served as a general in Napoleon’s army, Dumas grew up with a strong awareness of both social ambition and social exclusion. Moving to Paris as a young man, he first found success writing for the stage before turning to the serialized novel format that would make him internationally famous.

Dumas’s major works, including "The Count of Monte Cristo" and "The Three Musketeers," combined meticulous historical settings with fast-paced plots and vivid, larger-than-life characters. He often collaborated with researchers and assistants to sustain his extraordinary productivity, but his name and narrative imagination defined the works and cemented his literary reputation. Financially extravagant and intensely sociable, he moved in artistic and political circles, had numerous love affairs, and maintained complex relationships with his children, including his son Alexandre Dumas fils, who also became a prominent writer. Despite periods of bankruptcy and political exile, Dumas remained a central figure in French cultural life, and his novels continue to shape global popular imagination about heroism, justice, and friendship.
