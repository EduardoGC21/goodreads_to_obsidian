## Biography
Frank Herbert (1920–1986) was an American science fiction author best known for creating the Dune saga, a landmark in speculative fiction. Born in Tacoma, Washington, he worked variously as a journalist, photographer, and editor before fully establishing himself as a novelist. His broad interests in ecology, politics, religion, psychology, and anthropology deeply informed his writing style and thematic concerns.

Herbert’s breakthrough came with "Dune" (1965), later followed by "Dune Messiah" and "Children of Dune," which together helped redefine science fiction as a serious literary form. Set against a backdrop of interstellar empires, religious movements, and ecological struggle, these novels interrogate the dangers of charismatic leadership, resource dependency, and imperial power. Herbert’s intricate worldbuilding and philosophical depth exerted a major influence on subsequent science fiction authors, filmmakers, and thinkers.

Throughout his life, Herbert collaborated closely with his family, particularly his son Brian Herbert, who later continued the Dune universe with co-authors. Herbert spent his later years writing, lecturing, and exploring themes of human potential and systems of power. By the time of his death in 1986, he had secured a reputation as one of the most significant and intellectually ambitious writers in modern science fiction.
