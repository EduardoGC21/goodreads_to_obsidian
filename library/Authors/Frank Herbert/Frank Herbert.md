## Biography
Frank Herbert (1920–1986) was an American science fiction author best known for creating the Dune universe, one of the most influential works in speculative fiction. Born in Tacoma, Washington, he worked a variety of jobs—including photographer, journalist, and editor—before turning to fiction full time. His background in reporting, combined with a strong interest in ecology, politics, and religion, deeply informed his writing, which often explores how environments shape societies and how power operates over long spans of time.

Herbert’s 1965 novel "Dune" and its sequels, such as "Dune Messiah" and "Children of Dune," established him as a central figure in 20th‑century science fiction. These works are notable for their intricate world‑building, complex political and religious systems, and attention to ecological and cultural detail rather than straightforward heroism. Herbert’s relationships and collaborations within his family, particularly with his son Brian Herbert, helped preserve and extend the Dune legacy after his death. His influence can be seen across modern science fiction literature and film, especially in how creators handle grand political sagas, environmental themes, and morally ambiguous leaders.
