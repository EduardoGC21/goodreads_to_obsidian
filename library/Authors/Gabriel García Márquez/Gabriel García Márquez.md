## Biography
Gabriel García Márquez (1927–2014) was a Colombian novelist, journalist, and screenwriter widely regarded as one of the most influential writers of the 20th century. Born in Aracataca, a small town on Colombia’s Caribbean coast, he was raised largely by his grandparents, whose storytelling, superstitions, and memories of civil wars deeply shaped his imagination. He began his career as a journalist, working across Latin America and Europe, and his sharp eye for political injustice and everyday absurdity informed both his fiction and non-fiction.

García Márquez became a central figure of the Latin American literary “Boom” and a master of magical realism, a style that blends the fantastical with the ordinary. "Cien años de soledad" (One Hundred Years of Solitude) established him as a global literary force, using the mythical town of Macondo to explore themes of memory, solitude, power, and the cyclical nature of history in Latin America. Awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1982, he was celebrated not only for his imaginative prose but also for the way his work illuminated the lives, struggles, and dreams of people in his region. Throughout his life he maintained friendships with major political and cultural figures, including Fidel Castro, and his blend of artistic innovation and political engagement made him an enduring symbol of Latin American letters.

In his later years, García Márquez divided his time between Mexico City, Cartagena, and Havana, continuing to write novels, memoirs, and essays while his earlier works gained ever wider readership. His personal life was marked by a long and stable marriage to Mercedes Barcha and close relationships with fellow writers and journalists who saw him as both mentor and colleague. Even after his death in 2014, his influence persists in writers across the world who draw on his techniques, themes, and vision of a reality permeated by history, myth, and wonder.
