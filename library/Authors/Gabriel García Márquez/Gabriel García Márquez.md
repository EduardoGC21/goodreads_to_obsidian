## Biography
Gabriel García Márquez (1927–2014) was a Colombian novelist, journalist, and screenwriter, widely regarded as one of the most important authors of the 20th century. Born in Aracataca, a small town on Colombia’s Caribbean coast, he was raised largely by his maternal grandparents, whose storytelling, political convictions, and belief in omens and myths profoundly shaped his imagination. These formative years later inspired the atmosphere, characters, and family histories that permeate his fiction.

As a leading figure of the Latin American literary "boom," García Márquez helped bring international attention to Latin American literature through his masterful blend of the everyday and the fantastical, a style known as magical realism. His novel "Cien años de soledad" (One Hundred Years of Solitude) became a landmark of world literature, exploring themes of memory, power, violence, and the cyclical nature of history through the multi-generational Buendía family. In 1982 he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature for his novels and short stories, which were praised for combining the marvelous and the real in a richly composed world.

Beyond his literary achievements, García Márquez had deep ties to journalism and politics, working as a reporter and columnist and maintaining close friendships with various political leaders, notably in Latin America and the Caribbean. He lived in several countries, including Mexico and Spain, while remaining closely linked to Colombia and its social and political realities. His work and public life made him an emblematic cultural figure whose influence extended well beyond literature, shaping how Latin America is imagined and understood around the world.
