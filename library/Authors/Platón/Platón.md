## Biography
Plato (c. 427–347 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher from an aristocratic Athenian family, traditionally regarded as one of the founders of Western philosophy. A student of Socrates and later the teacher of Aristotle, he lived through the Peloponnesian War and the political turmoil of classical Athens, experiences that deeply shaped his concerns about justice, virtue, and the ideal form of government. After Socrates’ trial and execution, Plato became disillusioned with Athenian democracy and began developing his own systematic philosophical vision.

He founded the Academy in Athens, often considered the first institution of higher learning in the Western world, where he taught and wrote for much of his life. Plato expressed his ideas mainly through dialogues, in which Socrates frequently appears as a central character. In these works he explored metaphysics (the theory of Forms), ethics, epistemology, politics, and the nature of the soul, crafting a unified vision that sought to connect moral character, knowledge, and the structure of reality.

Plato’s influence has been enormous and enduring: his dialogues have shaped philosophical, theological, and educational traditions for over two millennia, and they remain central texts in the study of philosophy. Collections of his dialogues, such as those often simply titled “Dialogues,” continue to serve as a primary gateway into his thought and into the classical Greek intellectual world.
