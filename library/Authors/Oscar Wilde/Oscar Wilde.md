## Biography
Oscar Wilde (1854–1900) was an Irish playwright, novelist, essayist, and poet whose wit, flamboyant personality, and sharp social criticism made him one of the most celebrated literary figures of the late Victorian era. Born in Dublin to a prominent intellectual family, he studied at Trinity College Dublin and later at Oxford, where he excelled academically and began cultivating the aesthetic ideals that would define his work and public persona. Moving to London, he became famous both for his writing and for his dazzling conversational skill, moving in fashionable circles and cultivating an image of deliberate eccentricity.

Wilde’s literary achievements include his only novel, "The Picture of Dorian Gray," a provocative exploration of aestheticism, morality, and duplicity, as well as a series of highly successful comedies of manners such as "The Importance of Being Earnest" and "An Ideal Husband." These plays satirized the hypocrisy and pretensions of upper-class English society while showcasing his gift for paradox and epigram. His personal life, particularly his intense relationship with Lord Alfred Douglas, brought him into direct conflict with the era’s harsh laws against homosexuality; a widely publicized trial for “gross indecency” led to his conviction and imprisonment.

Following his release from prison, Wilde lived in exile in France under straitened circumstances and declining health, publishing little and adopting the name Sebastian Melmoth. He died in Paris in 1900, largely estranged from the world that had once celebrated him. Despite his tragic end, Wilde’s legacy is that of a pioneering figure in queer history, a master of English comedy, and a critic of Victorian moralism whose works remain central to discussions of art, identity, and social conformity.
