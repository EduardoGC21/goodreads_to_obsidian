## Biography
Oscar Wilde (1854–1900) was an Irish playwright, novelist, essayist, and poet, renowned for his wit, flamboyant personality, and incisive social commentary. Born in Dublin to a prominent intellectual family, he was educated at Trinity College Dublin and later at Oxford, where he distinguished himself academically and began cultivating the aesthetic ideals that would shape his work. After moving to London, Wilde became a central figure in the late Victorian cultural scene, celebrated for his sparkling conversation and paradoxical aphorisms as much as for his writing. His only novel, "The Picture of Dorian Gray," scandalized some contemporary readers with its exploration of beauty, moral corruption, and hedonism, and it remains one of the key works of English decadent literature.

Wilde achieved his greatest popular success in the 1890s with a series of comedies of manners—including "Lady Windermere’s Fan," "An Ideal Husband," and "The Importance of Being Earnest"—which satirized the hypocrisies and pretensions of the British upper classes. His life was dramatically shaped by his relationship with Lord Alfred Douglas and the resulting conflict with Douglas’s father, the Marquess of Queensberry. Wilde’s decision to sue Queensberry for libel led to highly publicized trials that exposed his same-sex relationships; he was convicted of “gross indecency” and sentenced to two years of hard labor, which ruined his health and reputation. After his release, he lived in exile in France, writing "The Ballad of Reading Gaol" before dying in relative obscurity in Paris in 1900. Today, Wilde is recognized as one of the most important voices of the fin de siècle, admired for his literary artistry, his challenge to conventional morality, and his role as a queer icon in modern cultural history.
