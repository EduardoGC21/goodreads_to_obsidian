## Biography
Peter John Rhodes (1940–2021), usually cited as P. J. Rhodes, was a British historian of ancient Greece and one of the leading authorities on classical Greek politics and institutions. Educated at Queen Elizabeth’s School, Barnet, and then at Wadham College, Oxford, he took first-class degrees in classics and completed a doctoral thesis on the Athenian boule, supervised by David Lewis and G. E. M. de Ste. Croix. In 1965 he joined the University of Durham, where he spent his entire academic career, rising from lecturer to Professor of Ancient History in 1983 and later becoming Honorary and Emeritus Professor.

Rhodes’s scholarship focused on the political history and institutions of the Greek world, especially democratic Athens, and he became widely known for major works such as *The Athenian Boule*, his commentary on Aristotle’s *Athenaion Politeia*, and collaborative volumes on Greek historical inscriptions. His book *A History of the Classical Greek World, 478–323 BC* synthesized decades of research into a lucid survey of the period between the Persian Wars and the death of Alexander, and it helped introduce students and general readers to the complexity of Greek interstate politics, empire, and democracy. Over his career he produced dozens of books and well over a hundred articles and reviews, contributing to reference works including the *Cambridge Ancient History* and the *Oxford Classical Dictionary* and helping to shape modern understanding of Greek democracy and epigraphy.

Rhodes was deeply embedded in the international community of classicists. He held visiting fellowships at Oxford colleges and universities abroad, served as a consultant to research centres such as the Copenhagen Polis Centre, and sat on editorial and advisory boards for journals across Europe and North America. Elected a Fellow of the British Academy in 1987 and later a foreign member of the Royal Danish Academy, he was also President of the Classical Association (2014–2015) and received Durham University’s Chancellor’s Medal in 2015. Colleagues and students remembered him as a generous mentor and meticulous scholar; he lived and worked in Durham for most of his life and died there in 2021, leaving a body of work that remains foundational for the study of ancient Greek political history.
