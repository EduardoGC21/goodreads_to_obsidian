## Biography
Juan Miguel de Mora Vaquerizo (1921–2017) was a Mexican writer, journalist, dramatist, filmmaker, and indologist whose life moved between front‑line history and rigorous scholarship. Born in 1921, he spent part of his youth studying in Paris before joining the International Brigades in the Spanish Civil War as a teenager, where he participated in the Battle of the Ebro and was wounded in combat. After the Republican defeat he went through exile in France and then returned to Mexico, where he began a long career in journalism, fiction, theater, and essay writing, often focusing on social injustice, political conflict, and the lived experience of the 20th century in Mexico and beyond.

Back in Mexico, de Mora combined his work as a reporter—covering, among other events, the Vietnam War as one of the first Spanish‑speaking correspondents—with advanced studies in Hispanic letters and then Sanskrit and Indian philosophy. He became a recognized specialist in Sanskrit literature, teaching at the National Autonomous University of Mexico and other institutions, and producing influential translations and studies that opened classical Indian texts to Spanish‑speaking readers. His work on the Rig Veda, in which he translated and analyzed over a hundred hymns, exemplifies his effort to interpret ancient religious and philosophical ideas in a contemporary, critical light.

Throughout his life, Juan Miguel de Mora received national and international distinctions for both his artistic and scholarly output. He wrote novels, plays, and essays that examined guerrilla movements, authoritarianism, and the struggles of ordinary people, while also directing theater and film and maintaining a prolific career as a cultural critic. Married to the writer and translator Ludwika Jarocka, with whom he collaborated on several books, he is remembered as a bridge between Mexico and Asia, between political engagement and erudition, and between lived historical experience and reflective literary creation.
