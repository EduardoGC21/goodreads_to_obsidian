## Biography
Juan Miguel de Mora Vaquerizo (1921–2017) was a Mexican indologist, writer, journalist, and translator whose life bridged political struggle and rigorous scholarship. Born in Mexico City to a Mexican father and Spanish mother, he studied in Tabasco and later in Paris before joining the International Brigades in Spain in 1936, where he fought in the Spanish Civil War and participated in the Battle of the Ebro. After the Republican defeat he spent time in France and then returned to Mexico, beginning a prolific career in journalism and literature while completing advanced studies in Hispanic letters in Mexico and Havana, where he earned a doctorate in 1951 with a thesis on Lope de Vega.

De Mora became one of the key mediators of Indian classical thought for the Spanish-speaking world. After discovering India in the 1950s, he undertook postdoctoral studies in Sanskrit literature in Paris and went on to translate major Sanskrit works directly into Spanish, including substantial selections of the Rig Veda, several Upanishads, and the drama "Uttaramacarita." His Spanish Rig Veda—prepared with the collaboration of his wife, the writer Marja Ludwika Jarocka—combined extensive translation with analytical essays that explored the social, religious, and philosophical world of Vedic India, making the text accessible to a wide non-specialist readership. Beyond indology, he wrote novels, plays, and essays on Mexican politics, guerrilla movements, and contemporary history, and his theater work "Plaza de las Tres Culturas" reflected his engagement with the 1968 Tlatelolco massacre.

As a journalist, de Mora founded and directed regional newspapers in Tabasco, reported for major Mexican outlets, and served as one of the first Spanish-speaking correspondents to witness the Vietnam War firsthand. His long career brought him numerous distinctions, including national theater awards, academic honors from European institutions, and recognition from Mexican and Spanish organizations for his cultural and political contributions. Throughout his life he remained committed to anti-fascist ideals and to illuminating, through both scholarship and fiction, the intersections of power, resistance, and culture across Mexico and the broader world.
