## Biography
Alexander Sergeyevich Pushkin (1799–1837) was a Russian poet, playwright, and novelist widely regarded as the founder of modern Russian literature. Born into a noble family in Moscow with both aristocratic and African ancestry through his great-grandfather Abram Gannibal, Pushkin received a cosmopolitan education and began publishing poetry as a teenager. His early works and political sympathies drew the suspicion of Tsarist authorities, resulting in periods of exile that nonetheless became fertile ground for his creativity.

Pushkin’s writing fused colloquial Russian speech with high literary style, shaping the language and themes of Russian literature for generations. His verse novel "Eugene Onegin" ("Eugenio Oneguin" in translation) is considered his masterpiece, blending social satire, psychological insight, and innovative narrative techniques. He also produced historical dramas, fairy tales, and prose narratives that explored Russian history, folklore, and the tensions of his contemporary society.

In his personal life, Pushkin’s passionate temperament and complicated social milieu often led to conflict. His marriage to Natalya Goncharova placed him at the center of court intrigue and gossip, culminating in a duel in 1837 with Georges d’Anthès, in which Pushkin was mortally wounded. Though he died at 37, his influence on later Russian authors—such as Gogol, Dostoevsky, Tolstoy, and Turgenev—was profound, and he remains a central figure in Russian cultural identity.
