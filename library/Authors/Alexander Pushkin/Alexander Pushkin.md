## Biography
Alexander Sergeyevich Pushkin (1799–1837) was a Russian poet, novelist, and playwright widely regarded as the founder of modern Russian literature. Born into a noble family in Moscow with both aristocratic and African (Abyssinian) ancestry through his great-grandfather Abram Gannibal, he was educated at the Imperial Lyceum in Tsarskoye Selo, where his early literary talent quickly attracted attention. Pushkin came of age in the turbulent post-Napoleonic era, and his work blended classical forms with colloquial Russian, helping to shape a new, distinctly national literary language.

Politically and socially engaged, Pushkin associated with liberal circles and was viewed with suspicion by authorities, leading to periods of exile within Russia. His verse novel "Eugene Onegin" became a landmark of Russian literature, fusing social commentary, psychological depth, and formal innovation, and influencing generations of writers after him. Known for his passionate temperament and complex personal relationships, Pushkin’s life was cut short when he died from wounds sustained in a duel defending his honor. His legacy as a central cultural figure in Russia endures, with his works continuing to inform Russian identity, literature, and opera adaptations in many languages.
