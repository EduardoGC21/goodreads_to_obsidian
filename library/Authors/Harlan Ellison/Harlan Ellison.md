## Biography
Harlan Ellison (1934–2018) was an American writer best known for his fiercely imaginative and often abrasive contributions to speculative fiction, spanning short stories, essays, television scripts, and criticism. Born in Cleveland, Ohio, he ran away from home as a teenager, working a series of odd jobs that later colored his gritty, socially charged fiction. Moving to New York in the 1950s, he became part of the science fiction magazine scene, quickly gaining notice for his intense prose, moral urgency, and refusal to follow genre conventions.

Ellison’s significance rests on both the quality of his work and his combative advocacy for author’s rights and creative integrity. Stories such as “I Have No Mouth & I Must Scream” and “"Repent, Harlequin!" Said the Ticktockman” exemplify his blend of psychological horror, social critique, and stylistic experimentation. In television he wrote influential episodes for series like "The Outer Limits" and contributed the original script for the celebrated "Star Trek" episode "The City on the Edge of Forever," while later serving as a creative consultant on "Babylon 5." A famously litigious and outspoken figure, he cultivated intense relationships—both loyal friendships and bitter feuds—with editors, producers, and fellow writers, but he was also widely admired as a mentor and champion of younger authors. Ellison’s work helped redefine the boundaries of science fiction and fantasy, leaving a lasting mark on late‑20th‑century popular culture.
