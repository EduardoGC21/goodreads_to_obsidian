## Biography
Harlan Ellison (1934–2018) was an American writer best known for his fiercely imaginative, socially conscious, and often abrasive speculative fiction. Born in Cleveland, Ohio, he spent parts of his youth running away from home, working odd jobs, and absorbing experiences that later fueled the intensity of his stories. Ellison’s career took off in the 1950s and 1960s in magazines and anthologies, where his short fiction, essays, and criticism stood out for their stylistic bravado and moral urgency.

Ellison became a central figure in science fiction and fantasy, while also writing for television shows such as “The Outer Limits” and “Star Trek,” where his episode “The City on the Edge of Forever” became a classic. His story collection “I Have No Mouth & I Must Scream,” among others, solidified his reputation for blending psychological horror, dystopian themes, and sharp social commentary. He was renowned both for his literary achievements—earning multiple Hugo, Nebula, and other awards—and for his combative defense of writers’ rights, often engaging in public disputes and legal battles to protect creative ownership.

Throughout his life, Ellison cultivated close relationships within the science fiction community, mentoring younger writers and participating in conventions, while also remaining a polarizing figure due to his outspoken, confrontational style. Despite the controversies, he is widely regarded as one of the most influential voices in modern speculative fiction, leaving behind a legacy of boundary-pushing work that challenged readers to confront cruelty, injustice, and the darker corners of the human psyche.
