## Biography
Samuel Beckett (1906–1989) was an Irish playwright, novelist, and poet whose stark, minimalist works reshaped twentieth‑century literature and theatre. Born in Foxrock, near Dublin, into a middle‑class Protestant family, he was educated at Trinity College Dublin before moving to Paris, where he became closely associated with James Joyce. Beckett’s early years were marked by restlessness, teaching posts, and a growing sense of artistic and existential crisis that would later define his work. During World War II he joined the French Resistance, an experience that deepened his preoccupation with human suffering, endurance, and moral ambiguity.

After the war, Beckett began writing primarily in French, stripping his style down to bare essentials and focusing on the absurdity and isolation of human existence. His play “Waiting for Godot” ("Esperando a Godot") brought him international fame in the 1950s and became a landmark of the Theatre of the Absurd, portraying characters trapped in repetitive, seemingly purposeless routines while still clinging to hope. Across his novels, plays, and short prose, he explored themes of memory, identity, and the limits of language, often using humor and silence to underscore despair.

Beckett spent most of his adult life in France, living modestly and shunning publicity even as his reputation grew. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1969 for his innovative writing, which, in the words of the Swedish Academy, achieved greatness in the destitution of modern man. Though intensely private, he maintained a circle of close relationships and quietly supported friends and collaborators. Beckett died in Paris in 1989, leaving a body of work that continues to influence writers, directors, and philosophers, and that stands as one of the most rigorous artistic examinations of what it means to persist in a seemingly indifferent universe.
