## Biography
Samuel Beckett (1906–1989) was an Irish novelist, playwright, poet, and director whose stark, minimalist works reshaped twentieth-century literature and theatre. Born in Foxrock, near Dublin, into a middle-class Protestant family, he was educated at Trinity College Dublin before moving to Paris, where he formed a pivotal friendship with James Joyce. Fluent in French and English, Beckett eventually chose to write many of his major works in French to strip his language down to its bare essentials.

During World War II, Beckett joined the French Resistance and was forced to flee Paris when his group was compromised, experiences that deepened his preoccupation with suffering, endurance, and the absurdity of existence. After the war, he wrote the plays and novels that made his reputation, most notably "En attendant Godot" ("Waiting for Godot"), known in Spanish as "Esperando a Godot", which revolutionized modern drama with its bleak humor, circular dialogue, and vision of life as an unresolved waiting. His relationships with long-term partner (later wife) Suzanne Déchevaux-Dumesnil and his close circle in Paris provided personal stability as his artistic renown grew.

Beckett received the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1969 for his innovative, uncompromising body of work, though he remained personally shy of publicity and fiercely protective of his privacy. Dividing his time mainly between France and trips back to Ireland, he continued to experiment with prose, theatre, radio, and television, refining a style marked by silence, repetition, and the erosion of narrative certainty. By the time of his death in Paris in 1989, Beckett had become a central figure of literary modernism and the Theatre of the Absurd, profoundly influencing writers and directors worldwide.
