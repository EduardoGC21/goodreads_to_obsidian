## Biography
Arthur C. Clarke (1917–2008) was a British science fiction author, futurist, and science popularizer whose work helped define 20th‑century speculative literature. Born in Minehead, Somerset, England, he developed an early fascination with astronomy and space travel, joined amateur astronomy societies, and moved to London as a young man. During World War II he served in the Royal Air Force as a radar specialist, an experience that sharpened his understanding of emerging technologies and their potential.

After the war Clarke studied mathematics and physics at King’s College London and soon became known both for visionary essays on spaceflight and for rigorously imagined fiction. He famously outlined the concept of communications satellites in geostationary orbit, anticipating a cornerstone of modern telecommunications. As a novelist he explored cosmic evolution, first contact, and the future of humanity in works such as “Childhood’s End,” “The City and the Stars,” “Rendezvous with Rama,” and “The Fountains of Paradise,” blending scientific plausibility with philosophical and religious themes.

Clarke spent much of his later life in Sri Lanka, where he pursued underwater exploration and championed scientific inquiry and international cooperation. His collaboration with filmmaker Stanley Kubrick on “2001: A Space Odyssey” cemented his status as one of science fiction’s central figures. Widely honored with awards and a knighthood, Clarke influenced generations of scientists, writers, and technologists with his optimistic yet clear‑eyed vision of humanity’s place in the universe.
