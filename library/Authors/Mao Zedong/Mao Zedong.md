## Biography
Mao Zedong (1893–1976) was a Chinese revolutionary leader, political theorist, and founding father of the People’s Republic of China, proclaimed in 1949 after the victory of the Chinese Communist Party in the civil war. Born to a peasant family in Shaoshan, Hunan, he was shaped by poverty, social upheaval, and the collapse of the Qing dynasty. Mao became active in student and Marxist circles in the 1910s and 1920s, helping to found the Chinese Communist Party in 1921 and rising to prominence through his focus on mobilizing the rural peasantry rather than the urban proletariat that classical Marxism emphasized.

Mao led the Red Army through the Long March (1934–1935), a pivotal retreat that helped consolidate his authority within the party and gave him immense symbolic stature. After defeating the Nationalists in 1949, he ruled as China’s paramount leader, advancing a radical program of land reform, collectivization, and social transformation. Campaigns such as the Great Leap Forward and the Cultural Revolution sought to remake Chinese society and preserve revolutionary fervor, but led to widespread famine, political persecution, and cultural destruction, leaving a deeply contested legacy. Collections of his speeches and quotations, such as the "Little Red Book" ("El Libro Rojo de Mao"), helped project his image as a central ideological figure both in China and internationally, making him one of the most influential and controversial leaders of the twentieth century.

Mao’s personal relationships were tightly interwoven with his political life. He maintained complex alliances and rivalries with fellow leaders such as Zhou Enlai, Liu Shaoqi, and Lin Biao, often using ideological campaigns to shift power balances within the party. His marriages, especially to Jiang Qing, who became a prominent political actor during the Cultural Revolution, further bound his private life to the broader political drama of the era. Mao’s rule profoundly reshaped China’s state structure, social fabric, and international position, laying the foundations for the modern Chinese state even as later leaders moved away from many of his most radical policies.
