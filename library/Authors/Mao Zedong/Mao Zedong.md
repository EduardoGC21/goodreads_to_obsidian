## Biography
Mao Zedong (1893–1976) was a Chinese revolutionary and political leader who played a central role in the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) and the founding of the People’s Republic of China in 1949. Born to a peasant family in Shaoshan, Hunan, he was shaped by late Qing social upheaval, the fall of the imperial system, and the May Fourth intellectual ferment. Mao rose from local activism and guerrilla organizing to national prominence during the Chinese Civil War, advocating a peasant-based revolution and developing theories of protracted people’s war and mass mobilization that heavily influenced global communist movements.

As the top leader of the new socialist state, Mao oversaw sweeping land reforms, campaigns to restructure society, and efforts to rapidly industrialize and collectivize agriculture. His initiatives, including the Great Leap Forward and later the Cultural Revolution, profoundly transformed Chinese society but also caused widespread suffering, famine, and political persecution, leaving a deeply contested legacy. Mao cultivated a quasi-charismatic relationship with the masses, reinforced through propaganda and widely circulated quotations from his speeches, sometimes known in the West as the “Little Red Book,” which became a symbol of his ideological authority. His life and rule fundamentally reshaped China’s political system, social structures, and international alignment, making him one of the most consequential and controversial figures of the twentieth century.
