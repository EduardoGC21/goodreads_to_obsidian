## Biography
Robert E. Howard (1906–1936) was an American pulp writer best known as the creator of Conan the Cimmerian, one of the most enduring characters in heroic fantasy. Born in Peaster, Texas and raised mostly in small oil-boom towns, Howard absorbed frontier lore, local history, and tales of violence and hardship that would later shape the gritty tone and physicality of his fiction. Although he wrote across many genres—historical adventure, boxing stories, westerns, horror, and humorous yarns—his vivid, hard-edged prose and powerful sense of myth helped define what would later be called sword and sorcery.

Howard sold much of his work to the pulp magazine Weird Tales, where he was a contemporary and correspondent of writers like H. P. Lovecraft. Their letters reveal his strong attachment to Texas, his fascination with history and barbarian cultures, and his struggles with mood and isolation. The Conan stories, often collected in volumes such as *The Complete Chronicles of Conan*, distilled his ideas about civilization, decadence, and individual strength, influencing generations of fantasy authors, comics, and film-makers.

Howard’s life was brief and troubled. He remained close to his parents, particularly his mother, whose long illness weighed heavily on him. In 1936, after learning her condition was hopeless, he died by suicide in Cross Plains, Texas, at the age of 30. Despite his short career, his contributions to fantasy and popular fiction were profound: he pioneered a fast-paced, visceral style and created settings and characters that remain central to modern fantasy culture.
