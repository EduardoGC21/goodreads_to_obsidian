## Biography
Robert E. Howard (1906–1936) was an American writer best known as one of the founding figures of sword-and-sorcery fantasy. Born in Peaster, Texas, and raised across various rough-and-tumble oil boomtowns, he developed a lifelong fascination with history, boxing, frontier life, and heroic legend. Much of his work was published in pulp magazines such as Weird Tales, where his vivid style, hard-edged action, and brooding atmospheres quickly distinguished him from his contemporaries.

Howard’s most famous creation is Conan the Cimmerian, whose adventures—later collected in volumes such as “The Complete Chronicles of Conan”—helped crystallize the sword-and-sorcery genre and influenced generations of fantasy writers, artists, and game designers. He also wrote horror, historical adventures, and Westerns, showing a breadth of interest that extended far beyond a single character. Howard maintained a close, long-distance friendship and correspondence with H. P. Lovecraft, and their exchanges about culture, history, and philosophy enriched both authors’ fictional worlds.

Living much of his life in the small town of Cross Plains, Texas, Howard supported himself and his parents largely through his writing, while struggling with bouts of depression and a strong sense of isolation. In 1936, after learning that his terminally ill mother was not expected to recover, he died by suicide at the age of 30. Despite his brief life, Howard’s work has remained continuously in print, and his larger-than-life heroes, dark mythologies, and driving prose have secured him a lasting place in the history of modern fantasy and popular culture.
