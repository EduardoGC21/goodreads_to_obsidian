## Biography
Hesiod was an early Greek poet traditionally dated to the late 8th or early 7th century BCE, often considered alongside Homer as one of the foundational voices of ancient Greek literature. Likely originating from the region of Boeotia in mainland Greece, he presents himself as a modest farmer whose life and family disputes shaped his concern with justice, work, and the proper ordering of human and divine affairs. His poetry reflects both intimate familiarity with rural life and a sophisticated engagement with myth and theology.

In works such as the Theogony and Works and Days, Hesiod systematized Greek myth about the gods, the origins of the world, and the moral structure of the cosmos, giving later Greek religion and philosophy a shared narrative and conceptual framework. He also explored themes of human suffering, the value of labor, and the role of divine justice in everyday life, influencing not only later poets and dramatists but also philosophers who drew on his depictions of Zeus’s justice and the moral order. Though little is known for certain about his personal relationships, his poetry suggests a world of small communities, family tensions, and devotional practices centered on the Muses, which together define his distinctive, down‑to‑earth perspective within archaic Greek culture.
