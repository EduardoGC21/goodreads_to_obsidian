## Biography
Hesiod was an early Greek poet, traditionally dated to around the late 8th to early 7th century BCE, and is often regarded—alongside Homer—as one of the foundational figures of ancient Greek literature. Little is known about his life with certainty, but ancient sources place him in Ascra, a rural village in Boeotia, where he is said to have been a small farmer and herdsman. This agrarian background deeply shaped his outlook, giving his poetry a distinctive focus on everyday labor, justice, and the precariousness of human fortune.

His two most important works, *Theogony* and *Works and Days*, played different but complementary roles in Greek culture. *Theogony* offers a sweeping genealogy of the gods and a mythic account of the cosmos’ ordering, becoming a key reference point for later Greek religion and mythology. *Works and Days*, by contrast, is a didactic poem addressed to his brother Perses, mixing moral exhortation with practical farming advice and reflections on justice, hard work, and the decline of the human condition through the Ages of Man. Other poems attributed to him in antiquity, such as the *Shield of Heracles* and the *Contest of Homer and Hesiod*, further enhanced his reputation as a poet who bridged myth, moral instruction, and everyday life.

Hesiod’s significance lies not only in preserving mythic traditions but also in giving voice to the experiences and anxieties of ordinary people in archaic Greece. His emphasis on justice under Zeus, the value of honest labor, and the realities of rural hardship shaped Greek ethical and social thought for centuries. Through his vivid portrayal of both divine and human worlds, he became a crucial source for later philosophers, dramatists, and historians seeking to understand the moral and cosmological framework of early Greek society.
