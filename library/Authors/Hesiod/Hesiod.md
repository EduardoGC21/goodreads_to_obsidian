## Biography
Hesiod (c. 8th–7th century BCE) was one of the earliest known Greek poets and, together with Homer, a foundational figure of ancient Greek literature. Little is known about his life with certainty, but ancient tradition places him in Boeotia, particularly the village of Ascra, where he is said to have been a farmer and shepherd before turning to poetry. His works reflect a world in transition, capturing the social, economic, and religious concerns of early archaic Greece.

In poems such as *Theogony* and *Works and Days*, Hesiod combined myth, practical wisdom, and moral instruction. *Theogony* offers one of the earliest systematic accounts of the origins and genealogy of the gods, shaping later Greek religious and philosophical thought. *Works and Days* is more personal, framed as advice to his brother Perses, and addresses themes of justice, labor, and proper conduct, giving modern readers rare insight into everyday life and values of his time.

Hesiod’s significance lies in his role as a didactic poet and moral commentator, distinct from the largely heroic and epic focus of Homer. He helped establish poetry as a medium not only for storytelling but also for instruction, reflection, and social critique. His influence extended throughout classical antiquity and beyond, informing later Greek poets, philosophers, and historians and contributing greatly to the development of Greek religious and ethical traditions.
