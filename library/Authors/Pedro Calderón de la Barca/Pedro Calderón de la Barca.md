## Biography
Pedro Calderón de la Barca (1600–1681) was a Spanish playwright, poet, and priest, and one of the central figures of the Baroque stage during Spain’s Golden Age. Born in Madrid into a family connected with royal administration, he received a humanist education at the Jesuit Colegio Imperial and later studied at the universities of Alcalá and Salamanca. After a period of military service in campaigns in Italy and Flanders, he settled in Madrid, where his talent for theater quickly made him the leading successor to Lope de Vega and a favorite at court.

Calderón became the foremost dramatist of philosophical and religious theater in 17th‑century Spain, refining the comedia form with tightly constructed plots, complex characters, and a rich, poetic language. His plays often explore honor, free will, and the nature of reality, as seen in works such as La vida es sueño and El alcalde de Zalamea, which remain cornerstones of Spanish literature. In 1651 he was ordained a priest and devoted much of his later career to writing autos sacramentales—allegorical one‑act plays for Corpus Christi—while serving as honorary chaplain and official court dramatist to King Philip IV. Calderón’s influence extended across Europe, shaping later drama in Germany and beyond, and he is widely regarded as one of the greatest dramatists of the early modern period.

Personally, Calderón maintained close ties with the royal court and the ecclesiastical hierarchy, balancing his duties as a cleric with his role as a public intellectual and artist. Though he never married, he was embedded in a network of fellow writers, actors, and patrons who sustained the vibrant theatrical culture of Madrid. He died in Madrid in 1681, leaving a vast body of work that secured his reputation as a defining voice of Counter‑Reformation and Baroque culture.
