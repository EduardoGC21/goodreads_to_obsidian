## Biography
Pedro Calderón de la Barca (1600–1681) was a Spanish playwright, poet, and priest, widely regarded as one of the greatest figures of Spain’s Golden Age of literature. Born in Madrid to a family connected to the minor nobility, he received a rigorous Jesuit education and studied at the universities of Alcalá and Salamanca. His early exposure to theology, philosophy, and classical rhetoric deeply shaped his vision of drama as a vehicle for exploring moral responsibility, honor, and the limits of human freedom.

Calderón became closely associated with the royal court in Madrid, writing for courtly celebrations, religious festivals, and the public theaters that flourished under Philip IV. Over his long career he produced tragedies of honor such as "El médico de su honra," philosophical dramas like "La vida es sueño," and autos sacramentales including "El gran teatro del mundo," which use rich allegory to examine human existence, divine justice, and social order. His works often revisit the tensions between personal honor and state authority, as in "El alcalde de Zalamea," reflecting the anxieties and ideals of a hierarchical early modern society.

Ordained as a priest in midlife, Calderón continued to write while serving as chaplain to the king, and his religious vocation intensified the theological depth and moral seriousness of his later plays. Admired by contemporaries and later by European Romantic and modernist writers, he helped define the Baroque stage with elaborate poetic language, complex structures, and a fusion of spectacle and reflection. His influence endures in debates about free will, identity, and political power, and he remains a central figure in the canon of Spanish and world drama.
