## Biography
Wu Cheng'en (c. 1500–1582) was a Chinese writer and scholar of the Ming dynasty, traditionally credited as the author of the classic novel *Journey to the West*. Born in Huai'an in what is now Jiangsu province, he grew up in a milieu shaped by the imperial examination system and Confucian learning. Although he studied for the examinations and held minor official posts, his career in government was limited, and he spent significant periods of his life in relative obscurity, teaching and writing while maintaining connections with local literati circles.

Wu’s importance lies less in his official career than in his literary achievement. In *Journey to the West* (known in Spanish as “Viaje al Oeste: Las aventuras del Rey Mono”), he synthesized centuries of folklore, religious tales, and popular drama into a sophisticated and humorous narrative about the monk Xuanzang’s pilgrimage to India, accompanied by the rebellious Monkey King Sun Wukong and other disciples. The work reflects his deep familiarity with Buddhism, Daoism, and popular religion, as well as a sharp eye for social satire and human weakness. Through playful storytelling and vivid characterization, Wu Cheng'en created one of the Four Great Classical Novels of Chinese literature, a work that has profoundly shaped Chinese cultural imagination and influenced later writers, operas, and modern adaptations.

Little is securely documented about Wu Cheng'en’s private relationships, but sources portray him as a somewhat eccentric and independent-minded scholar, more devoted to literary expression than to securing high office. His life spanned a period of complex social change in late Ming China, and his writing captures both a delight in the fantastical and a critical perspective on worldly ambition, corruption, and moral discipline. Despite the paucity of biographical records and the fact that the authorship of *Journey to the West* was only firmly attributed to him in later scholarship, Wu Cheng'en is now widely recognized as a pivotal figure in the development of the Chinese novel.
