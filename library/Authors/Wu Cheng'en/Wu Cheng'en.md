## Biography
Wu Cheng'en (c. 1500–1582) was a Chinese novelist and scholar of the Ming dynasty, traditionally regarded as the principal author of the classic novel now known in English as "Journey to the West." Born in Huai'an in what is today Jiangsu province, he grew up in a milieu shaped by the civil service examination system and the flourishing urban culture of late imperial China. Though he pursued the examinations and held minor official posts, his career in government was modest, and he spent much of his life as a private scholar and teacher, drawing on a wide knowledge of folklore, religion, and classical literature.

Wu’s significance lies above all in his creative transformation of popular tales about the Monkey King, the monk Xuanzang, and their pilgrimage to India into a sophisticated narrative that blends satire, religious allegory, and adventure. In "Journey to the West" (known in Spanish as "Viaje al Oeste: Las aventuras del Rey Mono"), he used humor and fantasy to comment on human weakness, bureaucratic corruption, and the spiritual quest, giving enduring literary form to themes that resonated deeply with Ming society. Although details of his personal relationships and daily life remain sparse, the novel’s rich characterizations and ironic tone suggest an author intimately familiar with both elite scholarly culture and common storytelling traditions. Over the centuries, Wu Cheng'en has come to be recognized as one of the great figures of Chinese literature, and his work remains central to understanding the imaginative and religious world of late imperial China.
