## Biography
Aristotle (384–322 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher, scientist, and polymath whose work shaped the foundations of Western thought. Born in Stagira in northern Greece, he traveled to Athens as a young man to study in Plato’s Academy, where he remained for about twenty years. After Plato’s death, Aristotle spent time at various courts, including that of Hermias of Atarneus, and later became tutor to the young Alexander of Macedon, who would become Alexander the Great—an association that helped secure Aristotle influence, patronage, and access to extensive resources.

Returning to Athens, Aristotle founded his own school, the Lyceum, where he and his students conducted research and taught in a wide array of disciplines, from logic, metaphysics, and ethics to biology, politics, and literary theory. His treatises, including works like the "Nicomachean Ethics," "Politics," "Metaphysics," and "Poetics" (often published in many languages and editions), systematized knowledge and introduced methods of classification, empirical observation, and rigorous argumentation that became models for later scholarship. Aristotle’s ideas on virtue, civic life, causality, and the structure of reality remained central in the Hellenistic world and were later transmitted through Byzantine, Islamic, and Latin medieval traditions, deeply influencing Christian, Jewish, and Muslim philosophy.

Aristotle’s personal life is less well documented than his intellectual legacy, but sources suggest close ties to Macedonian political circles, a marriage or partnership with Pythias (a relative of Hermias), and later companionship with Herpyllis, with whom he likely had a son. Forced to leave Athens amid anti-Macedonian sentiment after Alexander’s death, he withdrew to Chalcis on the island of Euboea, where he died. Despite the political and cultural upheavals of his time, Aristotle’s synthesis of rational inquiry and systematic investigation made him a central figure in philosophy and science for over two millennia.
