## Biography
Aristotle (384–322 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher, scientist, and educator whose work shaped the foundations of Western thought. Born in Stagira in northern Greece, he studied for roughly twenty years at Plato’s Academy in Athens before later tutoring Alexander the Great. After his time at the Macedonian court, he returned to Athens and founded his own school, the Lyceum, where he and his students collected data, observed nature, and systematically investigated topics ranging from logic and metaphysics to ethics, politics, biology, and rhetoric.

Aristotle’s significance lies in both the breadth and the organization of his thought. He developed the first formal system of logic, offered influential accounts of causality, substance, and change, and proposed virtue ethics as a way of understanding moral character and the good life, as seen in works like the Nicomachean Ethics and the Politics. His ideas on political community, friendship, education, and the structure of knowledge guided philosophical, theological, and scientific traditions for centuries in the Greek, Roman, Islamic, and Christian worlds. Through his students and later commentators, Aristotle’s work became a cornerstone of medieval scholarship and continues to be a central reference point in philosophy and the history of science today.
