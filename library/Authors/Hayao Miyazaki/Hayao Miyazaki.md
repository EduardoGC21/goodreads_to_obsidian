## Biography
Hayao Miyazaki (1941– ) is a Japanese film director, animator, and manga artist, widely regarded as one of the most influential figures in the history of animation. Born on January 5, 1941, in Tokyo, he grew up during and after World War II, experiences that later informed his recurring themes of pacifism, environmentalism, and the emotional costs of war. His father worked for a company that made parts for fighter planes, a biographical detail that shaped Miyazaki’s lifelong ambivalence toward aviation—both its beauty and its destructive potential.

Miyazaki joined Toei Animation in the early 1960s, steadily developing his craft as an animator, storyboard artist, and writer before co-founding Studio Ghibli in 1985. At Ghibli he directed internationally acclaimed films such as "My Neighbor Totoro," "Princess Mononoke," "Spirited Away," and "Howl’s Moving Castle." His works are known for their richly detailed worlds, morally complex characters, and strong, often young, female protagonists. "Spirited Away" won the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature and cemented his status as a master storyteller whose influence extends far beyond Japan. Books like "Art of the Wind Rises" and "Art of the Boy and the Heron" document his meticulous visual development process and highlight how his late-career films continue to probe themes of war, grief, and reconciliation.

Throughout his life, Miyazaki has been both celebrated and described as demanding and perfectionist, maintaining intense involvement in every aspect of production and forming close, sometimes challenging creative relationships with collaborators such as producer Toshio Suzuki and fellow director Isao Takahata. Despite multiple public retirements, he has repeatedly returned to filmmaking, driven by an enduring desire to tell new stories. His body of work and his role in shaping Studio Ghibli have made him a central figure in global cinema and a defining voice in modern animated storytelling.
