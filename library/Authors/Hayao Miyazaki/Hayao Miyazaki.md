## Biography
Hayao Miyazaki (1941– ) is a Japanese animator, director, and manga artist, widely regarded as one of the most influential figures in the history of animation. Born in Tokyo to a family involved in aircraft manufacturing, he grew up during and after World War II, an experience that deeply shaped his sensitivity to war, technology, and the natural world. Miyazaki joined Toei Animation in the early 1960s, steadily developing his craft as an animator and storyteller before co-founding Studio Ghibli in 1985, where he created films known for their rich worlds, nuanced characters, and moral complexity.

Miyazaki’s films often center on young protagonists, particularly girls, navigating worlds where environmental, political, and personal conflicts intersect. Works such as "Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind," "My Neighbor Totoro," "Princess Mononoke," and "Spirited Away" have become cultural touchstones, earning critical acclaim, major awards, and a devoted global audience. The art and making-of volumes for his films, such as those related to "The Wind Rises" and "The Boy and the Heron," highlight his meticulous storyboarding process and his insistence on hand-drawn animation, reflecting his belief that the act of drawing is central to honest, expressive filmmaking.

Known for his strong views, Miyazaki has been outspoken about pacifism, environmental responsibility, and the responsibilities of artists in society. He has maintained close working relationships with key collaborators at Studio Ghibli, including producer Toshio Suzuki and composer Joe Hisaishi, and his work has influenced generations of animators and filmmakers around the world. Despite multiple announced retirements, he has repeatedly returned to filmmaking, underscoring both his restless creativity and his lifelong commitment to animation as a serious art form.
