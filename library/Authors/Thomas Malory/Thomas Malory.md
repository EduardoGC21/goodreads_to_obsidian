## Biography
Sir Thomas Malory (c. 1415–1471) was an English writer and knight, traditionally associated with Newbold Revel in Warwickshire, whose life bridged the turbulent years of the Wars of the Roses. Historical records depict him as a man of some status, involved in local politics and periodically entangled in legal and military conflicts, including periods of imprisonment. It is widely believed that during one of these confinements he composed his great Arthurian compilation, drawing on French and English romances to reshape the legendary material for a late medieval audience.

Malory’s enduring significance rests almost entirely on his masterpiece commonly known as *Le Morte d’Arthur* (in Spanish, *La muerte de Arturo*), completed around 1469–1470 and later shaped for print by the pioneering English printer William Caxton. By organizing diverse tales of King Arthur, Guinevere, Lancelot, and the Knights of the Round Table into a single, continuous narrative, Malory created the defining English version of the Arthurian cycle and profoundly influenced subsequent literature, from Renaissance writers to modern novelists and poets. Although the details of his personal relationships and inner life remain largely obscure, his work reveals a preoccupation with chivalry, loyalty, betrayal, and the collapse of idealized communities, themes that likely reflected both his own experience as a knight and the broader political fractures of fifteenth‑century England.
