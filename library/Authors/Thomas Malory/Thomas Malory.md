## Biography
Sir Thomas Malory (c.1415–1471) was an English writer and knight best known as the author or compiler of *Le Morte d'Arthur*, one of the most influential retellings of the Arthurian legends in the English language. Little is known with certainty about his life, and much of his biography has been reconstructed from fragmentary legal and historical records. The most widely accepted candidate is Sir Thomas Malory of Newbold Revel in Warwickshire, a member of the gentry who was involved in the politics and conflicts of fifteenth‑century England during the latter stages of the Hundred Years’ War and the turbulent Wars of the Roses.

Malory’s life appears to have been marked by both chivalric status and serious legal troubles, including accusations of violent crimes and multiple imprisonments, though scholars debate the exact nature and reliability of these charges. It was likely during one of his terms in prison that he composed *Le Morte d'Arthur* (sometimes titled in Spanish as *La muerte de Arturo*), drawing on French and English Arthurian sources and reshaping them into a unified prose narrative. The work reflects Malory’s deep engagement with ideals of knighthood, loyalty, and honor at a time when the traditional chivalric order in England was under strain.
Le Morte d'Arthur* became a cornerstone of Arthurian literature, profoundly influencing later writers, artists, and the modern image of King Arthur and the Knights of the Round Table. Malory’s synthesis of earlier romances created a coherent life‑and‑death arc for Arthur, embedding themes of heroism, betrayal, and the tragic decline of a noble order—motifs that resonated with his own era’s instability. Although the details of Malory’s personal relationships and inner life remain obscure, his work stands as a major achievement of late medieval English prose and a lasting monument to the chivalric imagination.
