## Biography
Raúl Prebisch (1901–1986) was an Argentine economist and statesman who became one of the most influential figures in 20th‑century development economics. Born in San Miguel de Tucumán to a family of German-Argentine background, he studied economics at the University of Buenos Aires and, by his early twenties, was already a professor of political economy there. In the 1930s and early 1940s he held a series of key posts in Argentina’s public administration, including undersecretary of finance and, notably, founding director‑general of the Central Bank of the Republic of Argentina, where he helped design modern monetary and fiscal instruments for a country grappling with the effects of the Great Depression.

Initially a supporter of free trade, Prebisch radically revised his views as he observed the chronic vulnerabilities of primary‑exporting economies like Argentina during global downturns. His theoretical work, informed in part by his engagement with Keynesian ideas—reflected in writings such as "Introducción a Keynes"—led to what became known as the Prebisch‑Singer thesis: the argument that the terms of trade tend to move against countries exporting primary commodities and in favor of industrialized “center” economies. From this insight he developed a structuralist approach that advocated industrialization, regional integration, and active state policy as paths for Latin America and the broader “periphery” to escape dependency and persistent underdevelopment.

From 1950 to 1963 Prebisch served as executive secretary of the UN Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean (ECLAC), transforming it into a leading intellectual center for development thinking, and from 1964 to 1969 he was the founding secretary‑general of UNCTAD, where he championed a fairer international economic order for developing nations. Throughout his life he maintained close ties with Latin American political and intellectual circles, influencing generations of policymakers and scholars concerned with inequality, trade, and growth. Prebisch died in Santiago, Chile, in 1986, remembered as a central architect of structuralist and dependency theories and as a major voice for the Global South in international economic debates.
