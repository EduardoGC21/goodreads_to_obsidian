## Biography
Raúl Prebisch (1901–1986) was an Argentine economist whose work reshaped thinking about development and the global economy in the twentieth century. Born in San Miguel de Tucumán to a German–Argentine family, he studied economics at the University of Buenos Aires and began teaching political economy there in the early 1920s. Alongside his academic career he quickly rose through Argentina’s economic bureaucracy, serving in roles such as Director of Economic Research for the National Bank, Under-Secretary of Finance, and first director of the newly created Central Bank of Argentina, where he helped steer policy through the turmoil of the interwar years and the Great Depression.

Experiences with Argentina’s vulnerability as an exporter of primary goods led Prebisch to question orthodox free‑trade theory and articulate what became known as the Prebisch–Singer hypothesis: that the terms of trade tended to move against primary‑exporting countries over time. Building on this insight, he emerged as a leading figure of structuralist economics and a key architect of Latin American development thinking, including arguments for industrialization and state-led strategies to transform “peripheral” economies. After joining the United Nations Economic Commission for Latin America and the Caribbean (ECLAC), he served as its Executive Secretary from 1950 to 1963 and later as the founding Secretary‑General of the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD), where he championed a fairer international economic order for developing countries.

Prebisch authored numerous influential works that circulated widely in Latin America and beyond, including his study on the economic development of Latin America and texts such as “Introducción a Keynes,” which reflected his engagement with and adaptation of major economic ideas to the realities of the global South. Over a six‑decade career as scholar, civil servant, and international official, he maintained close relationships with political leaders and intellectuals across the region, advising governments while inspiring generations of economists concerned with inequality between the global “center” and “periphery.” He died in Santiago, Chile, in 1986, leaving a legacy central to development and dependency theory.
