## Biography
Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860) was a German philosopher born in Danzig (then part of Prussia) who became one of the most influential figures in 19th‑century thought. Raised in a wealthy merchant family, he had a difficult relationship with his parents: he clashed with his practical, business‑minded father and formed a closer, though later strained, bond with his cultured and socially ambitious mother, Johanna, a well‑known salonnière and writer. After his father’s probable suicide, Schopenhauer abandoned a commercial career for academic study, immersing himself in classical philology, philosophy, and science at Göttingen and Berlin. His life was marked by a proud and often combative temperament, which contributed to personal isolation and professional frustration, especially in his bitter rivalry with G. W. F. Hegel, whose fame he deeply resented.

Schopenhauer’s central work, “The World as Will and Representation,” developed a metaphysics in which the underlying reality of the world is an irrational, ceaseless striving “will,” and human life is characterized by suffering that can be mitigated only through aesthetic contemplation, compassion, and ascetic resignation. Although largely ignored for much of his career and living for years in relative obscurity in Frankfurt, he eventually gained recognition in the 1850s as younger intellectuals and artists discovered his writings. His ideas profoundly influenced later thinkers and creators, including Friedrich Nietzsche, Richard Wagner, Thomas Mann, and Sigmund Freud, and he is now regarded as a major precursor of existentialism and psychoanalysis, as well as a key bridge between Western philosophy and certain Buddhist and Hindu themes.
