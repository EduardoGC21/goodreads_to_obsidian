## Biography
Arthur Schopenhauer (1788–1860) was a German philosopher best known for developing a radical metaphysics of the will, in which he argued that an irrational, striving will underlies all reality and human experience. Born in Danzig (now Gdańsk, Poland) into a wealthy merchant family, he received a broad education, studied at Göttingen and Berlin, and was deeply influenced by Immanuel Kant’s transcendental idealism as well as emerging Romantic thought. Schopenhauer’s personal life was marked by strained family relationships, especially with his authoritarian mother, the writer Johanna Schopenhauer, and by periods of isolation and pessimism that would later color his philosophical outlook.

His major work, "The World as Will and Representation," articulated a profoundly pessimistic view of existence, arguing that human suffering is rooted in the ceaseless and insatiable nature of the will. Although largely ignored or marginalized during his early career—and overshadowed at the time by Hegel—Schopenhauer eventually gained a devoted readership in the latter half of the 19th century. His ideas on art, compassion, asceticism, and the denial of the will influenced a wide range of figures, including Friedrich Nietzsche, Richard Wagner, Thomas Mann, and later existentialist and psychoanalytic thinkers. Today he is regarded as one of the most important and original philosophers of the 19th century, a key bridge between Kantian idealism and modern existential and psychological approaches to human life and suffering.
