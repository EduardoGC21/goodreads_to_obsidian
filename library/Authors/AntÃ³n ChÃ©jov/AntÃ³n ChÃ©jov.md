## Biography
Anton Chekhov (1860–1904) was a Russian playwright and short-story writer who became one of the central figures in modern literature. Trained and practicing as a physician, he often said that medicine was his lawful wife and literature his mistress, yet it was his writing that transformed the short story into a subtle, psychologically rich form. Raised in a struggling merchant family in Taganrog, he experienced hardship and social humiliation early on, which later informed his unsentimental depictions of poverty, boredom, and quiet despair in provincial Russian life.

Chekhov’s stories and plays are marked by understatement, irony, and a deep sympathy for ordinary people caught between hope and disappointment. He rejected moralizing and dramatic plot twists, instead allowing character, mood, and small everyday incidents to carry emotional weight. Collections such as those represented in "Cuentos Chejov" helped secure his reputation for capturing the inner lives of teachers, doctors, landowners, and clerks with rare nuance and compassion. His relationships and friendships with other writers and theatre practitioners, including collaborators at the Moscow Art Theatre, shaped the development of realistic drama and influenced generations of authors and playwrights worldwide.

Despite years of ill health from tuberculosis, Chekhov continued to write and support his extended family, while also engaging in charitable work, such as his journey to document conditions in the penal colony on Sakhalin Island. He married actress Olga Knipper late in life, a partnership intertwined with the theatrical world that brought his plays to the stage. Chekhov died relatively young, but his innovative approach to narrative and drama, along with his keen insight into human frailty, secured his lasting significance in world literature.
