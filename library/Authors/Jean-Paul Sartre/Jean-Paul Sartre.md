## Biography
Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980) was a French philosopher, novelist, playwright, and public intellectual, and one of the central figures in twentieth-century existentialism and phenomenology. Born in Paris, he studied at the École Normale Supérieure, where he formed important lifelong relationships, most notably with Simone de Beauvoir, his intellectual and romantic partner. Sartre rejected traditional religious and moral frameworks, arguing instead that humans are radically free and responsible for giving their own lives meaning in an indifferent or even absurd universe.

Sartre’s philosophical outlook was powerfully expressed through both his theoretical works and his literature. His novel "Nausea" dramatizes the disorienting experience of contingency and the search for authentic existence, while his lecture "Existentialism Is a Humanism" popularized his ideas for a broader audience by defending existentialism against charges of pessimism and amorality. In his autobiographical work "Les Mots" ("Words"), he reflected critically on his bourgeois upbringing and on literature’s role in his life.

Politically engaged throughout his career, Sartre was an outspoken critic of colonialism, authoritarianism, and social injustice, often taking controversial stances on issues such as the Algerian War and the Vietnam War. He declined the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1964, claiming that a writer should not allow himself to be institutionalized. Until his death in 1980, Sartre remained a symbol of committed intellectual life, using his public influence to link philosophical reflection with concrete political and ethical responsibility.
