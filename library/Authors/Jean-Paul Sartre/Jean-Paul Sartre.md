## Biography
Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980) was a French philosopher, novelist, playwright, and public intellectual, widely regarded as one of the central figures of 20th-century existentialism and phenomenology. Born in Paris, he studied at the École Normale Supérieure, where he formed key friendships and developed the philosophical interests that would shape his life. His early novel "Nausea" helped establish his reputation, presenting the themes of freedom, absurdity, and the contingency of existence that became hallmarks of his thought.

Sartre’s philosophy emphasized radical human freedom, personal responsibility, and the idea that existence precedes essence—that humans first exist and then define themselves through their choices and actions. He explored these ideas in major philosophical works such as "Being and Nothingness" and in popular lectures like "Existentialism Is a Humanism," which brought complex ideas to a wider audience. Sartre’s long and intellectually rich partnership with Simone de Beauvoir was central to his life; together they developed overlapping existentialist and feminist perspectives and cultivated a shared milieu of writers and activists.

Deeply engaged in political life, Sartre aligned himself at different times with socialist and Marxist movements, spoke out against colonialism, and became an outspoken critic of oppression in contexts such as the Algerian War and the Vietnam War. In 1964 he was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature but famously refused it, arguing that a writer should not become an institution. Until his death in 1980, Sartre remained a prominent, often controversial public figure whose work has had lasting influence on philosophy, literature, and political thought.
