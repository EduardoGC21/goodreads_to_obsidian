## Biography
Karl Marx (1818–1883) was a German philosopher, economist, historian, and revolutionary socialist whose work fundamentally reshaped modern political and social thought. Born in Trier in the Kingdom of Prussia, he studied law and philosophy at the universities of Bonn and Berlin, where he was influenced by German idealism and the Young Hegelians. Forced into exile for his radical views, Marx spent much of his adult life in cities such as Paris, Brussels, and ultimately London, living in relative poverty while dedicating himself to research and writing.

Marx’s collaboration with Friedrich Engels produced some of his most influential works, including *The Communist Manifesto*, which articulated a theory of history grounded in class struggle and called for the overthrow of capitalist systems. His multi-volume *Capital* ("El Capital" in Spanish editions) represented a sustained critique of political economy, aiming to expose the dynamics and contradictions of capitalist production and circulation. Although he did not live to see mass movements fully realize his ideas, Marx became a central intellectual figure for socialist, communist, and labor movements worldwide, and his analyses continue to influence philosophy, economics, sociology, and political theory.

In his personal life, Marx married Jenny von Westphalen, with whom he had several children, many of whom faced illness and early death amid the family’s financial hardship. His close friendship and intellectual partnership with Engels were crucial to his work; Engels frequently supported him financially and helped edit and publish his writings, including volumes of *Capital* after Marx’s death. Marx died in London in 1883 and was buried in Highgate Cemetery, leaving a legacy that remains both highly influential and deeply contested.
