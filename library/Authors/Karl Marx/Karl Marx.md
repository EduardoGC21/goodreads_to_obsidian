## Biography
Karl Marx (1818–1883) was a German philosopher, economist, journalist, and revolutionary socialist born in Trier, in the Kingdom of Prussia. Educated in law and philosophy at the universities of Bonn and Berlin, he was deeply influenced by German idealism, especially Hegel, and later turned to a materialist critique of society and history. Political repression and his radical views forced him into exile, first in Paris and Brussels and ultimately in London, where he spent most of his adult life in relative poverty, supported in large part by his close friend and collaborator Friedrich Engels.

Marx is best known for developing the theory of historical materialism and for analyzing capitalism as a system based on exploitation and class struggle. His major works include “The Communist Manifesto” (co‑authored with Engels) and the multi‑volume “Capital” (“Das Kapital”), in which he examined the dynamics of capital, labor, and profit. These writings provided the intellectual foundation for many later socialist and communist movements and profoundly shaped political theory, economics, and sociology. Marx’s personal life was marked by intense intellectual labor, chronic ill health, and family hardship, yet his ideas spread worldwide after his death, making him one of the most influential thinkers of the 19th and 20th centuries.
