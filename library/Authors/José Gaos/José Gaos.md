## Biography
José Gaos y González-Pola (1900–1969) was a Spanish-born philosopher and translator who became a central figure in 20th‑century Mexican intellectual life. Born in Gijón and raised partly in Asturias and Valencia, he studied philosophy at the universities of Valencia and Madrid, where he worked under leading Spanish thinkers such as José Ortega y Gasset, Manuel García Morente, and Xavier Zubiri. His early work, including a prize‑winning doctoral thesis on Husserl’s critique of psychologism, already showed his lifelong concern with the historicity of philosophy and the way thought is conditioned by circumstance.

Committed to the Republican cause during the Spanish Civil War, Gaos served as rector of the University of Madrid before the triumph of Franco’s forces forced him into exile. After a brief period in Cuba he settled in Mexico in 1938, later taking Mexican nationality and describing himself not as an exile but as a “transterrado,” transplanted into a new intellectual homeland. In Mexico he taught at institutions such as El Colegio de México and the National Autonomous University of Mexico, where his lectures and seminars shaped a generation of Latin American philosophers, including Leopoldo Zea, and inspired groups like the Hiperión circle. His extensive translations of German and other European philosophers—among them Heidegger’s *Being and Time*, which he introduced in works such as his “Introducción a El ser y el tiempo de Martin Heidegger”—were crucial in transmitting contemporary philosophy to the Spanish‑speaking world.

Gaos’s own writings developed a distinctive blend of metaphysics and radical historicism, emphasizing how philosophical systems arise from concrete historical situations. Through his teaching, translations, and essays on Hispanic and Mexican thought, he helped establish the history of ideas as a rigorous field of study in Latin America and contributed to the self‑understanding of Mexican philosophy in the mid‑20th century. He died in Mexico City in 1969, leaving behind a multi‑volume collected works and an archival legacy that continues to inform scholarship on Iberian and Latin American intellectual history.
