## Biography
José Gaos (1900–1969) was a Spanish-born philosopher and translator whose life bridged European and Latin American intellectual worlds. Born in Gijón as the eldest of a large, culturally engaged family that included the actress Lola Gaos and the poet Vicente Gaos, he spent his childhood between Asturias and Valencia before studying philosophy in Madrid, where he earned his doctorate in 1928 with a prize-winning thesis on Husserl’s critique of psychologism. In Spain he became part of the so‑called Madrid School, closely linked to his teacher José Ortega y Gasset as well as Manuel García Morente and Xavier Zubiri, and he held academic posts in León, Zaragoza, and ultimately at the University of Madrid, where he rose to the rectorship during the turbulent years of the Spanish Civil War.

A committed republican, Gaos went into exile during the Civil War and in 1938–1939 resettled in Mexico, where he obtained political asylum and later Mexican nationality. At the National Autonomous University of Mexico he became a central figure of Mexican and Latin American philosophy, mentoring students such as Leopoldo Zea and helping to shape several generations of thinkers. His work combined phenomenology, historicism, and a distinctive “philosophy of philosophy” that reflected on the historicity and personal character of philosophical thinking itself, often engaging critically with modernity, technocracy, and totalitarianism. His “Introducción a El ser y el tiempo de Martin Heidegger” exemplifies his role as a mediator of contemporary European thought while also contributing to debates about the specificity of Hispanic and Mexican philosophy.

Gaos was also one of the most influential translators of German and other European philosophy into Spanish, producing major translations of Heidegger’s “Being and Time” as well as works by Kant, Hegel, Husserl, Scheler, Fichte, Kierkegaard, and John Dewey. Through his translations, teaching, and extensive essays on the history of ideas and on Mexican and Latin American thought, he became a foundational figure in the intellectual life of mid‑20th‑century Mexico, leaving behind collected works and an archive preserved at UNAM that testify to his dual significance as both Spanish exile and Mexican philosopher.
