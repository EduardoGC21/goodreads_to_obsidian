## Biography
Walter Friedrich Gustav Hermann Otto (1874–1958) was a German classical philologist whose work reshaped the modern understanding of ancient Greek religion and mythology. Born in Hechingen to a pharmacist’s family and raised in Stuttgart, he initially began studies in Protestant theology at the Tübinger Stift but soon turned to classical philology, training under prominent scholars in Tübingen and Bonn. Over the course of an academic career that took him through several German-speaking universities, he became known for combining rigorous philological method with a deep philosophical and religious sensitivity to ancient texts.

Otto’s scholarship focused on the living reality of the Greek gods as they appear in myth, cult, and literature, rather than treating them merely as poetic figures or products of social evolution. In works such as “Die Götter Griechenlands” (The Homeric Gods) and “Dionysos: Mythos und Kultus,” he argued that Greek religion expressed a profound encounter with being and nature, influencing later thinkers in religious studies, philosophy, and classical scholarship. His interpretation of Dionysus, for example, emphasized the god’s dual aspects of ecstasy and destruction, life and death, as a key to understanding Greek experience of the divine.

Respected by contemporaries and later generations, Otto stood within the German philhellenic tradition that runs from Winckelmann and Goethe to Nietzsche, but developed his own ontological approach to myth. His work inspired students and colleagues such as Karl Kerényi and sparked debate among theologians who sometimes accused him of a too-sympathetic stance toward ancient polytheism. Until his death in Tübingen in 1958, Otto remained committed to presenting Greek religion as a serious, enduring way of understanding reality, helping to secure its place at the center of 20th‑century reflections on myth and the sacred.
