## Biography
Walter Friedrich Gustav Hermann Otto (1874–1958) was a German classical philologist whose work reshaped the modern understanding of ancient Greek and Roman religion. Born in Hechingen in the German Empire, he was the son of a pharmacist and was educated in Stuttgart before entering the Protestant seminary at Tübingen. Although he began in theology, he soon turned to classical philology, studying under influential scholars in Tübingen and Bonn and completing early work focused on Latin language and Roman culture.

Otto’s academic career took him through major German-speaking universities, including Vienna, Basel, Frankfurt, and Königsberg, where he held professorships in classical philology. In Frankfurt he developed close intellectual friendships, notably with Karl Reinhardt, and it was during this period that he produced his most important books on ancient religion, above all *Die Götter Griechenlands* (The Homeric Gods) and *Dionysos. Mythos und Kultus* (Dionysus: Myth and Cult). In these works he advanced a distinctive interpretation of Greek religion as a vivid, rational engagement with the divine presence in the natural world, a view that influenced later classicists, historians of religion, and philosophers. His study of Dionysus, later translated and published in various languages, used the god’s myth and cult to illuminate Greek conceptions of ecstasy, nature, and community.

During the Nazi era Otto accepted a chair in Königsberg under politically fraught circumstances and participated for a time in the Nietzsche Archive’s scholarly committee, positions that later drew critical scrutiny. He lost his home and library when he fled Königsberg in 1944 and spent the last phase of the war in Bavaria, continuing to lecture informally. After 1945 he served in temporary posts in Munich and Göttingen before settling in Tübingen as a professor emeritus. There he remained active into old age, lecturing and writing on myth and the gods until his death in 1958, leaving a legacy as one of the 20th century’s most significant interpreters of Greek religion and mythology.
