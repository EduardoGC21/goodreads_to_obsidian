## Biography
René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist whose work helped lay the foundations of modern philosophy and analytic geometry. Born in La Haye en Touraine in France, he studied at the Jesuit college of La Flèche and later trained in law, but soon turned toward a life of independent inquiry across Europe, serving in military campaigns and traveling widely. Seeking certainty in knowledge, he developed a method of systematic doubt that led to his famous conclusion “cogito, ergo sum” (“I think, therefore I am”), presented in works such as *Discourse on the Method*, of which many translations and editions (including Spanish ones) helped spread his ideas across Europe.

Descartes’ philosophical system sought to ground science and metaphysics on clear and distinct ideas, sharply distinguishing between mind and body (dualism) and influencing debates on consciousness, identity, and the nature of reality for centuries. As a mathematician, he introduced Cartesian coordinates, uniting geometry and algebra, which became central to the development of modern mathematics and physics. He maintained important intellectual relationships, especially his extensive correspondence with Princess Elisabeth of Bohemia, where he refined many of his views on mind, body, and emotion. Late in life he accepted an invitation from Queen Christina of Sweden to tutor her in philosophy; the harsh Swedish winter and demanding schedule are often cited as contributing to the illness that led to his death in Stockholm in 1650. His legacy endures as one of the central figures of the scientific revolution and the history of Western thought.
