## Biography
René Descartes (1596–1650) was a French philosopher, mathematician, and scientist, often regarded as a founding figure of modern Western thought. Born in La Haye en Touraine, he was educated by Jesuits and developed a lifelong interest in the relationship between mind, body, and knowledge. After military service and extensive travel in Europe, he settled for long periods in the Dutch Republic, where he produced most of his major works and maintained an active correspondence with leading thinkers and patrons.

Descartes is best known for his method of systematic doubt, encapsulated in the famous formulation “Cogito, ergo sum” (“I think, therefore I am”), and for his effort to ground knowledge on clear and distinct ideas. His philosophical writings, including the discourse in which he sets out his method, reshaped debates on metaphysics, epistemology, and the relation between soul and body. As a mathematician, he pioneered analytic geometry, linking algebra and geometry in ways that became foundational for later science. Descartes spent his final years in Sweden at the court of Queen Christina, where he died in 1650, leaving a legacy that influenced Enlightenment philosophy, modern science, and the development of rationalism.
