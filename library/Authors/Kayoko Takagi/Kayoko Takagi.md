## Biography
Kayoko Takagi is a Japanese scholar, translator, and cultural mediator whose work has focused on bringing the literary and folkloric heritage of Japan to Spanish-speaking audiences. Trained in Japanese language and literature, she has developed her career largely in Spain, where she teaches Japanese language and culture at the Universidad Autónoma de Madrid and conducts research at its Centro de Estudios de Asia Oriental. Through this academic base she has contributed to fostering a deeper and more nuanced understanding of Japan in the Spanish‑speaking world, especially among students and general readers interested in East Asia.

Takagi is particularly known for her careful and accessible presentations of classical texts and traditional tales. Her editions and translations, such as her work on “El cuento del cortador de bambú” and the anthology “Cuentos tradicionales de Japón,” combine philological rigor with an emphasis on narrative and cultural context, helping readers appreciate both the literary qualities of the works and the social and religious backgrounds from which they emerge. By selecting and framing stories that range from courtly literature to popular folklore, she has played a notable role in shaping how Japanese narrative traditions are read and received in Spanish.

In addition to her publishing work, Takagi’s academic and public activities—lectures, teaching, and participation in Asian studies networks—have positioned her as a bridge figure between Japan and the Spanish‑speaking world. Her scholarship often highlights the dynamics of cultural translation and the ways in which images of Japan are formed through literature, making her contributions significant not only for Japanese studies but also for broader discussions of cross‑cultural representation.
