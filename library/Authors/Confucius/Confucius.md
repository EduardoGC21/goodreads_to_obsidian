## Biography
Confucius (551–479 BCE) was a Chinese philosopher, teacher, and statesman whose ideas became the foundation of much of East Asian moral, social, and political thought. Born in the state of Lu during the late Spring and Autumn period, he came from modest circumstances but received a classical education and entered public service. Over time he became known for his integrity, commitment to ritual propriety, and belief that good government depends on the moral character of rulers. After periods of frustrated office-holding and political exile, he devoted himself to teaching a circle of disciples, traveling among various states to promote his vision of a harmonious, well-ordered society rooted in virtue and humane governance.

Confucius’s teachings, later compiled by his followers into texts such as the Analects and canonized alongside works by figures like Mencius, emphasized moral self-cultivation, filial piety, respect for tradition, and the centrality of righteous conduct in both private and public life. His thought deeply shaped family structures, education, and bureaucracy in China for over two millennia, and it influenced neighboring cultures in Korea, Japan, and Vietnam. Although many details of his life are semi-legendary, Confucius is widely remembered as a paradigmatic teacher whose relationships with his students and patrons exemplified the ethical bonds he taught, and whose legacy continues to inform debates about ethics, leadership, and social responsibility worldwide.
