## Biography
Confucius (551–479 BCE), known in Chinese as Kong Qiu or Kongzi, was a philosopher, teacher, and statesman from the state of Lu in ancient China. Born into a family of modest, once-aristocratic background, he experienced poverty and social instability in his youth, which shaped his conviction that moral cultivation and proper governance were essential for a harmonious society. He devoted his life to teaching a circle of disciples, emphasizing virtues such as humaneness (ren), righteousness (yi), ritual propriety (li), and filial piety as the foundations of personal integrity and public life.

Though Confucius held minor governmental posts, his political reforms were largely rejected during his lifetime, and he spent years traveling among different states, offering counsel to rulers and instructing students. After his death, his followers compiled his sayings and conversations in the "Analects," and his thought became the core of the Confucian tradition that shaped Chinese education, bureaucracy, and family ethics for over two millennia. Later texts such as the "Four Books" (including the Analects and the writings of Mencius) canonized his legacy and have been translated and studied worldwide, contributing to his enduring status as one of the most influential moral and political thinkers in East Asian and global intellectual history.
