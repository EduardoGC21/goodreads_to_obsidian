## Biography
Confucius (551–479 BCE) was a Chinese philosopher, teacher, and statesman whose ideas became the foundation of one of East Asia’s most influential intellectual and ethical traditions. Born in the state of Lu, he experienced early poverty and social instability, which shaped his conviction that moral cultivation and just governance were essential for a harmonious society. He worked in various minor government posts before becoming a respected teacher, attracting disciples who preserved and transmitted his teachings.

Confucius emphasized personal virtue, proper conduct in familial and social relationships, and the importance of ritual as a means of cultivating respect and harmony. His thought centers on ideals such as benevolence, righteousness, filial piety, and the moral responsibilities of rulers and officials. Though he never achieved lasting political power in his lifetime, his conversations with students and his interpretations of earlier classics were later compiled and systematized, giving rise to a tradition in which works like the Four Books became core texts for education and civil service examinations.

Over centuries, Confucianism shaped governance, education, and family life throughout China, Korea, Japan, and Vietnam, influencing law, social hierarchy, and everyday ethics. Confucius’ legacy is not that of a remote metaphysical thinker but of a moral educator concerned with how people should live together in concrete relationships—parent and child, ruler and subject, friend and friend—so that both individuals and societies can flourish.
