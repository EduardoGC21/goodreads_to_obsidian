## Biography
Stendhal, the pen name of Marie-Henri Beyle (1783–1842), was a French novelist, memoirist, and civil servant whose psychologically acute portraits of ambition, love, and disillusionment made him a major precursor of modern realist fiction. Born in Grenoble into a conservative, bourgeois family, he lost his mother at a young age and grew up in tension with his authoritarian father, experiences that shaped his skepticism toward social convention and his fascination with individual passion. As a young man he went to Paris, then followed Napoleon’s armies as an administrator and auditor, traveling widely in Italy and Germany and developing a lifelong love for Italian culture, opera, and art.

After the fall of Napoleon, Stendhal spent much of his life oscillating between Paris and Italy, working in various bureaucratic posts while writing under pseudonyms to shield his private life and political opinions. His best-known novel, "Le Rouge et le Noir" ("The Red and the Black"), uses the story of Julien Sorel’s rise and fall to dissect the hypocrisies of Restoration France and to explore ambition, class, and erotic desire with unusual psychological depth. Stendhal’s prose, often brisk and ironic, puzzled many contemporaries, and his books were only modestly successful in his lifetime. He died in Paris in 1842, largely uncelebrated, but later generations of writers—from Balzac and Flaubert to the modernists—hailed him as one of the foundational figures of the realist and psychological novel, admired for his clear-eyed portrayal of character and his candid treatment of love and inner life.
