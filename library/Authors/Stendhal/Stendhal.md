## Biography
Stendhal (1783–1842), born Marie-Henri Beyle in Grenoble, France, was a pioneering French novelist and a sharp observer of post-Revolutionary European society. Raised in a middle-class provincial family, he lost his mother young and developed a strong attachment to more sensitive, liberal-minded relatives, while rebelling against his conservative father. After moving to Paris, he became closely associated with Napoleonic circles and served in various administrative and military-related roles during the Napoleonic campaigns in Italy and Germany. These travels, especially in Italy, profoundly shaped his artistic sensibility and lifelong fascination with Italian culture, politics, and passion.

Following Napoleon’s fall, Stendhal lived largely as an expatriate, spending extended periods in Italy and later working as a French consul in Italian cities such as Trieste and Civitavecchia. He moved in cosmopolitan literary and artistic circles, yet often felt personally isolated, which sharpened his introspective and psychological approach to character. Stendhal’s major novels, including "Le Rouge et le Noir" ("The Red and the Black"), broke new ground in psychological realism, exploring ambition, desire, and the constraints of class and social convention in Restoration France. Little recognized in his own lifetime, he died relatively obscure in 1842, but was later acclaimed as a precursor of modern psychological and realist fiction, influencing writers such as Tolstoy, Proust, and Joyce through his precise, unsentimental depiction of inner life and social hypocrisy.
