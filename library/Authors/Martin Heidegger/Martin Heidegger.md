## Biography
Martin Heidegger (1889–1976) was a German philosopher whose work fundamentally reshaped 20th‑century continental thought, especially phenomenology, existentialism, and hermeneutics. Born in Messkirch, in the Baden region of Germany, he studied theology and then philosophy, eventually becoming a student and later assistant of Edmund Husserl. Heidegger’s early masterpiece, "Being and Time" (1927), sought to renew the question of the meaning of Being through an analysis of human existence (Dasein), influencing later figures such as Jean-Paul Sartre, Hannah Arendt, and Hans-Georg Gadamer. His teaching career was centered at the University of Freiburg, with a period at Marburg, and he became a major intellectual force in the German academy between the World Wars.

Heidegger’s life and legacy are deeply marked by his 1933–34 rectorship at Freiburg and his membership in the Nazi Party, a political entanglement that has generated intense controversy and continues to shape debates about the ethical and political implications of his thought. After World War II he was briefly banned from teaching but gradually resumed seminars and lectures, including later reflections collected in works such as "Letter on Humanism" and various seminars on pre-Socratic thinkers like Heraclitus. Throughout his career he maintained complex relationships with students and contemporaries—most notably Arendt and Gadamer—who both drew on and critically reworked his ideas. Heidegger died in his hometown of Messkirch, leaving a vast body of writings that remain central, yet contested, in philosophy, literary theory, theology, and political thought.
