## Biography
Martin Heidegger (1889–1976) was a German philosopher widely regarded as one of the most influential figures in 20th‑century continental thought. Born in Messkirch in southwestern Germany, he studied theology and philosophy before becoming a student and later assistant of Edmund Husserl, whose phenomenology deeply shaped Heidegger’s own work. Heidegger’s major work *Being and Time* recast the question of the meaning of being by analyzing human existence (Dasein) in terms of temporality, care, and being‑toward‑death, profoundly influencing existentialism, hermeneutics, theology, and literary theory.

Heidegger held academic positions at the University of Freiburg and the University of Marburg and became rector of Freiburg in 1933. His involvement with National Socialism during the early 1930s, including his brief membership in the Nazi Party and his rectoral address, has made his legacy ethically and politically controversial, sparking ongoing debate about the relation between his politics and his philosophy. He maintained complex personal and intellectual relationships, including with Husserl and his former student Hannah Arendt, and continued to develop his thought after the 1930s through lectures and seminars, such as those on Heraclitus, that turned increasingly toward language, poetry, and a critique of modern technology.

Across his later essays, letters, and seminars, Heidegger reflected on humanism, metaphysics, and the history of philosophy, often revisiting and reinterpreting themes from his earlier work. His influence extends across philosophy, theology, literary criticism, and political theory, while his political choices continue to prompt critical reassessment of how his philosophical insights relate to the historical context of 20th‑century Germany.
