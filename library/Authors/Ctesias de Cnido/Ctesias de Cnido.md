## Biography
Ctesias of Cnidus was an ancient Greek physician and historian from the city of Cnidus in Caria, on the coast of Asia Minor. Trained in the medical traditions of his home city, he entered the service of the Achaemenid Persian court and became court physician to King Artaxerxes II in the late 5th and early 4th centuries BCE. His position placed him close to the center of imperial power, giving him access to royal archives, officials, and first‑hand accounts of Persian politics and warfare.

While at court, Ctesias composed a large historical work on the Persian Empire, commonly known as the *Persiká* ("Histories of Persia"), of which only fragments and later summaries survive. Writing in Greek for a Greek audience, he offered an alternative to Herodotus, sometimes contradicting him and presenting himself as a more accurate witness to Persian affairs. He also wrote a companion work on India (*Indiká*), mixing court information, travelers’ tales, and marvels. Though modern scholars treat many of his reports with caution, Ctesias remains significant as a cultural mediator between the Greek world and the Achaemenid Empire, illustrating how Greek intellectuals at the Persian court interpreted and reshaped Near Eastern history for consumption back in the Greek cities.
