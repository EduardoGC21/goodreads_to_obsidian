## Biography
Thomas William Hazen Rolleston (1857–1920) was an Irish writer, poet, translator, and literary critic whose work helped popularize Celtic culture for English-speaking audiences in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Born in Shinrone, County Offaly, he was educated at St Columba’s College and Trinity College Dublin, entering a vibrant intellectual milieu that was increasingly preoccupied with Irish cultural revival and national identity.

Rolleston’s career was strikingly varied: he founded the *Dublin University Review* in 1885, worked as a journalist and civil servant, and lived for periods in Dublin, County Wicklow, Germany, and London. Closely connected to the Irish Literary Revival, he moved in the same circles as W. B. Yeats and other writers of the Rhymers’ Club and the Irish Literary Society, sometimes as collaborator and sometimes as rival. Beyond his poetry and criticism, he became best known for accessible retellings of Celtic and especially Irish myth, such as *Myths and Legends of the Celtic Race* (often issued in translation as “Mitos y leyendas Celtas”), which framed ancient narratives in a form suitable for modern readers while arguing for the enduring importance of Celtic heritage.

Through his essays, translations, and mythological studies, Rolleston contributed to shaping early twentieth‑century perceptions of Celtic history, religion, and legend, helping to embed Irish myth in the broader imagination of Europe and the English‑speaking world. He spent his later years in Hampstead, London, where he continued to write and engage with literary and political questions until his death in 1920.
