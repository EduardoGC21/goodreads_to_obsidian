## Biography
Thomas William Hazen Rolleston (1857–1920) was an Irish writer, critic, poet, and translator closely associated with the Irish Literary Revival. Born in Glasshouse, near Shinrone, County Offaly, into a legal and professional family, he was educated at St Columba’s College and Trinity College Dublin, where his literary ambitions first emerged. After marrying Edith Caroline de Burgh, he spent several years in Germany, deepening his interest in German philosophy and literature and beginning a notable engagement with European thought, including an early attempt to translate Walt Whitman into German.

Returning to Ireland in the 1880s, Rolleston co‑founded the "Dublin University Review" and became an important organiser and mediator in Irish literary circles. He helped to launch the careers of writers such as W. B. Yeats, was a founding member and first secretary of the Irish Literary Society in London, and took part in the Gaelic League and the broader cultural nationalist movement, even as his own politics moved toward a belief in the value of economic development and the imperial connection. Beyond poetry and criticism, he played a practical role in Irish public life through work with the Irish Industries Association, the Arts and Crafts Society of Ireland, and agricultural and cooperative initiatives. His later years were spent mainly in London, where he edited for the Times Literary Supplement and worked in government information services during World War I.

Rolleston’s most enduring reputation rests on his retellings and syntheses of Celtic and Irish tradition, including works such as "Myths and Legends of the Celtic Race" and related volumes on Irish hero cycles. These books, along with collections like "A Treasury of Irish Poetry in the English Tongue," helped shape popular understanding of Celtic myth and Irish literary heritage for English‑speaking audiences. His personal life included two marriages, to Edith de Burgh and later Maud Brooke (daughter of the critic and clergyman Stopford A. Brooke), and a large family that linked him to other literary figures of his time. He died in Hampstead, London, in 1920, leaving a legacy as both a cultural mediator and a key interpreter of Ireland’s mythic past.
