## Biography
Mika Toimi Waltari (1908–1979) was a Finnish writer whose historical novels made him one of Finland’s best-known authors internationally. Born in Helsinki, he lost his Lutheran pastor father at the age of five and grew up in modest circumstances supported by his widowed mother and extended family. As a child he witnessed the turmoil of the Finnish Civil War, an experience that sharpened his historical awareness and sense of human conflict. He initially enrolled at the University of Helsinki to study theology, but soon turned to philosophy, aesthetics, and literature, graduating in 1929 while already publishing poems and stories.

In the late 1920s Waltari spent time in Paris, where he wrote his breakthrough novel about bohemian life, establishing himself as a major voice of his generation. Over the following decades he worked intensely as a journalist, critic, editor, and versatile author, producing novels, novellas, crime fiction, plays, essays, travelogues, screenplays, and comics texts. His most famous work, the historical novel "The Egyptian" ("Sinuhé, el egipcio"), published in 1945, used ancient Egypt to explore moral and spiritual crises in a materialist world and brought him worldwide fame, including a major film adaptation. Waltari’s historical novels, set in civilizations from ancient Egypt to the late Roman Empire, are marked by journeys, encounters with power, and a deep, often pessimistic reflection on the fragility of human ideals.

Waltari married Marjatta Luukkonen in 1931, and they had a daughter, Satu, who also became a writer, illustrating the literary continuity in his family life. Behind his strict work discipline he struggled with manic-depressive episodes, often falling into depression after completing a book and only writing during more manic phases. His achievements were widely recognized: he received the Finnish State literature award multiple times, became a member of the Finnish Academy, and was granted an honorary doctorate. Waltari spent his later years revisiting his earlier works and mentoring younger writers, remaining an influential cultural figure in Finland until his death in Helsinki in 1979.
