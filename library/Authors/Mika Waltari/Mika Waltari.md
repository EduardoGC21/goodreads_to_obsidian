## Biography
Mika Waltari (1908–1979) was a Finnish novelist, journalist, and playwright best known internationally for his sweeping historical epics. Born in Helsinki, he lost his father at a young age, an event that shaped both his inner life and his early maturity. He studied theology and later philosophy at the University of Helsinki but soon gravitated toward literature and journalism, becoming a prolific writer in multiple genres, from detective fiction and screenplays to essays and historical novels.

Waltari achieved global fame with the historical novel "Sinuhe, the Egyptian" (originally published in Finnish in 1945), a richly detailed narrative set in ancient Egypt that reflects his deep interest in the rise and fall of civilizations, moral ambiguity, and the burdens of power. His extensive travels across Europe and the Mediterranean before and after World War II informed the cosmopolitan outlook and meticulous historical texture of his work. Waltari is widely regarded as one of Finland’s most significant 20th‑century authors, whose novels helped bring Finnish literature to a broad international readership.

Throughout his life, Waltari maintained a complex relationship with questions of faith, ethics, and human responsibility, themes that recur in his historical characters’ struggles. He married Marjatta Luukkonen in 1931, and they had one daughter. Esteemed at home and abroad, he received numerous literary honors in Finland and remained a central cultural figure until his death in Helsinki in 1979.
