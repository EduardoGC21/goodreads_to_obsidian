## Biography
Stefan Zweig (1881–1942) was an Austrian writer, biographer, and essayist who became one of the most widely read authors in Europe between the two world wars. Born into a wealthy Jewish family in Vienna, he was educated in philosophy and literature and moved in cosmopolitan intellectual circles that included Rainer Maria Rilke, Sigmund Freud, and many leading artists of his time. Zweig wrote poetry, novellas, and psychologically acute short fiction, but he gained particular renown for his sensitive biographies and character studies of historical figures, as well as for works like "Momentos estelares de la humanidad" that turned key turning points in history into vivid literary miniatures. His work is marked by a deep humanism, a fascination with moral choice, and an acute awareness of the fragility of European civilization.

A committed pacifist during and after World War I, Zweig grew increasingly disillusioned as nationalism and totalitarianism spread across Europe. As a Jewish intellectual, he was forced into exile after the rise of Nazism, living successively in Britain, the United States, and finally Brazil. Cut off from his linguistic and cultural homeland and despairing over the destruction of the Europe he had idealized, he and his second wife, Lotte Altmann, died by suicide in Petrópolis in 1942. Zweig’s legacy endures through his psychologically rich novellas, his finely crafted essays on the tragedies and glories of European history, and his poignant memoir of a lost world, which together have secured his place as a major voice of 20th‑century literature.
