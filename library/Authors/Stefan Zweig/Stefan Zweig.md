## Biography
Stefan Zweig (1881–1942) was an Austrian writer, biographer, and essayist born into a wealthy Jewish family in Vienna, then part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Educated in philosophy and literature, he moved in prominent European intellectual circles and formed lasting relationships with figures such as Romain Rolland, Sigmund Freud, and Rainer Maria Rilke. Before and after World War I he became one of the most widely read authors in the German language, known for psychologically acute novellas, biographies, and historical portraits that explored human passion, moral conflict, and the fragility of civilization.

A committed humanist and pacifist, Zweig was deeply shaken by the rise of nationalism and fascism. As a Jewish intellectual, he was forced into exile in the 1930s, living in the United Kingdom, the United States, and finally Brazil. In works like the historical miniatures later known in Spanish as "Momentos estelares de la humanidad" and in novellas such as "Mendel el de los libros," he combined empathy with a keen sense of historical turning points and the fate of individuals swept up in them. Feeling hopeless about Europe’s future during World War II, Zweig and his second wife, Lotte Altmann, died by joint suicide in Petrópolis, Brazil, in 1942, leaving behind a body of work that remains influential for its elegant prose and profound meditation on the collapse of the old European world.
