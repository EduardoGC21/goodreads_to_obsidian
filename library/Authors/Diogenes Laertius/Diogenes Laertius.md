## Biography
Diogenes Laertius was an ancient Greek biographer of philosophers, best known as the author of *Lives and Opinions of Eminent Philosophers*, a comprehensive collection of biographies, anecdotes, and doctrinal summaries of Greek thinkers. Very little is known about his own life: even his birthplace, education, and social milieu remain matters of scholarly conjecture. Nevertheless, his work preserves an enormous amount of information about earlier philosophers, some of which survives only through his compilations.

His book functions less as a philosophical treatise and more as an encyclopedic record of stories, lineages, and sayings, often emphasizing the personalities, habits, and scandals of philosophers as much as their doctrines. Because he drew on a wide range of earlier sources—many now lost—Diogenes Laertius became a crucial intermediary between the classical and Hellenistic philosophical traditions and later readers in late antiquity and beyond. His influence is especially significant for the study of schools such as the Stoics, Epicureans, and Cynics, for whom he is sometimes our most substantial or only narrative source.

Though he is not regarded as an original philosopher, his significance lies in his role as a transmitter of the ancient philosophical heritage. Later scholars, humanists, and modern historians of philosophy have depended heavily on his collection to reconstruct the lives, successions, and interpersonal relationships of major and minor figures alike. In this way, Diogenes Laertius stands as a central, if personally obscure, figure in the preservation of classical intellectual history.
