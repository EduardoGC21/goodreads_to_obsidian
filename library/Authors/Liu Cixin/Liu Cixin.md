## Biography
Liu Cixin (1963– ) is a Chinese science fiction author widely credited with bringing Chinese hard science fiction to global prominence. Born in Yangquan, Shanxi province, he grew up during a period of rapid social and technological change, experiences that later shaped his interest in grand historical cycles and the long-term fate of civilizations. Trained as a power plant engineer, Liu worked for many years at a power station in Shanxi, writing fiction in his spare time before becoming a full-time author.

Liu’s work is known for combining rigorous scientific speculation with vast cosmic scales and a generally sober, often pessimistic view of humanity’s place in the universe. The trilogy that begins with "The Three-Body Problem" and continues through "The Dark Forest" and "Death’s End" established him as a major figure in world science fiction, winning major awards such as the Hugo Award and influencing both Chinese and international discussions about technology, deterrence, and first-contact scenarios. Though he tends to keep his private life relatively low-profile, Liu has become an important cultural figure in contemporary China, helping to legitimize science fiction as a serious literary form and inspiring a new generation of Chinese SF writers and readers.
