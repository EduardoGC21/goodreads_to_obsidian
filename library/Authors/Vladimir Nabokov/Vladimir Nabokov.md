## Biography
Vladimir Nabokov (1899–1977) was a Russian-American novelist, poet, translator, and literary critic, born into an aristocratic, liberal family in St. Petersburg in the final years of the Russian Empire. Raised trilingually in Russian, English, and French, he received an elite education and developed an early passion for literature, chess problems, and lepidoptery (the study of butterflies). The Russian Revolution forced his family into exile, first to Crimea and then to Western Europe, an upheaval that shaped his lifelong themes of displacement, memory, and the fragility of personal identity.

In the 1920s and 1930s Nabokov lived primarily in Berlin and then Paris, writing significant Russian-language novels under the name Vladimir Sirin while supporting himself with various modest jobs. After his father was assassinated by political extremists and Europe grew increasingly dangerous, Nabokov emigrated with his wife Véra and their son to the United States in 1940. There he gradually transitioned to writing in English, producing works that combined intricate wordplay, narrative experimentation, and psychological depth. His controversial novel "Lolita" brought him international fame and financial independence, allowing him to leave his teaching posts and eventually settle in Switzerland.

Nabokov’s life was marked by a strong, enduring partnership with his wife Véra, who served as his closest reader, editor, and protector of his time and work. Alongside his literary career, he was a respected lepidopterist who published scientific papers and worked at the Museum of Comparative Zoology at Harvard. His writings—across Russian and English—are now regarded as central to 20th-century literature, notable for their sophisticated narrative structures, ironic humor, and exploration of exile, artifice, and consciousness.
