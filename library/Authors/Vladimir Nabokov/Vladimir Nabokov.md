## Biography
Vladimir Nabokov (1899–1977) was a Russian-born novelist, poet, translator, and literary scholar, born into an affluent, liberal aristocratic family in Saint Petersburg in the then-Russian Empire. Educated by private tutors and fluent in several languages from childhood, he fled Russia with his family after the 1917 Revolution, eventually settling in Western Europe. He studied at Trinity College, Cambridge, then lived largely in Berlin and Paris, writing in Russian and supporting himself through teaching, translations, and chess problems while processing the experience of exile and the loss of his homeland.

In 1940 Nabokov emigrated again, this time to the United States, where he reinvented himself as an English-language writer and academic. He taught at Wellesley College and later at Cornell University, and during this period produced some of his most significant works, including the controversial novel "Lolita," which brought him international fame for its daring subject matter, intricate structure, and innovative use of language. Nabokov developed a reputation as a meticulous craftsman of prose, a master of narrative play, and a sharp, sometimes combative critic.

Later in life, Nabokov moved to Switzerland, where he continued to write and revise his works, often in close collaboration with his wife, Véra, who served as his literary assistant and protector of his legacy. His son Dmitri also became an important translator and guardian of his father’s work. Nabokov’s life and career traversed three major cultures—Russian, European émigré, and American—and his legacy is that of a profoundly cosmopolitan writer whose inventive style, multilingual sensitivity, and exploration of memory, identity, and art have made him one of the most influential authors of the twentieth century.
