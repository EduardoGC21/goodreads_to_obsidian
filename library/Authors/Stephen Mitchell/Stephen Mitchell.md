## Biography
Stephen Mitchell (1948–2024) was a British historian and epigrapher whose work reshaped modern understanding of the eastern provinces of the Roman Empire, especially Anatolia in the Hellenistic, Roman, and Byzantine periods. Born into an academically oriented family in Oxford—his father a philosophy tutor and his mother a classicist—he was educated in literae humaniores at St John’s College, Oxford, and completed a DPhil on the history and archaeology of Galatia. This grounding in both textual and material evidence defined his later scholarship, which combined close reading of inscriptions with broad historical synthesis.

Mitchell taught at Bristol before holding long-term posts at Swansea University, where he became professor of ancient history, and later at the University of Exeter as Leverhulme Professor of Hellenistic Culture. He was deeply involved with the British Institute at Ankara, serving in multiple leadership roles and helping turn it into a major research center for the ancient and modern history of Turkey. Internationally, he was a leading figure in Greek and Latin epigraphy, serving as president of key professional societies. His major works, including studies of Anatolia and his wide-ranging survey "A History of the Later Roman Empire, AD 284–641: The Transformation of the Ancient World," made him a central voice in debates about late Roman political structures, provincial society, and the transition from paganism to Christianity.

Beyond his institutional roles, Mitchell was known for mentoring younger scholars and fostering collaborative fieldwork and publication projects across Europe and Turkey. His scholarship is characterized by clarity of argument and a careful integration of literary, legal, and archaeological evidence, which has made his books standard reference works for students and specialists alike. His death in 2024 marked the loss of one of the most influential historians of Anatolia and late antiquity of his generation.
