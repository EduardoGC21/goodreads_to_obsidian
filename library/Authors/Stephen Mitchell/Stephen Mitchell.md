## Biography
Stephen Mitchell (1948–2024) was a British historian and epigrapher who became one of the leading authorities on the history of Anatolia and the later Roman Empire. Educated at St John’s College, Oxford, where he studied literae humaniores and completed a DPhil on the history and archaeology of Galatia in 1975, he combined literary, archaeological, and epigraphic evidence in ways that reshaped understanding of Asia Minor from the Hellenistic through the Byzantine periods. His long academic career included posts at Bristol and Oxford before he became a lecturer, and later professor, in ancient history at Swansea, and subsequently Leverhulme Professor of Hellenistic Culture at the University of Exeter, where he also founded a Centre for Hellenistic Culture and Society.

Mitchell’s research ranged from regional studies, such as his work on Galatia, Cremna, and Pisidian Antioch, to wider syntheses of religion and empire. He directed the Pisidia Survey Project, advancing knowledge of the historical geography and urbanism of central Anatolia, and played a key role in major epigraphic enterprises, including corpora of Greek and Latin inscriptions from Ankara. His book “A History of the Later Roman Empire, AD 284–641: The Transformation of the Ancient World” distilled decades of research into a comprehensive survey that has become a standard reference for the political, military, social, and religious history of late antiquity.

Beyond his publications, Mitchell was deeply involved in scholarly institutions and international collaboration. A long‑standing member and later chair and vice‑president of the British Institute at Ankara, he helped transform it into a major hub for research on Turkey’s past and present. He served as president of both the British Epigraphy Society and the Association Internationale d’Épigraphie Grecque et Latine, and was elected a Fellow of the British Academy in 2002. Remembered as a generous mentor and collaborator, he built an influential network of students and colleagues whose work continues his legacy in the study of the ancient Mediterranean and Near East.
