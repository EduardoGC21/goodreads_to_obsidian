## Biography
Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900) was a German philosopher, philologist, and cultural critic whose work profoundly reshaped modern thought. Born in Röcken, in the Prussian province of Saxony, he was a precocious student of classical languages and became, in his mid-twenties, one of the youngest professors ever appointed at the University of Basel. Poor health forced his early resignation, and he spent most of his later life living modestly in Swiss, Italian, and German towns, writing in relative obscurity and supported in part by a small pension and the care of family, especially his devoted sister Elisabeth. A mental collapse in 1889 left him incapacitated; he spent his final years under his mother’s and then his sister’s guardianship until his death in 1900.

Nietzsche’s writings—among them "Thus Spoke Zarathustra," "Beyond Good and Evil," "On the Genealogy of Morality" and "The Antichrist"—challenged traditional morality, Christianity, and the philosophical foundations of Western culture. He explored themes such as the death of God, the will to power, the revaluation of values, and the ideal of the free spirit who creates meaning in a world without transcendent certainties. Though his ideas were later distorted, especially by nationalist and authoritarian movements that misused his work, Nietzsche himself was sharply critical of anti-Semitism and German nationalism. Today he is recognized as a central figure in existentialism, hermeneutics, and post-structuralism, and as a thinker whose provocative style and radical critique continue to influence philosophy, literature, psychology, and cultural theory.
