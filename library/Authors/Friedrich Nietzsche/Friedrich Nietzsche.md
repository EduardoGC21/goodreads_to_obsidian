## Biography
Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900) was a German philosopher, classical philologist, and cultural critic whose work profoundly reshaped modern thought. Trained as a philologist, he became a professor at an unusually young age but soon left academia due to ill health and dissatisfaction with scholarly life. Living much of his adult life in relative isolation and poor health, he wrote intensely original works that challenged the foundations of morality, religion, and Western culture, developing ideas such as the will to power, the death of God, and the Übermensch.

Nietzsche’s philosophy was deeply shaped by his fraught relationship with Christianity, his admiration and later rejection of Richard Wagner, and his engagement with Greek culture and tragedy. Works like “Thus Spoke Zarathustra,” “Beyond Good and Evil,” “On the Genealogy of Morality,” “The Birth of Tragedy,” and “The Antichrist” articulate his critique of conventional morality, herd thinking, and pessimistic resignation, while exploring the possibility of creating new values in a disenchanted world. His style combined aphorism, irony, and prophetic rhetoric, making his writing both philosophically challenging and literary.

In 1889 Nietzsche suffered a mental collapse, after which he never recovered his intellectual capacities and spent his remaining years under the care of his mother and then his sister, Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche, who later edited and sometimes distorted his writings. Despite early neglect and later political misappropriations of his ideas, Nietzsche came to be recognized as one of the most influential thinkers of the 19th century, shaping existentialism, psychoanalysis, post-structuralism, and modern critiques of culture and morality.
