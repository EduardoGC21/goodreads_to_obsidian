## Biography
Percy Bysshe Shelley (1792–1822) was an English Romantic poet whose radical politics, unconventional beliefs, and lyrical genius made him one of the most influential writers of the 19th century. Born into a wealthy Sussex family, he was educated at Eton and briefly at Oxford, from which he was expelled for publishing a pamphlet defending atheism. Rejecting the expectations of his aristocratic background, Shelley eloped as a teenager, embraced republican and egalitarian ideas, and lived much of his short life in self-imposed exile across Europe, often in financial difficulty but surrounded by a circle of writers and intellectuals.

Shelley’s poetry combines intense emotional vision with a passionate commitment to political and spiritual liberation. Works such as "Prometheus Unbound" recast myth in visionary terms to explore resistance to tyranny and the possibility of moral and social renewal, while his shorter lyrics show a profound sensitivity to nature, love, and transience. His life was deeply intertwined with that of Mary Shelley, author of "Frankenstein," with whom he eloped and shared a complex, often tragic domestic life marked by the deaths of several children. Shelley's friendships with fellow Romantics like Lord Byron further shaped his literary and personal world.

Shelley died at age 29 in a boating accident off the coast of Italy, leaving behind a body of work that was only fully appreciated after his death. His "Selected Poems and Prose" reveal a writer who fused philosophical inquiry with musical language, and plays such as "The Cenci" show his attempts to grapple with power, violence, and moral responsibility. Posthumously, Shelley became a symbol of artistic idealism and radical thought, influencing generations of poets, political thinkers, and readers drawn to his vision of human freedom and moral progress.
