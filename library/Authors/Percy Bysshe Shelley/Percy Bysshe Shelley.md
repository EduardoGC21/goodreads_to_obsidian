## Biography
Percy Bysshe Shelley (1792–1822) was an English Romantic poet and radical thinker whose life and work challenged the political, religious, and social conventions of his age. Born into a wealthy Sussex family, he rejected his aristocratic background early, embracing atheism, republicanism, and social reform. Expelled from Oxford for a pamphlet defending atheism, he became an enduring symbol of intellectual rebellion and personal nonconformity.

Shelley’s life was marked by intense personal relationships and restless wandering across Britain and continental Europe. He eloped first with Harriet Westbrook and later formed a famous partnership with Mary Godwin, who became Mary Shelley, author of "Frankenstein." Their circle included Lord Byron and other leading Romantics, and together they forged a new, idealistic vision of art and politics. In works such as "Prometheus Unbound" and "The Cenci," Shelley explored themes of tyranny, moral courage, and human perfectibility, uniting lyrical beauty with philosophical depth.

Though he died at age 29 in a boating accident off the coast of Italy, Shelley’s reputation grew steadily after his death. His passionate defense of liberty, imaginative engagement with revolution and injustice, and innovative lyric style made him a central figure in English literature. Later generations saw him as both a martyr to his ideals and a pioneering voice for individual freedom, social justice, and the transformative power of the imagination.
