## Biography
Daron Acemoğlu (b. 1967) is a Turkish-born economist whose work has deeply shaped modern understanding of how political and economic institutions influence long-run development. Raised and educated in Istanbul before pursuing graduate studies abroad, he combined rigorous economic theory with historical and empirical analysis to explain why some societies achieve prosperity while others remain trapped in poverty. His research emphasizes how inclusive institutions, constraints on elites, and broad-based political participation are critical for sustained growth.

Over his career, Acemoğlu has been closely associated with the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), where he has mentored generations of economists and collaborated widely across disciplines. He is best known to a broader audience for co‑authoring "Why Nations Fail: The Origins of Power, Prosperity, and Poverty," which synthesizes his academic work into an accessible account of state-building, colonial legacies, and institutional change. An influential voice in debates on democracy, technology, and inequality, he has received numerous honors and awards, and his ideas inform policy discussions in both advanced and developing economies.

Acemoğlu’s significance extends beyond his publications: he is part of a global intellectual network examining how institutions evolve under pressure from social conflict, technological disruption, and globalization. His collaborations and exchanges with fellow economists, political scientists, and historians have helped bridge disciplinary divides and brought institutional economics into mainstream public debate, making him one of the leading figures in contemporary political economy.
