## Biography
Daron Acemoğlu (1967–) is a Turkish-born economist whose work has profoundly shaped contemporary understanding of how political institutions and power structures drive long‑run economic development. Born in Istanbul, he studied at the Galatasaray High School and then moved to the United Kingdom for university, earning his undergraduate degree from the University of York and his Ph.D. in economics from the London School of Economics. He later joined the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT), where he became a leading figure in the fields of political economy, development economics, and institutional economics.

Acemoğlu’s research focuses on how inclusive versus extractive political and economic institutions emerge, persist, and affect prosperity, themes that are central to his influential book "Why Nations Fail: The Origins of Power, Prosperity, and Poverty" (co‑authored with James A. Robinson). Across his career, he has combined rigorous formal modeling with historical and empirical analysis to argue that differences in institutions—rather than geography, culture, or simple policy choices—largely explain why some societies become wealthy and democratic while others remain poor and authoritarian. His work has had broad interdisciplinary impact in economics, political science, history, and public policy.

Over the years, Acemoğlu has received numerous prestigious awards, including the John Bates Clark Medal, and has been elected to several learned societies, reflecting his status as one of the most cited and influential economists of his generation. He has collaborated closely with scholars such as James A. Robinson and Simon Johnson, often using historical episodes—from colonial Latin America to modern authoritarian regimes—to illuminate how conflicts over power shape institutional paths. Through both academic research and public engagement, he has become a central voice in debates on democracy, inequality, state capacity, and the future of work in technologically advanced economies.
