## Biography
James Baldwin (1924–1987) was an American novelist, essayist, and social critic born in Harlem, New York City. Raised in poverty by his mother and strict stepfather, a Pentecostal minister, Baldwin became a teenage preacher before leaving the pulpit to pursue writing. His early experiences with racism, religion, and family life in Harlem deeply shaped his literary voice, which blended personal testimony with sharp moral and political insight.

In the 1950s Baldwin moved to France to escape American racism and homophobia, yet his work remained intensely focused on the United States, the Black experience, and the complexities of identity. Novels such as "Go Tell It on the Mountain" and "Giovanni’s Room" broke new ground by portraying both Black life and same-sex desire with psychological depth and compassion. His essays, including those in "Notes of a Native Son" and "The Fire Next Time," made him a key intellectual figure in the civil rights era, engaging publicly with leaders like Martin Luther King Jr. and Malcolm X. Across his life, Baldwin’s significance lies in his ability to illuminate the intersections of race, sexuality, faith, and belonging, challenging America to confront its history and its conscience.
