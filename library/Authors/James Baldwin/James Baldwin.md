## Biography
James Baldwin (1924–1987) was an American novelist, essayist, and social critic whose work probed the intersections of race, sexuality, religion, and identity in the United States and abroad. Born in Harlem, New York, and raised in a strict religious household, he briefly served as a teenage preacher, an experience that deeply informed the spiritual and moral struggles depicted in works like "Go Tell It on the Mountain." Leaving the pulpit and later the U.S. for extended periods in France and other parts of Europe, he sought both artistic freedom and respite from American racism, while continuing to write incisively about his homeland.

Baldwin became one of the most powerful voices of the Civil Rights era, using essays, novels, and public appearances to confront the psychological and social realities of racism and homophobia. In works such as "Giovanni’s Room" he explored queer desire and exile with unprecedented nuance, challenging prevailing norms about sexuality long before LGBTQ+ discourse was mainstream. He was closely associated with figures like Martin Luther King Jr., Malcolm X, and Medgar Evers, engaging them in dialogue while also critiquing American liberalism’s limitations.

Across his career, Baldwin’s eloquent and prophetic prose influenced generations of writers, activists, and thinkers, making him a central figure in American letters and global discussions on justice and human dignity. His legacy endures in ongoing debates about race, sexuality, and democracy, where his insights remain strikingly contemporary.
