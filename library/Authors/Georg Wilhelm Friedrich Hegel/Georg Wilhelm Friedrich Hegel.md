## Biography
Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831) was a German philosopher whose work became one of the central pillars of 19th‑century European thought. Born in Stuttgart in the Duchy of Württemberg, he studied theology and philosophy at the Tübinger Stift, where he formed important friendships with Friedrich Hölderlin and Friedrich Wilhelm Joseph Schelling. After periods as a tutor and journalist, he held academic posts in Jena, Heidelberg, and ultimately Berlin, becoming one of the most prominent philosophers of his time. His life unfolded against the backdrop of the French Revolution and the Napoleonic Wars, events that deeply shaped his reflections on history, freedom, and the modern state.

Hegel’s philosophy is best known for its dialectical method, its account of the development of self-consciousness, and its systematic attempt to unify logic, nature, and spirit within a single conceptual framework. In works such as the *Phenomenology of Spirit* (to which the Spanish title "Fenomenología del espíritu" refers), he traced the gradual unfolding of consciousness and human freedom through various historical and cultural forms. His lectures on the philosophy of history, art, religion, and law further elaborated how reason manifests in the institutions and practices of human life.

Although Hegel was controversial in his own day—drawing both devoted followers and sharp critics—his influence on later philosophy, theology, political theory, and the social sciences has been immense. Marxism, existentialism, phenomenology, and many strands of continental philosophy developed in dialogue with or in opposition to his ideas. Hegel’s legacy thus extends far beyond his immediate circle of students in Berlin, shaping debates about history, freedom, and rationality up to the present.
