## Biography
Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770–1831) was a German philosopher born in Stuttgart, in the Duchy of Württemberg. Educated in theology and philosophy at the Tübinger Stift alongside contemporaries such as Friedrich Hölderlin and Friedrich Schelling, he developed an early interest in classical culture, religion, and politics. After years working as a private tutor and lecturer, he began to formulate his distinctive idealist system, which aimed to understand reality as the unfolding of Geist (Spirit) through history, culture, and thought.

Hegel’s major works—including *Phenomenology of Spirit* ("Fenomenología del espíritu"), *Science of Logic*, and the *Encyclopaedia of the Philosophical Sciences*—sought to show how consciousness, social institutions, art, religion, and philosophy develop dialectically: through conflicts and their resolutions. His philosophy presents history as a rational process in which freedom gradually becomes explicit and realized in ethical and political life, especially in modern constitutional states. As a professor in Berlin, he became one of the most influential thinkers of his time, shaping subsequent movements such as Marxism, existentialism, phenomenology, and various strands of social and political theory.

Hegel’s personal life was relatively private compared with his intellectual legacy, but he maintained important relationships with students and colleagues who carried his ideas forward and often contested them. After his death in Berlin in 1831, his followers split into “Right” and “Left” Hegelians, debating the religious and political implications of his work. Despite these divisions, Hegel’s ambitious attempt to synthesize logic, metaphysics, history, and society into a single philosophical system has secured his reputation as one of the central figures of Western philosophy.
