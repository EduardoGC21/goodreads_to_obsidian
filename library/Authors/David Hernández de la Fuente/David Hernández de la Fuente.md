## Biography
David Hernández de la Fuente (born 1974, Madrid) is a Spanish writer, translator, and scholar of the ancient world whose career bridges academic research and literary creation. Trained at the Complutense University of Madrid, he earned degrees in Classics, Spanish Philology, and Law, and completed doctorates in Classical Philology and in Social History of Antiquity. His academic path has taken him through positions at Charles III University of Madrid, the University of Potsdam, Spain’s National University of Distance Education (UNED), and visiting appointments at institutions such as Columbia University, the University of Florence, Paris Nanterre, and the Free University of Berlin. He is currently Professor of Greek Philology in the Department of Classics at the Complutense University of Madrid, where his work focuses on Greek religion and mythology, late antique Greek literature and society, the history of Platonism, and the classical tradition.

Hernández de la Fuente has played a notable role in making classical culture accessible to wider audiences while maintaining rigorous scholarship. Alongside editing and translating authors like Plato, Plutarch, Pindar, and Nonnus of Panopolis, he has published numerous essays and monographs on the reception of classical antiquity in modern culture, including studies of Pythagoras, Byzantium, and Greek political thought. His book on Pythagoras was distinguished as a “Book of the Year” by the cultural supplement of El País, and works such as "Oráculos griegos" illustrate his interest in how ancient religious practices and prophetic discourse shaped Greek society and continue to inform our understanding of myth and belief. He also serves on the editorial boards of several scholarly series and is editor-in-chief of the journal "Estudios Bizantinos," contributing to international research networks on late antiquity and classical studies.

In parallel with his academic achievements, Hernández de la Fuente is an accomplished fiction writer whose novels and short stories have been recognized with major literary awards, including the Young Artists Prize of the Community of Madrid and the Valencia Prize for Narrative. This dual vocation situates him at a crossroads between scholarship and creative writing, giving his work on the ancient world a distinctive narrative sensibility and ensuring that his studies of Greek religion, mythology, and literature resonate with both specialists and a broader reading public.
