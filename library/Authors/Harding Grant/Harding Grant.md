## Biography
Harding Grant was a British barrister and legal writer active in the early to mid-nineteenth century. Known primarily for his expertise in the practice of the High Court of Chancery, he authored influential multi-volume manuals such as “The practice of the High Court of Chancery” and its later revised editions, together with “Advice to trustees, and to those who appoint to that office.” These works systematized equity procedure, official forms, and costs at a time when Chancery practice was intricate and evolving, and their repeated editions suggest that practitioners relied on his guidance in everyday legal work.

In 1830 Grant also entered contemporary religious and literary debate by publishing “Lord Byron's Cain, a Mystery: With Notes, Wherein the Religion of the Bible Is Considered in Reference to Acknowledged Philosophy and Reason,” a lengthy commentary that reads Byron's drama and its theology with the close, argumentative attention of a trained lawyer. Contemporary references describe him simply as a barrister and author of Chancery practice, and beyond his published legal treatises and this major work on Byron, little is securely recorded about his private life, personal relationships, or exact birth and death years.
