## Biography
Arrian of Nicomedia (c. 86–c. 160) was a Greek historian, philosopher, and statesman of the Roman Empire, best known as one of the most important transmitters of classical Greek thought to later ages. Born in Nicomedia in the province of Bithynia, he was a Greek-speaking aristocrat who rose to prominence in Roman public life, studying philosophy in his youth under the Stoic teacher Epictetus. Arrian became one of Epictetus’ most devoted students and later wrote down his master’s teachings, preserving them in works that became foundational for the Stoic tradition.

In addition to his philosophical writings, Arrian had a distinguished political and military career, serving as a Roman senator and eventually as governor of the province of Cappadocia. He combined practical administrative experience with literary skill, composing historical works such as his account of Alexander the Great’s campaigns, which aimed to present a critical and reliable narrative based on earlier sources. His epitome of Epictetus’ teachings, commonly known in various languages (including Spanish editions such as the "Manual" or "Enchiridion" of Epictetus), played a central role in transmitting Stoic ethics through late antiquity, the Middle Ages, and the modern era. Arrian’s life and work stand at the intersection of Greek intellectual culture and Roman imperial power, making him a key figure in the continuity of classical philosophy and historiography.
