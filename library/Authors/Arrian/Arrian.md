## Biography
Arrian (c. 86 – c. 160 CE), also known as Lucius Flavius Arrianus, was a Greek historian, philosopher, and Roman imperial official born in Nicomedia in Bithynia. Educated in the Greek tradition, he became a devoted pupil of the Stoic philosopher Epictetus, whose oral teachings he carefully recorded. From these notes he later compiled influential works on Stoic ethics, including the Enchiridion (or Handbook), a concise guide to practical moral discipline that would shape later Stoic reception in both the ancient and modern worlds.

Arrian enjoyed a distinguished public career within the Roman Empire, rising to the rank of senator and serving as consul and governor of Cappadocia, where he commanded troops and defended the eastern frontier. As an author, he is best known for his "Anabasis of Alexander," one of the most important surviving sources on the life and campaigns of Alexander the Great, written in an analytic and sober style that seeks to sift reliable testimony from legend. Through his combination of administrative service, historical inquiry, and philosophical transmission—especially his preservation and systematization of Epictetus’s thought—Arrian stands as a key figure linking Greek intellectual traditions with Roman political power, and as a crucial conduit through which later generations came to know both Alexander and Stoic philosophy.
