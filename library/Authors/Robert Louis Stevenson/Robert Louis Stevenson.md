## Biography
Robert Louis Stevenson (1850–1894) was a Scottish novelist, essayist, and travel writer, born in Edinburgh into a family of civil engineers. Frail health and chronic lung problems shaped much of his early life, but he developed a strong imaginative world and a passion for storytelling that drew him away from the family profession and into literature. After abandoning engineering and then law, he traveled widely across Europe and later to the United States and the South Pacific, experiences that deeply colored the settings and themes of his work.

Stevenson became one of the most widely read authors of the late 19th century through works such as "Treasure Island" (known in Spanish as "La isla del tesoro"), "Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde," and "Kidnapped," which helped define modern adventure fiction and psychological horror. His marriage to Fanny Osbourne, an American divorcée, was central to his personal life; they eventually settled in Samoa, where Stevenson became involved in local affairs and was known to the Samoan people as "Tusitala" (teller of tales). Despite his relatively short life, his narrative skill, vivid characters, and exploration of moral duality and heroism secured his lasting significance in English-language literature.
