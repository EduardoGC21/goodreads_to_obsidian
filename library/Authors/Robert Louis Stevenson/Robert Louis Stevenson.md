## Biography
Robert Louis Stevenson (1850–1894) was a Scottish novelist, essayist, and travel writer whose adventurous life and vivid imagination helped define popular fiction in the late nineteenth century. Born in Edinburgh to a family of lighthouse engineers, he struggled with chronic ill health from childhood, which shaped both his introspective temperament and his lifelong search for milder climates. Despite initially studying engineering and then law, he gravitated toward literature and bohemian circles, developing a distinct narrative voice that blended romance, adventure, and psychological depth.

Stevenson achieved international fame with works such as "Treasure Island" (known in Spanish as "La isla del tesoro"), "Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde," and "Kidnapped," which cemented his reputation as a master storyteller and a pioneer of modern adventure and psychological fiction. His personal life was marked by an unconventional and devoted marriage to Fanny Osbourne, an American divorcee with children, whose family he embraced as his own. Later in life, seeking better health, he settled in Samoa, where he became deeply involved in local community affairs and was affectionately known by the Samoans as "Tusitala" (the teller of tales), reflecting both his literary stature and his close relationships with the islanders.

Stevenson’s early death at 44 did little to diminish his influence; his works remained widely read and inspired later writers ranging from adventure novelists to modernists. His fiction combined moral complexity with accessible storytelling, exploring themes of duality, loyalty, and the clash between respectability and desire. Today he is remembered as one of the great figures of English-language literature, whose life of travel and cultural engagement enriched the imaginative worlds he created.
