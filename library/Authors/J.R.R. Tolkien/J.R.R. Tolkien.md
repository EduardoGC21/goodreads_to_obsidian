## Biography
John Ronald Reuel Tolkien (1892–1973) was an English writer, philologist, and academic whose imaginative creation of Middle-earth reshaped modern fantasy literature. Born in Bloemfontein in the Orange Free State (now South Africa) and raised in England after his father’s early death, he was deeply marked by his Catholic faith, his love of languages, and the trauma of serving in World War I. He studied at Exeter College, Oxford, became a distinguished scholar of Old and Middle English, and worked on the Oxford English Dictionary before returning to Oxford as a professor, where his friendship with C. S. Lewis and the Inklings literary group helped foster his creative work.

Tolkien’s legendarium, especially "The Hobbit" and "The Lord of the Rings" (including "The Fellowship of the Ring," "The Two Towers," and "The Return of the King"), together with the posthumously published "The Silmarillion," established him as the father of high fantasy, influencing generations of writers, artists, and filmmakers. His meticulous world-building, invented languages, and moral vision created a richly textured mythology that readers treat with the seriousness of real history. Beyond his literary achievements, he was a devoted husband to Edith Bratt, whose love story with him echoes in his tales of Beren and Lúthien, and a dedicated father who first told many of his stories to his children; his son Christopher Tolkien later became a key editor and steward of his unpublished work, ensuring his legacy would continue to shape fantasy and mythopoeic literature worldwide.
