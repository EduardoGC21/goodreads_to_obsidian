## Biography
J.R.R. Tolkien (1892–1973) was an English writer, philologist, and academic whose legendarium reshaped modern fantasy literature. Born in Bloemfontein in the Orange Free State and raised in England after his father’s death, he grew up steeped in languages, mythology, and Catholic faith, all of which profoundly informed his creative work. Educated at Oxford, he served in World War I, where the trauma and loss of close friends left a lasting mark on his imagination and themes of fellowship, sacrifice, and the corrupting power of evil.

After the war, Tolkien became a professor of Anglo-Saxon and later of English language and literature at Oxford University, renowned among students and colleagues for his scholarship on Beowulf and medieval languages. In his spare time he developed an elaborate private mythology and constructed languages, which eventually became the foundation for works like "The Hobbit" and the trilogy commonly known as "The Lord of the Rings" (including "The Fellowship of the Ring," "The Two Towers," and "The Return of the King"), later expanded through posthumous publications such as "The Silmarillion." His close friendship with C.S. Lewis and participation in the literary group The Inklings provided a nurturing environment for his stories, and his fiction’s depth of history, moral seriousness, and linguistic invention set a new standard for world-building.

Tolkien’s influence extends far beyond literature, shaping the aesthetics and narrative structures of contemporary fantasy, film adaptations, and gaming, while also contributing to renewed popular interest in myth and medieval studies. His work continues to be studied for its rich interweaving of philology, theology, and narrative craft, and he is widely regarded as one of the most significant and formative authors in twentieth-century English literature.
