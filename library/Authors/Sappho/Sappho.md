## Biography
Sappho was an Archaic Greek lyric poet from the island of Lesbos, active around the late 7th to early 6th century BCE. Little is known with certainty about the details of her life, but ancient sources portray her as a woman of aristocratic background who led a circle of young women devoted to poetry, music, and perhaps religious or educational training. Her work, originally composed to be sung with lyre accompaniment, focused on personal emotion, desire, and relationships, particularly the bonds between women, which made her both celebrated and controversial in antiquity and later eras.

In her own time, Sappho was admired alongside the greatest male poets; later Greek and Roman writers sometimes called her the “Tenth Muse,” a title that reflects her exceptional status in the literary tradition. Although most of her poetry survives only in fragments, recovered from quotations and papyrus finds, the intensity and refinement of her voice have had a lasting influence on Western literature and concepts of lyric subjectivity. Modern collections, such as those titled in various languages (including Spanish editions that gather her surviving poems and fragments), testify to her continuing importance and the ongoing effort to reconstruct and interpret her work.

Sappho’s legacy extends beyond poetry into cultural and social history. The association of her name and homeland, Lesbos, with female same-sex desire has shaped discussions of gender and sexuality for centuries. While the historical realities of her intimate relationships remain uncertain, her surviving lines about love and longing have made her a central figure for later readers seeking expressions of women’s voices in the ancient world.
