## Biography
Max Tegmark (born 1967) is a Swedish‑American physicist and cosmologist whose work bridges precision measurements of the universe, the philosophical foundations of reality, and the future impact of artificial intelligence. Raised in Stockholm by Karin Tegmark and mathematician Harold S. Shapiro, he studied economics and engineering physics in Sweden before moving to the United States to pursue graduate work in physics at the University of California, Berkeley. During his time in Berkeley he adopted his mother’s surname, Tegmark, and completed an influential PhD in cosmology in 1994.

After postdoctoral research at the Max Planck Institute for Physics in Munich and as a Hubble Fellow at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, Tegmark joined the faculty of the University of Pennsylvania, receiving tenure in 2003. In 2004 he became a professor of physics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology, where he has helped shape modern precision cosmology through large galaxy surveys and studies of the cosmic microwave background. Over the course of his career he has written hundreds of scientific papers and received major recognitions, including election as a Fellow of the American Physical Society.

Tegmark has also become a prominent public intellectual on the long‑term future of humanity, especially in relation to artificial intelligence. As co‑founder and president of the Future of Life Institute he works to reduce existential risks from advanced technologies, and in his bestselling book “Life 3.0: Being Human in the Age of Artificial Intelligence” he explores scenarios for how AI might transform civilization. His public advocacy, media appearances, and collaborations with researchers and technologists have made him a central figure in global conversations about aligning powerful technologies with human values.
