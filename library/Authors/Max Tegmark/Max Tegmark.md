## Biography
Max Tegmark (born 1967) is a Swedish-American physicist and author whose work spans cosmology, fundamental physics, and the societal implications of artificial intelligence. Raised in Stockholm by Swedish mother Karin Tegmark and American mathematician Harold Shapiro, he showed early aptitude for computing and science, writing software and games as a teenager. After studying economics and engineering physics in Sweden, he moved to the United States for graduate work at the University of California, Berkeley, where he completed his PhD in physics and adopted his mother’s surname, Tegmark.

Tegmark’s early research helped shape modern precision cosmology, using observations of the cosmic microwave background and large-scale structure to constrain models of the universe. He has held academic positions at the Max Planck Institute, the University of Pennsylvania, and became a professor of physics at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. Over time his interests expanded from the structure and origins of the cosmos to questions about the nature of reality and the mathematical structure of physical laws, themes he explored in later popular-science works and public lectures.

In parallel, Tegmark emerged as a prominent voice on the long-term impact of advanced artificial intelligence, co-founding the Future of Life Institute to study and reduce existential risks from emerging technologies. In “Life 3.0: Being Human in the Age of Artificial Intelligence,” he connects his background in physics and cosmology with concerns about how intelligent systems could shape the future of consciousness and civilization. His career combines technical scholarship with institution-building and public engagement, making him an influential figure at the intersection of science, philosophy, and technology policy.
