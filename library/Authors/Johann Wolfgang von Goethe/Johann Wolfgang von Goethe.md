## Biography
Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749–1832) was a German writer, thinker, and statesman who became one of the central figures of European literature and intellectual life. Born in Frankfurt am Main into a well-to-do family, he received a broad humanist education and showed early talent for languages, poetry, and the arts. His breakthrough came with the epistolary novel *The Sorrows of Young Werther*, whose intense exploration of passion and despair made him an instant celebrity and a leading voice of the Sturm und Drang movement.

Goethe spent much of his adult life in Weimar, where he served as a senior advisor at court, overseeing cultural and political affairs while also pursuing scientific studies in fields such as botany, anatomy, and color theory. His friendships and intellectual exchanges—with figures like Friedrich Schiller and other Weimar classicists—shaped a body of work that blended classical ideals with modern psychological insight. The dramatic poem *Faust*, which occupied him for decades, stands as his most ambitious work, reflecting his lifelong engagement with questions of knowledge, morality, and human striving.

Throughout his long life, Goethe’s achievements extended beyond literature to include influential essays, autobiographical writings such as *Poetry and Truth*, and a model of the cultivated, independent thinker engaged with both art and public life. His relationships, travels (especially to Italy), and public service all fed into a vision of Bildung—personal cultivation—that left a lasting imprint on German and world culture.
