## Biography
Johann Wolfgang von Goethe (1749–1832) was a German writer, statesman, and polymath whose work helped shape European literature and intellectual life at the turn of the 18th to 19th centuries. Born in Frankfurt am Main into a comfortable bourgeois family, he received a broad humanistic education and studied law before turning decisively to literature. His early fame came with the novel "The Sorrows of Young Werther" ("Las penas del joven Werther"), which captured the emotional intensity of the Sturm und Drang movement and made him a literary celebrity across Europe, influencing both Romantic sensibility and debates about individual passion versus social constraint.

Goethe spent most of his adult life in Weimar, where he served as a trusted adviser and minister to Duke Carl August, overseeing cultural, educational, and administrative reforms while also directing the court theater. In Weimar he developed a close and formative friendship with Friedrich Schiller, and together they became central figures of Weimar Classicism, promoting an ideal of harmonious balance between reason and feeling, form and freedom. His dramatic masterpiece "Faust" reworked the legend of a scholar who bargains with the devil into a vast poetic exploration of human striving, knowledge, desire, and redemption, and is widely regarded as one of the crowning achievements of German and world literature.

Beyond his literary production—poetry, drama, novels, autobiography (including "Poetry and Truth" / "Poesía y verdad: de mi vida"), and scientific writings—Goethe was deeply engaged with natural science, studying botany, anatomy, and color theory, and corresponding with many leading intellectuals of his time. His personal relationships, including early attachments such as the one that inspired Werther and his long partnership and later marriage to Christiane Vulpius, left clear traces in his work but were always framed by a broader quest for self-cultivation and understanding of the human condition. Goethe’s legacy endures not only in his poems and plays (often encountered today in selected collections and anthologies) but also in his model of the modern intellectual who moves freely between art, science, and public life.
