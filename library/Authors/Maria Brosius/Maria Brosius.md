## Biography
Maria Brosius is a German ancient historian whose work has reshaped modern understanding of the Achaemenid Persian Empire and its place within the wider ancient Near Eastern and classical worlds. Trained in ancient history at the University of Münster and later at the University of Oxford, where she completed a doctorate on women in ancient Persia, she has combined close reading of Greek literary sources with expert use of cuneiform and documentary evidence. Her monograph *Women in Ancient Persia, 559–331 BC* and later *A History of Ancient Persia: The Achaemenid Empire* positioned her as one of the leading authorities on Persian social and political history, especially the roles of royal women and court elites.

Over the course of her career, Brosius has held academic posts in the United Kingdom, Germany, and Canada, including positions at Queens’ College, Cambridge, Newcastle University, the Free University of Berlin, and the University of Toronto. She has also been closely involved with research centres devoted to documentary cultures and archives, editing the influential volume *Ancient Archives and Archival Traditions: Concepts of Record-Keeping in the Ancient World*. Her work emphasizes how documents and archival practices shaped the transmission of knowledge across the ancient Near East and into the Greek world, highlighting political and cultural links between these regions.

Brosius’s scholarship is widely cited for its nuanced picture of the Achaemenid Empire as a complex, multiethnic polity rather than merely the backdrop to Greek history. She has played a significant role in integrating Persian materials into mainstream ancient history and in foregrounding gender, family networks, and administrative practices as key to understanding imperial power. Through her teaching, supervision, and international collaborations, she has helped train a generation of scholars working at the intersection of Near Eastern and classical studies, and advanced broader recognition of Persia’s central place in the ancient Mediterranean and Near Eastern worlds.
