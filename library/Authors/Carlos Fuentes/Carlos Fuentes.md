## Biography
Carlos Fuentes (1928–2012) was a Mexican novelist, essayist, and diplomat whose work helped define the Latin American literary “Boom” of the 1960s and 1970s. Born in Panama City to Mexican parents, he grew up following his father’s diplomatic postings through various Latin American capitals and Washington, D.C., an itinerant childhood that gave him a cosmopolitan outlook and a critical, comparative view of Mexico and the wider region. He studied law in Mexico City and international relations in Geneva, then entered the Mexican diplomatic service, serving in roles at the International Labour Organization and later as Mexico’s ambassador to France.

Fuentes became internationally renowned for his formally experimental and politically engaged fiction, which interrogated Mexican history, national identity, and the legacy of revolution. Early works such as “Where the Air Is Clear” and “The Death of Artemio Cruz” established him as a central voice in 20th‑century Spanish‑language literature, and shorter works like “Aura” showcased his ability to blend the fantastic with psychological and historical themes. Over his career he published dozens of novels, stories, and essays, and he received many of the Spanish‑speaking world’s highest literary honors, including the Cervantes Prize.

Deeply involved in intellectual and political debates, Fuentes maintained complex relationships with Mexico’s ruling institutions while remaining an outspoken left‑leaning critic of authoritarianism and social injustice. He taught at major universities—especially in the United States—where he furthered dialogue about Latin American culture and politics. Personally, he moved in a cosmopolitan circle of artists and writers and maintained notable friendships and rivalries with figures such as Gabriel García Márquez and Octavio Paz, reinforcing his role as one of the most influential literary and public intellectuals of modern Mexico.
