## Biography
Carlos Fuentes (1928–2012) was a Mexican novelist, essayist, and diplomat, and one of the central figures of the Latin American literary "Boom." Born in Panama City to Mexican parents in the diplomatic service, he spent his childhood in various Latin American countries and the United States before settling in Mexico as a teenager. This cosmopolitan upbringing shaped his lifelong preoccupation with national identity, history, and the conflicts between tradition and modernity in Mexico and Latin America.

Fuentes achieved international recognition with novels such as "The Death of Artemio Cruz" and the novella "Aura," whose experimental structures and play with time, memory, and the supernatural helped redefine the Latin American novel. His work blends historical narrative, political critique, and myth, probing the legacy of colonialism, the Mexican Revolution, and the workings of power and desire. He was closely associated, intellectually and personally, with contemporaries like Gabriel García Márquez, Mario Vargas Llosa, and Octavio Paz, participating in a wider regional conversation about culture and politics.

Beyond literature, Fuentes was an influential public intellectual. Trained as a lawyer, he served as Mexico’s ambassador to France in the mid-1970s and often wrote and spoke about democracy, inequality, and the responsibilities of the cultural elite. He received numerous international awards and honors, and until his death in Mexico City he remained a prominent voice interpreting Mexico to the world and urging Latin America to confront its past in order to imagine a more just future.
