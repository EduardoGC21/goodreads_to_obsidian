## Biography
Leo Tolstoy (1828–1910) was a Russian novelist, moral philosopher, and social critic, widely regarded as one of the greatest writers in world literature. Born into an aristocratic family at the Yasnaya Polyana estate, he experienced privilege alongside deep personal and spiritual unrest. After a youth marked by gambling, military service, and travel, he transformed these experiences into early works like the Sevastopol tales, which drew on his time as an officer during the Crimean War and established his reputation as a powerful realist.

Tolstoy’s mature masterpieces, especially "War and Peace" and "Anna Karenina", combined psychological depth, philosophical reflection, and detailed social observation, reshaping the possibilities of the novel. Over time he underwent a profound religious and ethical conversion, rejecting much of his former aristocratic life to embrace ideals of Christian nonviolence, simplicity, and social justice. In this later period he wrote shorter, probing works such as "The Death of Ivan Ilyich" and the novella "The Forged Coupon", critiquing hypocrisy, state violence, and spiritual emptiness.

Tolstoy’s radical moral teachings, opposition to institutional churches, and advocacy of nonviolent resistance influenced figures such as Mohandas Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr. His complex relationships—with his wife Sophia, with the Russian Orthodox Church that excommunicated him, and with the tsarist state that surveilled him—reflected the tension between his global moral authority and his status as a dissident within his own society. He died in 1910 after leaving his estate in search of a simpler life, by then a towering moral and artistic figure whose work continues to shape literature, ethics, and political thought.
