## Biography
Federico García Lorca (1898–1936) was a Spanish poet, playwright, and theatre director, widely regarded as one of the most important literary figures of the 20th century in the Spanish‑speaking world. Born in Fuente Vaqueros, near Granada, he grew up in rural Andalusia, a landscape and culture that deeply shaped his imagination—especially its folk songs, gypsy traditions, and strong sense of tragedy. After moving to Madrid, he became part of the cultural vanguard known as the Generation of ’27, forming close relationships with artists and writers such as Salvador Dalí and Luis Buñuel, and developing a style that fused popular tradition with avant‑garde experimentation.

Lorca’s work, including his poetry collections and plays, is marked by an intense lyricism, symbolic imagery, and a preoccupation with love, death, repression, and freedom. His plays like the rural tragedies brought to the stage the struggles of women and marginalized communities, while selected poems, such as those found in collections of chosen or “escogidos” poems, helped secure his reputation as a master of modern Spanish verse. Openly expressing his artistic and personal nonconformity in a conservative society, he became both a literary innovator and a cultural symbol of resistance.

At the outbreak of the Spanish Civil War, Lorca’s left‑leaning sympathies, prominence, and homosexuality made him a target for the Nationalist authorities. He was arrested near Granada and executed without trial in 1936, his body buried in an unmarked grave. His early death turned him into a powerful martyr‑figure and intensified the impact of his work, which continued to influence poets, dramatists, and artists worldwide long after his writings were censored in Spain. Today, he is remembered not only for the beauty and emotional force of his poetry and drama, but also for the way his life and fate encapsulate the cultural and political tensions of modern Spain.
