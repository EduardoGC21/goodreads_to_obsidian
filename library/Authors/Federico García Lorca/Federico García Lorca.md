## Biography
Federico García Lorca (1898–1936) was a Spanish poet, playwright, and theatre director born in Fuente Vaqueros, near Granada, Spain. Raised in rural Andalusia in a middle-class family, he absorbed local folklore, music, and religious traditions that later permeated his work. After studying in Granada and Madrid, he became part of the influential Generation of ’27 alongside artists such as Salvador Dalí and filmmaker Luis Buñuel, forming intense personal and creative relationships that shaped his artistic vision. Lorca’s poetry, including collections later represented in selections like "Poemas escogidos," blended popular song forms, surreal imagery, and deep empathy for marginalized people, particularly Andalusian peasants, Roma communities, and women.

Lorca also transformed Spanish theatre with plays such as "Bodas de sangre" (Blood Wedding), "Yerma," and "La casa de Bernarda Alba," which explored themes of desire, repression, honor, and social constraint. A gay man in a rigidly conservative, Catholic society, he coded many of his personal conflicts into his characters and symbols, making his work both intensely personal and broadly resonant. At the outbreak of the Spanish Civil War in 1936, Lorca was arrested and executed by Nationalist forces near Granada, likely because of his left-leaning sympathies, prominence as an intellectual, and sexual identity. His violent death turned him into a powerful symbol of artistic freedom crushed by authoritarianism, and his poetry and plays remain central to Spanish-language literature and world theatre.
