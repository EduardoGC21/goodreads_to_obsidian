## Biography
Mary Beard (born 1955) is an English classicist whose work has profoundly shaped contemporary understanding of the ancient Roman world. Raised in Shropshire as the only child of a school headmistress and an architect, she developed an early fascination with antiquity, spending school holidays on archaeological digs before studying classics at Newnham College, Cambridge. After early teaching posts, she returned to Cambridge, where she became a leading professor of classics and a fellow of Newnham, known for combining rigorous scholarship with an accessible, often provocative public voice.

Beard’s research and writing range from Roman religion and politics to art, everyday life, and the reception of antiquity in later periods. Books such as “Pompeii: The Life of a Roman Town” and “SPQR: A History of Ancient Rome” helped reframe Rome not just as an empire of emperors and generals but as a living society full of ordinary people, conflict, and humor. Her public work—including television series like “Meet the Romans,” her long-running blog “A Don’s Life,” and numerous essays—has made her one of Britain’s best-known scholars, frequently defending the value of the humanities and challenging simplistic uses of classical history in modern politics.

A committed feminist and public intellectual, Beard has spoken candidly about misogyny, online abuse, and the challenges faced by women in academia, often drawing connections between ancient and modern power structures. She has been honored with major prizes and distinctions, including a Damehood for services to classical scholarship, and has served as a trustee of major cultural institutions while mentoring generations of students. Her marriage to art historian Robin Cormack and their family life sit alongside a career that has made her an influential figure in debates about how, and why, we study the ancient world today.
