## Biography
Mary Beard (born 1955) is a British classicist, historian, and public intellectual renowned for making the ancient Roman world accessible to broad audiences. Educated at Newnham College, Cambridge, she later became a professor of Classics at the University of Cambridge and a fellow of Newnham, where she built her reputation as both a rigorous scholar and an engaging teacher. Her work often combines close analysis of classical texts and material culture with an eye for how ordinary people lived, thought, and exercised power in antiquity.

Beard’s scholarship spans Roman politics, religion, social life, and the reception of classical antiquity in later eras. Her book "SPQR" (including editions such as "SPQR: Una historia de la antigua Roma") helped cement her status as one of the most influential interpreters of Roman history, synthesizing a vast body of research into a vivid narrative that challenges myths about Rome’s rise and governance. Beyond her academic publications, she has been a prominent voice in newspapers, television documentaries, and public lectures, where she advocates for critical, inclusive, and sometimes irreverent engagement with the classical past.

Beard has also become an important figure in contemporary cultural and intellectual life, known for her willingness to speak publicly about issues such as women’s voices in public discourse, academic freedom, and the uses and abuses of history. Her career is marked by numerous honors and awards recognizing both scholarly excellence and public service to the humanities, and she has influenced generations of students and readers by showing how the ancient world can illuminate modern debates about citizenship, empire, gender, and power.
