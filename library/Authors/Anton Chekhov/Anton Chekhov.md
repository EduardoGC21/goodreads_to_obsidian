## Biography
Anton Pavlovich Chekhov (1860–1904) was a Russian playwright, short-story writer, and practicing physician who became one of the central figures in modern literature. Born in Taganrog, a port town on the Sea of Azov, into a family marked by poverty and a strict, sometimes abusive father, he supported himself through medical studies by writing humorous sketches and short pieces for newspapers and magazines. Chekhov’s medical work informed his detached yet compassionate observation of human suffering, and he famously described medicine as his lawful wife and literature as his mistress.

Chekhov revolutionized the short story and modern drama through his subtle, understated style and focus on ordinary lives. Collections such as his "Cuentos" (short stories) and works like "La estepa" and "En el barranco" exemplify his ability to reveal moral complexity, quiet despair, and fleeting moments of grace through seemingly simple situations. As a playwright, he helped redefine theatre with his so‑called "comedies"—including "The Seagull", "Uncle Vanya", "Three Sisters", and "The Cherry Orchard"—in which external action is minimal but inner emotional currents are intense; these plays became foundational for Stanislavski’s psychological realism and for 20th‑century drama more broadly.

Despite chronic illness—especially tuberculosis, which ultimately caused his early death in Badenweiler, Germany—Chekhov maintained close relationships and a wide circle of friends in literary and theatrical circles. He was devoted to his family, supported relatives financially, engaged in philanthropic projects such as building schools and clinics, and later married the actress Olga Knipper, a leading performer in his plays. His life and work bridged late 19th‑century Russian society and the emerging modernist sensibility, and his narrative techniques, quiet irony, and empathy for flawed, often passive characters profoundly shaped later writers and dramatists around the world.
