## Biography
Anton Chekhov (1860–1904) was a Russian playwright, short-story writer, and practicing physician who became one of the most influential figures in modern literature and drama. Born in Taganrog, in the Russian Empire, to a struggling merchant family, he financed his medical studies in Moscow by publishing humorous sketches and stories. Although he often said “medicine is my lawful wife and literature my mistress,” his literary work soon eclipsed his medical career, and he developed a distinctive style marked by psychological subtlety, understatement, and a focus on everyday life rather than grand plots.

Chekhov’s short stories revolutionized the form, replacing conventional climaxes with open endings and finely observed moments of human contradiction. His plays, including “The Seagull,” “Uncle Vanya,” “Three Sisters,” and “The Cherry Orchard,” helped lay the foundations of modern theater through their emphasis on subtext, ensemble acting, and the inner lives of ordinary people. Collaborations with director Konstantin Stanislavski at the Moscow Art Theatre were especially significant in shaping 20th‑century acting and dramaturgy. Chekhov’s personal life was marked by ill health—he suffered from tuberculosis for many years—and a late marriage to actress Olga Knipper. He died relatively young, but his work profoundly influenced later writers and dramatists worldwide.
