## Biography
Dante Alighieri (1265–1321) was a Florentine poet, thinker, and statesman whose work profoundly shaped European literature and the Italian language. Born into a minor noble family in Florence, he received a solid education in poetry, philosophy, and theology, and became deeply involved in the city’s political life during the turbulent conflict between the Guelph and Ghibelline factions. Dante aligned with the White Guelphs, and when the rival Black Guelphs seized power in 1302, he was condemned to exile under threat of execution if he returned.

His long years of wandering from court to court in northern and central Italy deeply marked his life and writings, and it was during this exile that he composed his masterpiece, the “Divine Comedy” (“La divina commedia”), an epic poem that narrates an allegorical journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise. Drawing on classical learning, Christian theology, and his personal experience of love, loss, and political betrayal, Dante used the poem to reflect on justice, salvation, and the moral order of the universe. His idealized love for Beatrice Portinari, first explored in “La Vita Nuova,” and his complex relationships with patrons and contemporaries, gave emotional depth and personal urgency to his vision.

Dante’s decision to write major works in the Tuscan vernacular rather than in Latin played a decisive role in establishing the basis of modern Italian, earning him the title “father of the Italian language.” Beyond the “Divine Comedy,” his treatises on language, politics, and philosophy—such as “De vulgari eloquentia,” “Convivio,” and “Monarchia”—show him as a rigorous thinker concerned with the proper relationship between spiritual and temporal power. He died in Ravenna in 1321, still an exile from Florence, but his legacy grew over the centuries, and he is now regarded as one of the central figures of Western literature and intellectual history.
