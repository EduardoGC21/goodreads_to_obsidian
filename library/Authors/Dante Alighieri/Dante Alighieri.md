## Biography
Dante Alighieri (1265–1321) was an Italian poet, thinker, and statesman whose life was deeply intertwined with the turbulent politics of medieval Florence. Born into a minor noble family, he became involved with the city’s factional struggles between Guelphs and Ghibellines, and later within the Guelphs themselves, which shaped both his public career and his personal fortunes. His early life included military service, political offices, and an education in philosophy, theology, and classical literature, all of which would later inform the intellectual breadth of his writings.

Dante’s political alignment with the White Guelphs led to his exile from Florence in 1302, an event that proved decisive for his life and work. Condemned under charges of corruption and barred from returning on pain of death, he spent the rest of his life as a wanderer among various Italian courts, dependent on patrons and separated from his home and family. This exile sharpened his reflections on justice, morality, and the fate of cities and individuals, themes that pervade his poetry.

During his years in exile, Dante composed his masterpiece, commonly known as “The Divine Comedy” (“La divina commedia”), a visionary journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Paradise that blends personal experience, theology, politics, and philosophy into a single vast allegory. The work established him as a foundational figure of Italian literature and a key architect of the Italian language. His imagined love for Beatrice Portinari, first celebrated in earlier works, becomes in the Comedy a symbol of divine grace and guidance. Dante’s fusion of personal emotion, political commentary, and spiritual aspiration secured his legacy as one of the central authors of the Western canon.
