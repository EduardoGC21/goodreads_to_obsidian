## Biography
Jacob Ludwig Karl Grimm (1785–1863) was a German scholar whose life bridged rigorous legal training, pioneering linguistic research, and the collection of folk literature. Born in Hanau in the Landgraviate of Hesse-Kassel, he lost his father early, leaving the family in strained circumstances but also drawing him into a close partnership with his younger brother Wilhelm. The two brothers were educated in Kassel and studied law at the University of Marburg, where Jacob came under the influence of jurist Friedrich Carl von Savigny and the Romantic movement, turning his interests from legal practice to the historical study of language, literature, and custom.

Working first in Kassel as a librarian and civil servant, and later as a professor and member of the Academy of Sciences in Berlin, Jacob Grimm helped to found modern Germanic philology and folklore studies. With Wilhelm he assembled and repeatedly revised the collection known in English as “Grimm’s Fairy Tales,” not simply as children’s stories but as documents of German and broader European oral tradition. At the same time, Jacob pursued ambitious scholarly projects on his own, including the formulation of Grimm’s law of sound shifts in Indo‑European languages, the multi-volume "Deutsche Grammatik," the mythological study "Deutsche Mythologie," and the vast "Deutsches Wörterbuch" German dictionary begun with Wilhelm. Unmarried and deeply bound to his family, Jacob spent most of his adult life living and working alongside his brother, and his reputation rests both on that fraternal collaboration and on his role in establishing historical linguistics and folklore as rigorous academic disciplines.
