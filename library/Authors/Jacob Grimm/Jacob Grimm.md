## Biography
Jacob Grimm (1785–1863) was a German philologist, legal scholar, and collector of folklore, best known for his collaboration with his younger brother Wilhelm Grimm. Born in Hanau in what is now Germany, he studied law at the University of Marburg, where the influence of historical and linguistic scholarship led him to comparative philology and the study of medieval literature and legal traditions. Alongside his brother, he gathered oral tales from various regions and social classes, work that culminated in the publication of collections such as “Grimm’s Fairy Tales,” which helped preserve and shape the canon of German and European folk narratives.

Beyond fairy tales, Jacob Grimm was a pioneering linguist whose work laid foundations for modern Germanic philology and historical linguistics. His multi‑volume "Deutsche Grammatik" systematically described the Germanic languages and formulated what became known as Grimm’s Law, explaining consonant shifts in the Indo‑European language family. Later in life, he and Wilhelm embarked on the monumental "Deutsches Wörterbuch," a comprehensive German dictionary. Jacob’s scholarship, his role in the liberal “Göttingen Seven” protesting absolutist rule, and his lifelong intellectual partnership with his brother made him a central figure in 19th‑century German cultural and academic life.
