## Biography
Adrian Goldsworthy (born 1969) is a British historian and author specializing in the military and political history of the ancient Mediterranean world, particularly Rome and its rival powers. Educated at Oxford, where he completed a D.Phil. on aspects of the Roman army, he went on to build a career that bridges academic scholarship and accessible popular history, becoming one of the most widely read interpreters of Roman warfare and statecraft in the English-speaking world. His writing is noted for combining close use of primary sources with clear narrative and attention to the practical realities of campaigning, logistics, and leadership.

Goldsworthy has written influential studies of the Roman army and key conflicts that shaped Rome’s rise, including works on the Punic Wars that explore the struggle between Rome and Carthage, and broader surveys of Roman imperial power such as "Pax Romana". He has also produced major biographies of central Roman figures, including "Augustus: First Emperor of Rome" and a widely read life of Julius Caesar, in which he situates these leaders within the social, political, and military structures of their times. Through this body of work, he has helped refine modern understanding of how Rome expanded, governed, and eventually transformed, influencing both academic debate and popular perceptions of antiquity.

Beyond his published books, Goldsworthy’s significance lies in his role as a mediator between the scholarly world and a broad public interested in ancient history. He frequently synthesizes complex debates on topics such as Roman governance, provincial life, and civil–military relations in ways that remain faithful to the evidence while remaining readable and engaging. This combination of rigorous research, narrative skill, and sustained focus on the lived experience of soldiers, commanders, and statesmen has made him a leading contemporary voice on Rome and its neighbors.
