## Biography
Adrian Goldsworthy (b. 1969) is a British historian and author best known for his accessible yet scholarly works on ancient Rome and the wider classical world. Trained as an academic military historian, he completed his doctorate at the University of Oxford with a focus on Roman warfare, and went on to lecture at several universities before becoming a full‑time writer. His work combines close reading of ancient sources with an interest in how armies, institutions, and political cultures actually functioned.

Goldsworthy has written widely on Roman military history and biography, including detailed studies of figures such as Julius Caesar and Augustus, and broader surveys like *The Punic Wars* and *Pax Romana* that explore Rome’s expansion and methods of rule. Books such as *Augustus: First Emperor of Rome* and his major biography of Caesar have helped shape modern popular understanding of how charismatic leaders, civil war, and political reform transformed the Roman Republic into an empire. He is recognized for balancing narrative drive with careful analysis, making complex scholarly debates accessible to general readers without sacrificing nuance.

Beyond his monographs, Goldsworthy has contributed to documentaries, lectures, and essays that situate Roman history within larger questions about power, governance, and military organization. While much of his work centers on the Roman world, he has also written historical novels that draw on his expertise in ancient and early modern warfare. His influence lies in bringing rigorous classical scholarship to a wide audience, deepening public appreciation of Rome’s institutions, conflicts, and key personalities.
