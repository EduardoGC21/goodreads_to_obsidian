## Biography
John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) was an English philosopher, economist, and political theorist whose work helped shape modern liberal thought. Educated intensively from childhood by his father James Mill and influenced by Jeremy Bentham, he became a leading voice for utilitarianism while also refining it to emphasize qualitative differences in pleasures and the importance of individual character. His intellectual life included periods of severe depression, during which he re-evaluated his earlier, strictly rationalist outlook and came to value emotion, poetry, and personal development more deeply.

Mill’s significance lies in his defense of individual liberty, free expression, and representative government, arguing that society should only restrict individual action to prevent harm to others, a view he articulated powerfully in works such as “On Liberty.” He was also a pioneering advocate for women’s rights, arguing for legal and social equality and supporting women’s suffrage. Professionally, he worked for the East India Company and later served as a Member of Parliament, where he used his position to support progressive reforms. Mill’s relationships—with his father as mentor and critic, and with Harriet Taylor Mill as his intellectual partner and later wife—were central to his development and deeply influenced his mature philosophy.

Across philosophy, economics, and social theory, Mill sought to reconcile individual freedom with the common good, promoting a vision of society in which open debate, education, and moral progress allow individuals and communities to flourish. His ideas on liberty, democracy, and rights continue to frame contemporary discussions about the limits of state power, the protection of minorities, and the ethical responsibilities of citizens in modern societies.
