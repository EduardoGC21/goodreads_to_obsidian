## Biography
John Stuart Mill (1806–1873) was an English philosopher, economist, and public intellectual who became one of the most influential liberal thinkers of the 19th century. Born in London to the Scottish philosopher James Mill, he was subjected to an intense and highly structured education from early childhood, reading Greek and Latin classics and studying logic and political economy while still a boy. Closely associated with Jeremy Bentham and the utilitarian circle, Mill initially adopted strict Benthamite utilitarianism before later revising it to emphasize the quality of pleasures and the importance of individual character.

Mill’s mature work developed a powerful defense of individual freedom, representative government, and women’s rights within the broader framework of liberalism. In "On Liberty" (1859), written under the profound intellectual influence of his long-time companion and later wife Harriet Taylor, he articulated the harm principle, arguing that power can only be rightfully exercised over individuals to prevent harm to others, not to enforce moral or social conformity. Beyond his philosophical writings, Mill worked for the East India Company, served as a Member of Parliament, advocated for women’s suffrage and social reform, and left a lasting legacy on political philosophy, economics, and modern conceptions of personal autonomy and social progress.
