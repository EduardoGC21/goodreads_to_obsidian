## Biography
Julio Cortázar (1914–1984) was an Argentine writer, translator, and intellectual whose innovative fiction helped redefine 20th‑century Latin American literature. Born in Brussels to Argentine parents and raised near Buenos Aires, he worked as a teacher and later as a translator, eventually settling in France. Cortázar was deeply engaged with politics and culture, participating in debates around Latin American identity, revolutionary movements, and the role of the writer in society, while maintaining close ties to fellow authors, artists, and exiles.

Cortázar’s fiction is known for blurring the boundaries between realism and the fantastic, exploring chance, play, and the instability of everyday life. Early works like "Los reyes" show his interest in myth and reimagined classical narratives, while "Rayuela" (“Hopscotch”) became a landmark of the Latin American Boom for its experimental structure and its portrayal of dislocated, introspective characters moving between Paris and Buenos Aires. Beyond his novels and stories, his essays, translations, and political engagement cemented his significance as a central figure of modern Latin American and world literature.
