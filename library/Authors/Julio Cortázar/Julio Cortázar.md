## Biography
Julio Cortázar (1914–1984) was an Argentine writer, translator, and intellectual who became one of the central figures of the Latin American Boom. Born in Brussels to Argentine parents and raised in Argentina, he worked as a teacher and later as a translator for UNESCO, eventually settling in Paris. His life was marked by a deep engagement with politics and culture, and he often supported leftist and anti-dictatorial movements in Latin America, which informed the ethical and existential tension in much of his work.

Cortázar’s fiction is known for its formal experimentation, playfulness, and blending of the fantastic with the everyday. "Rayuela" (Hopscotch) is his most influential novel, a landmark of modernist and postmodernist narrative that invites readers to navigate its chapters in non-linear sequences, challenging conventional reading habits and exploring alienation, love, and the search for meaning among expatriates in Paris and intellectuals in Buenos Aires. Earlier works such as "Los reyes" reimagine classical myths, showing his early interest in reworking tradition through a highly personal, lyrical lens.

Beyond his literary innovations, Cortázar’s significance lies in how he reshaped Spanish-language narrative, opening space for metafiction, structural games, and philosophical reflection within popular and high literature alike. He maintained close relationships with other writers and artists of the Boom generation, contributing to a transnational network of Latin American literature that gained worldwide recognition. His legacy endures as a touchstone for writers exploring experimental form, political engagement, and the porous boundary between reality and imagination.
