## Biography
Molière (Jean-Baptiste Poquelin, 1622–1673) was a French playwright and actor who became one of the central figures of Western theatre and a defining voice of 17th‑century French comedy. Born in Paris to a prosperous upholsterer for the royal household, he abandoned a comfortable bourgeois career to join the theatre, a decision that caused tension with his family and risked social disgrace. After years touring the provinces with his company, he honed a sharp sense of stagecraft, timing, and audience taste that later underpinned his greatest successes at court.

Under the patronage of Louis XIV in Paris, Molière wrote and performed comedies that dissected hypocrisy, vanity, and social pretension with wit and precision. Works such as “Tartuffe” satirized religious hypocrisy so piercingly that they provoked censorship and controversy, yet also cemented his reputation as a bold moral observer of his age. As both actor-manager and playwright, he managed complicated troupe finances, rivalries with other theatre companies, and fluctuating royal favor, all while struggling with chronic illness.

Molière’s personal life was intertwined with his work: he married actress Armande Béjart, a union that fed gossip and speculation but also contributed to a close-knit theatrical ensemble that could realize his demanding comedies of manners. He died shortly after collapsing onstage during a performance of “Le Malade imaginaire” (The Imaginary Invalid), emblematic of a life spent in the theatre. His legacy endures in the central place his plays hold in the French canon, the influence they exerted on later European drama, and the continuing relevance of his satirical portraits of human folly.
