## Biography
Molière (Jean-Baptiste Poquelin, 1622–1673) was a French playwright and actor born in Paris into a prosperous bourgeois family; his father served as an upholsterer to the king. Rejecting a conventional legal or court career, he founded the Illustre Théâtre in 1643 and spent years touring the provinces, honing his craft as both performer and author. Returning to Paris under the protection of powerful patrons, eventually including Louis XIV, he established himself as the leading comic dramatist of his era.

Molière’s comedies combined sharp social observation with farce, exposing hypocrisy, pretension, and moral rigidity in French society. Works such as "Tartuffe" ("Tartufo"), which satirized religious impostors, brought him into direct conflict with church authorities and moralists, leading to bans and heated public debates that only increased his notoriety. He innovated by blending Italian commedia dell’arte traditions with finely drawn characters and elegant verse, elevating comedy to a serious literary and theatrical form.

In his personal life, Molière’s close collaboration and later marriage to the actress Armande Béjart fueled gossip and controversy, reflecting broader tensions around the status of actors in 17th‑century France. Despite illness and opposition, he led his troupe until his death in 1673, reportedly collapsing after a performance of "Le Malade Imaginaire". His legacy shaped the development of European comedy and secured his place as a central figure of French literature and theater.
