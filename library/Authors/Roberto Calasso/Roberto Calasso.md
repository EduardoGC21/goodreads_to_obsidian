## Biography
Roberto Calasso (1941–2021) was an Italian writer, editor, and publisher whose work wove together myth, literature, and philosophy into a single, ongoing meditation on modern consciousness. Born in Florence on May 30, 1941, into an intellectually prominent Tuscan family, he grew up surrounded by books: his maternal grandfather Ernesto Codignola was a philosopher and publisher, his mother a scholar of German literature, and his father a legal historian. During World War II his antifascist father was arrested and condemned to death, forcing the family into hiding—an early encounter with history and violence that later underpinned Calasso’s fascination with sacrifice, power, and the sacred. He studied English literature in Rome under Mario Praz, writing a thesis on Sir Thomas Browne’s hieroglyphs, and mastered numerous languages, including Sanskrit, which opened for him the worlds of Vedic and Hindu myth later explored in books like “Ka” and “El ardor/Ardor.”

In 1962 Calasso joined the nascent Milan publisher Adelphi Edizioni and devoted his life to it, eventually becoming editorial director, then chairman and, later, its controlling owner in order to preserve its independence. As Adelphi’s guiding spirit he combined the roles of erudite author and visionary publisher, curating a catalogue that brought demanding works of philosophy, literature, and religion to a wide readership. His own series of interconnected books—including “Las bodas de Cadmo y Harmonía/The Marriage of Cadmus and Harmony,” “Ka,” “Literature and the Gods,” “El cazador celeste/The Celestial Hunter,” and others—reimagined Greek and Indian myth, nineteenth‑ and twentieth‑century literature, and political history as a continuous web of stories that reveal how the modern mind emerged from archaic ritual and divine presences.

Calasso’s personal life was closely linked to the literary world: he married the Swiss novelist Fleur Jaeggy and maintained deep friendships with writers and scholars across Europe. Often described as “a literary institution of one,” he was celebrated for his vast reading, allusive style, and the way he made ancient myths feel urgently contemporary. Until his death in Milan on July 28, 2021, he remained at the center of European letters, both as an author whose books were widely translated and as the emblematic publisher of Adelphi, shaping several generations’ understanding of what serious literature and thought could be.
