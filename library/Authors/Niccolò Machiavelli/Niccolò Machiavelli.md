## Biography
Niccolò Machiavelli (1469–1527) was an Italian diplomat, political theorist, and writer from Florence whose life was deeply entwined with the turbulent politics of Renaissance Italy. Born into a modestly well‑connected Florentine family, he entered public service in the Florentine Republic in 1498, soon after the fall of the Dominican reformer Girolamo Savonarola. As a senior official and diplomat, Machiavelli undertook numerous missions to foreign courts, including France and the papal states, giving him firsthand experience of power politics, war, and shifting alliances in an era marked by invasions, internal strife, and the rivalry of powerful city‑states and monarchies.

In 1512 the Medici family regained control of Florence, the republic collapsed, and Machiavelli was dismissed, briefly imprisoned, and subjected to torture on suspicion of conspiracy. Banished from political life, he retired to his estate at Sant’Andrea in Percussina, where he turned to writing. There he composed his most famous work, "Il Principe" (known in Spanish as "El Príncipe" and in English as "The Prince"), along with the "Discourses on Livy" and various histories and plays. These writings, especially "The Prince," distilled his observations about power, statecraft, and human nature, making him a foundational figure in modern political thought.

Machiavelli’s significance lies in his unsentimental, sometimes shocking analysis of political necessity, the use of force, and the gap between moral ideals and effective rule. His name later gave rise to the term “Machiavellian,” reflecting the widespread view that he separated politics from conventional morality. Yet he also envisioned strong republican institutions and civic virtue as the basis of a resilient state. Though he never regained major office, he maintained ties with leading Florentine families and intellectuals until his death in 1527, leaving a legacy that has influenced statesmen, philosophers, and writers for centuries.
