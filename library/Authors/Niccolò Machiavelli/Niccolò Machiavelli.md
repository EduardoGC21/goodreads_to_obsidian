## Biography
Niccolò Machiavelli (1469–1527) was an Italian diplomat, political thinker, and writer from Florence, active during the turbulent years of the Italian Renaissance. Born into a modestly well-off family, he entered public service for the Florentine Republic in 1498 and rose to prominence as a senior official and envoy, negotiating with powerful figures such as Cesare Borgia and the papacy. These experiences, amid constant war and shifting alliances, deeply shaped his understanding of power, statecraft, and human behavior.

After the Medici family returned to power in 1512, Machiavelli was dismissed from office, briefly imprisoned and tortured on suspicion of conspiracy, and then forced into a life of internal exile on his family’s small estate outside Florence. It was in this period that he wrote his major works, including “Il Principe” (“The Prince”), where he analyzed how rulers gain and maintain power, often in starkly realistic and unsentimental terms. Although long accused of endorsing immorality, his writings are now widely read as a penetrating, sometimes ironic analysis of politics as it is practiced, not as it ought to be. Machiavelli’s blend of classical learning, firsthand diplomatic experience, and sharp psychological insight has made him a foundational figure in modern political thought, influencing debates on republicanism, leadership, and statecraft for centuries.
