## Biography
Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a philosopher, writer, and composer born in Geneva, then an independent republic, whose ideas became central to the European Enlightenment and the politics of the French Revolution. Orphaned of his mother shortly after birth and raised in modest circumstances, he spent his youth as an apprentice and wanderer before establishing himself in Paris, where he entered the circle of the philosophes and contributed to the Encyclopédie. His personal relationships—most notably with Thérèse Levasseur, with whom he had several children placed in foundling hospitals, and with intellectual contemporaries such as Diderot and Voltaire—were intense and often conflict-ridden, shaping both his self-understanding and his later isolation.

Rousseau’s writings on education, politics, and the self—exemplified by works such as “Emile,” “The Social Contract,” and his “Confessions”—challenged existing social and political orders. He argued that legitimate political authority rests on a social contract among free and equal citizens and that society often corrupts a naturally good human nature. His emphasis on authenticity, moral sentiment, and the general will influenced revolutionary leaders, romantic writers, and modern ideas of democracy and individual autonomy. In his later years he lived under suspicion and exile in various European locales, but his posthumous reputation grew steadily, and he is now regarded as one of the most consequential thinkers in modern political and educational theory.

His legacy is marked by a striking tension between his ideals and his personal failings, which he himself laid bare in his autobiographical writings. This openness about his inner life, combined with his critique of inequality and artificial social conventions, made him a foundational figure for later notions of introspective authorship, human rights, and popular sovereignty, ensuring his continuing relevance in philosophical, literary, and political debates.
