## Biography
Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778) was a Genevan-born philosopher, writer, and composer whose ideas profoundly shaped the European Enlightenment and later revolutionary and romantic movements. Born in Geneva to a watchmaker father and losing his mother shortly after birth, he spent a restless youth as an apprentice, servant, and wanderer before finding his way into intellectual circles in Paris. There he became associated with the philosophes, including Denis Diderot, contributing influential articles on music and politics to the Encyclopédie. Though often grouped with other Enlightenment thinkers, Rousseau’s emphasis on feeling, authenticity, and moral simplicity set him apart, making him a crucial precursor of Romanticism.

Rousseau’s major works explore education, politics, and the inner life of the individual. In "Emile, or On Education" he proposed a natural, child-centered model of education, while "The Social Contract" articulated the idea of popular sovereignty and the general will, concepts that would inspire leaders and critics of the French Revolution alike. His autobiographical "Confessions" pioneered modern introspective writing, revealing his personal struggles, sensitivities, and complicated relationships, including his long partnership with Thérèse Levasseur and his painful decision to place their children in a foundling hospital. Often at odds with religious and political authorities, Rousseau spent his later years moving between countries in search of safety and peace, but his writings continued to circulate widely, leaving a lasting legacy on political theory, pedagogy, literature, and modern conceptions of individual freedom and authenticity.
