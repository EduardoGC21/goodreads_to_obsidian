## Biography
Francisco García Bazán (1940–2025) was an Argentine philosopher, historian of religions, and university professor whose work profoundly shaped the study of early Christianity, gnosticism, and the relationship between ancient philosophy and Christian thought. Born in Málaga and raised from adolescence in Argentina, he pursued philosophy at the University of Buenos Aires, graduating summa cum laude, and later completed a doctorate in philosophy at the Universidad del Salvador, also with highest honors. He trained in Rome at the Pontifical Gregorian University, where he studied under Antonio Orbe, and joined Argentina’s National Scientific and Technical Research Council (CONICET), eventually reaching its highest rank of Investigador Superior.

Over several decades García Bazán combined intense research with influential teaching at institutions such as the University of Buenos Aires, Universidad del Salvador, and Universidad Argentina John F. Kennedy, where he directed the Center for Research in Philosophy and History of Religions and edited the journal "Epimelia". An extraordinarily prolific scholar, he produced dozens of books and hundreds of articles on gnosticism, neoplatonism, hermetic religion, and the phenomenology of religion; his study "El Evangelio de Judas" is one of several works in which he used specific ancient texts to illuminate broader questions about early Christianity and its intellectual milieu. His contributions were recognized with major distinctions including the Konex and Bernardo Houssay awards and an honorary doctorate, and he remained a public intellectual who wrote for newspapers and engaged wider audiences in religious and philosophical debates.

Remembered by colleagues and students as both rigorous and personally committed, García Bazán cultivated a profile that joined scholarly precision with a concern for the spiritual and ethical dimensions of religious traditions. His legacy endures in the generation of researchers he trained, in the institutional spaces he helped build for the academic study of religions in Argentina, and in a body of work that continues to serve as a key reference for the study of gnosis, early Christianity, and the history of ideas across the Mediterranean world.
