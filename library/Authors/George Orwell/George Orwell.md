## Biography
George Orwell (1903–1950), born Eric Arthur Blair in Motihari, British India, was an English novelist, essayist, journalist, and critic whose work explored authoritarianism, social injustice, and the manipulation of truth. Raised in a lower-upper-middle-class British family, he was educated at Eton but did not attend university, instead serving in the Indian Imperial Police in Burma. Disillusioned with imperialism, he resigned and later immersed himself in the lives of the poor in Britain and France, experiences he chronicled in early works like "Down and Out in Paris and London" and "The Road to Wigan Pier." His time fighting for the Republicans in the Spanish Civil War, described in "Homage to Catalonia," was pivotal in shaping his fierce opposition to totalitarianism on both the right and the left.

Orwell’s most influential works, the allegorical novella "Animal Farm" and the dystopian novel "1984," crystallized his warnings about state propaganda, surveillance, and the erosion of individual freedom, making him one of the 20th century’s most significant political writers. Throughout his career he combined moral seriousness with clarity of style, writing essays and reviews that dissected language, politics, and culture, and developing the idea that political language can be a tool of deception. Despite chronic ill health, including the tuberculosis that led to his early death in 1950, Orwell left a body of work that continues to shape debates about power, truth, and democracy.
