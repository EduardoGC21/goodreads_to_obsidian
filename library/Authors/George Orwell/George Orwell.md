## Biography
George Orwell (1903–1950), born Eric Arthur Blair, was a British writer and journalist whose life and work were deeply shaped by class, empire, and political conflict. Raised in a lower‑middle‑class family and educated at elite schools in England, he served as a colonial police officer in Burma, an experience that left him acutely critical of imperialism and social injustice. Determined to become a writer, he lived among the poor in London and Paris, transforming these experiences into early works that examined poverty, power, and inequality.

Orwell’s political commitments intensified during the 1930s, especially after he fought in the Spanish Civil War on the Republican side and witnessed both fascist brutality and authoritarian tendencies within leftist movements. These experiences fed directly into his later critiques of totalitarianism and propaganda, most famously in the allegorical novella “Animal Farm” and the dystopian novel “Nineteen Eighty-Four,” which have become touchstones for discussions of surveillance, censorship, and political manipulation. Though often ill and financially insecure, he maintained a reputation for personal integrity and plainspoken honesty in his essays, journalism, and novels, leaving a lasting influence on political thought and modern literature.

Orwell’s personal life was marked by close friendships in literary and journalistic circles, a strong sense of moral duty, and persistent ill health, including the tuberculosis that eventually killed him. His first wife, Eileen O’Shaughnessy, shared and supported his political and intellectual commitments until her early death, and he later adopted a son, Richard, whom he was raising largely alone near the end of his life. Orwell’s blend of political engagement, stylistic clarity, and willingness to confront uncomfortable truths has ensured his continuing relevance across generations and political contexts.
