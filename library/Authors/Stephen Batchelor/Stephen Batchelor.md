## Biography
Stephen Batchelor (1953– ) is a British writer, teacher, and former Buddhist monk known for his secular and questioning approach to Buddhism. Born in Scotland, he encountered Buddhism as a young man while traveling in Asia in the early 1970s. He ordained first in the Tibetan tradition and later trained in Korean Seon (Zen), spending years in monastic communities where he immersed himself in meditation, philosophy, and classical Buddhist texts.

Over time Batchelor became increasingly committed to re-examining Buddhism without metaphysical claims, focusing instead on its ethical, psychological, and existential dimensions. Leaving monastic life, he married and settled into a vocation as an independent scholar and teacher, working primarily in Europe and the United States. His books, including the widely read "Buddhism Without Beliefs" (translated into Spanish as "Budismo sin creencias"), have helped shape contemporary discussions of secular or agnostic Buddhism, emphasizing doubt, inquiry, and personal responsibility over dogma. Batchelor’s work has made him an influential and sometimes controversial figure, respected for his deep training in traditional schools and his willingness to rethink their assumptions for a modern, pluralistic world.

In addition to writing, Batchelor has taught retreats and seminars internationally, often in collaboration with his wife, the artist Martine Batchelor, herself a former Zen nun and author. Their partnership reflects a shared commitment to integrating contemplative practice with ordinary life, art, and social engagement. Through his teaching, translations, and essays, Stephen Batchelor has played a key role in making Buddhist ideas accessible to people who may not identify as religious, while remaining attentive to the historical and cultural contexts from which those ideas emerged.
