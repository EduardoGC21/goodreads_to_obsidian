## Biography
Stephen Batchelor (1953– ) is a Scottish-born Buddhist author, translator, and teacher whose work has been central to the development of secular and agnostic approaches to Buddhism in the contemporary West. Born in Dundee, Scotland, and raised in a humanist household in Watford, England, he left school at eighteen to travel overland to India, where immersion in the Tibetan exile community at Dharamsala led to his ordination as a Gelug novice monk in the mid-1970s. Years of monastic study in India, Switzerland, and Germany gave him a deep grounding in classical Buddhist philosophy and Tibetan scholasticism, even as he quietly wrestled with doubts about doctrinal metaphysics such as karma and rebirth.

In the early 1980s Batchelor moved to Songgwangsa monastery in South Korea to train in Son (Zen) meditation under Kusan Sunim, an experience that redirected his practice toward rigorous questioning and contemplative inquiry rather than dogmatic certainty. There he met the French nun Martine Fages, with whom he later disrobed and married. Returning to England, they joined the Sharpham community in Devon, where Batchelor coordinated the Sharpham Trust, co-founded Sharpham College for Buddhist Studies and Contemporary Enquiry, and served as a guiding teacher at Gaia House while also working as a Buddhist chaplain in a local prison. During these years he began to focus increasingly on the early discourses of the Pali Canon and to articulate a vision of Buddhism as an ethical, this-worldly practice.

Batchelor’s books—most notably “Buddhism Without Beliefs,” “Confession of a Buddhist Atheist,” and later works on secular Buddhism—have had wide influence among practitioners who seek a non-theistic, naturalistic understanding of the dharma. His Spanish-language readership includes titles such as “Budismo sin creencias,” which helped introduce his secular perspective to the Spanish-speaking world. Now based in southwestern France with his wife Martine, he continues to teach, write, and lead retreats internationally, exploring the dialogue between early Buddhist thought and modern philosophical and cultural concerns.
