## Biography
Charles Dickens (1812–1870) was an English novelist, journalist, and social critic whose vivid storytelling and memorable characters made him one of the most influential writers of the Victorian era. Born in Portsmouth to a lower-middle-class family, he experienced early hardship when his father was imprisoned for debt and Charles was sent to work in a blacking factory. These formative experiences of poverty, child labor, and social injustice deeply shaped his imagination and fueled the reformist impulse that runs through much of his fiction.

Over the course of his career, Dickens produced a remarkable body of work, including "Oliver Twist," "David Copperfield," "Bleak House," and "Great Expectations," often published in serial form to a vast and devoted readership. "A Christmas Carol" in particular helped redefine modern ideas of Christmas as a season of compassion, generosity, and moral renewal, cementing his status as a moral voice of his age. Beyond his novels, he was a tireless public reader, editor, and philanthropist, maintaining complex personal relationships, including a troubled marriage to Catherine Hogarth and a long, discreet partnership with actress Ellen Ternan. By the time of his death in 1870, Dickens was widely mourned as a national figure whose life and work had brought attention to the struggles of the poor while transforming the possibilities of popular fiction.
