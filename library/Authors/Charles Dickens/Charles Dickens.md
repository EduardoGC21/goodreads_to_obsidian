## Biography
Charles Dickens (1812–1870) was an English novelist, journalist, and social critic whose works helped define the Victorian era in literature and public life. Born in Portsmouth and raised in a family that struggled financially, he experienced childhood hardship when his father was sent to debtors’ prison and Charles was forced to work in a blacking factory. These formative experiences of poverty, injustice, and class inequity deeply informed his fiction, which combined vivid storytelling with sharp critiques of social conditions.

Rising to fame first as a journalist and then through serialized novels, Dickens became one of the most widely read authors of his time, beloved for his memorable characters, humor, sentiment, and moral urgency. Works such as “Oliver Twist,” “David Copperfield,” and “Great Expectations” explore themes of social responsibility, the vulnerability of children, and the possibility of moral redemption. “A Christmas Carol,” one of his most enduring stories, crystallized his concern with charity, empathy, and the spirit of community, and it helped shape modern conceptions of Christmas as a season of generosity and reflection.

Beyond his literary achievements, Dickens was a public figure who gave popular readings, advocated for educational and social reforms, and maintained complex personal relationships, including a long, strained marriage and a later companionship with the actress Ellen Ternan. His influence on narrative form, social realism, and the English language has been profound, and his works continue to be adapted and read worldwide for their emotional power and insight into human character and society.
