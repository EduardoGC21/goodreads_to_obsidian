## Biography
Apollodorus (often referred to in Spanish as Apolodoro) was an ancient Greek scholar and mythographer traditionally associated with the authorship of the *Bibliotheca*, a comprehensive handbook of Greek myths. Although details of his life are sparse and often debated, he is generally connected with the intellectual world of Hellenistic and early Roman-era scholarship, when writers compiled and systematized earlier poetic and local traditions. His work reflects deep familiarity with epic, tragedy, and local cult lore, suggesting that he moved in learned circles and had access to extensive libraries and prior scholarly commentaries.

The *Bibliotheca* (often published in modern editions and translations such as the Spanish "Biblioteca") is one of the most important surviving sources for Greek mythology, preserving coherent narratives of the gods, heroes, and legendary genealogies. Because many earlier poetic and prose sources are lost, Apollodorus’ compilation became a crucial bridge between archaic and classical literature and later readers, shaping how subsequent generations—Byzantine scholars, Renaissance humanists, and modern classicists—understood Greek mythic tradition. His influence lies less in originality than in organization and preservation: by arranging scattered stories into a relatively continuous account, he provided a reference work that has remained indispensable for the study of classical mythology and its reception.

The historical Apollodorus is sometimes associated with Apollodorus of Athens, a scholar active in the 2nd century BCE, though the exact relationship between that figure and the author of the *Bibliotheca* is uncertain. Because the evidence for his personal life, family relationships, and exact dates is fragmentary and often indirect, modern scholars treat many biographical details with caution. Nonetheless, the work attributed to him stands as a testament to the collaborative and cumulative nature of ancient scholarship, in which one compiler could preserve centuries of earlier poetic and local traditions for future ages.
