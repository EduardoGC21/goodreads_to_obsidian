## Biography
Jack McIver Weatherford (born 1946) is an American anthropologist and author best known for his influential work on indigenous peoples and the Mongol Empire. Raised on a farm in Dovesville, South Carolina, as the eldest of seven children, he came of age in a family shaped by military service and the upheavals of mid‑twentieth‑century America. After studying political science and sociology at the University of South Carolina, he completed a Ph.D. in anthropology at the University of California, San Diego, and pursued additional graduate work at Frankfurt University and Duke University. These academic foundations led him to a lifelong engagement with how marginalized societies shape global history.

Weatherford’s early career focused on Native American and indigenous Latin American cultures, where he combined fieldwork in Bolivia and the Amazon with studies of how American Indian civilizations transformed world economies, politics, and ideas. His books on Native Americans won multiple Minnesota Book Awards and helped popularize the notion that so‑called “tribal” societies are central, not peripheral, to the modern world. Later, he turned to the history of Mongolia and the steppe, spending decades researching Mongol sources and landscapes; his bestseller "Genghis Khan and the Making of the Modern World" significantly reshaped public understanding of Genghis Khan and the Mongol Empire’s role in global development.

For nearly three decades Weatherford taught at Macalester College in Saint Paul, Minnesota, holding the DeWitt Wallace Distinguished Chair of Anthropology and mentoring generations of students in cultural anthropology and global history. His work has been recognized internationally, including Mongolia’s highest national honor for foreigners, the Order of the Polar Star, and later the Order of Chinggis Khan, reflecting the esteem in which his scholarship is held in that country. Dividing his time between the United States and Mongolia, Weatherford continues to serve as a bridge between academic research and a broad reading public, emphasizing the interconnectedness of cultures and the often‑overlooked contributions of indigenous and nomadic peoples to the modern world.
